# Meteora — Stones Suspended in the Sky

## The Place

Northwestern Greece, Thessaly region. Prefecture of Trikala. Between the towns of Kalambaka and Kastraki. Eastern foothills of the Pindus Mountains, near the Pineios River. Coordinates 39°42′51″N 21°37′52″E.

Sandstone pillars rise vertically from the plain. The tallest exceed 400 meters. Average height approximately 300 meters. The Greek Μετέωρα — meaning "suspended in the air," "raised high."

## Geology

Sixty million years ago, the Paleogene. This place was a sea. Stone, sand, and mud washed down from rivers, depositing at the delta to form layers of conglomerate and sandstone. Then the seabed uplifted to become a plateau, split along fault lines, and tens of millions of years of weathering and erosion carved the present pillar shapes.

They are not volcanic in origin. They are the product of sedimentation and uplift dating back to the age of Pangaea. In the nearby Theopetra Cave, diatom fossils have been found, contributing to paleoclimate research.

## Theopetra Cave

Four kilometers from Kalambaka. A limestone cave. From the Middle Paleolithic through the Neolithic — traces of continuous human habitation for at least 50,000 years. Excavation began in 1987. Traces of both Neanderthals and Homo sapiens were found in the same cave.

The most remarkable discovery: a stone wall erected approximately 23,000 years ago. Believed to have partially sealed the cave entrance to block the cold winds of the Ice Age. Regarded as one of the oldest man-made structures in the world.

## History of the Monasteries

From around the 9th century, hermits began living in rock crevices and caves. At first it was a solitary life. Only on Sundays and feast days did they gather for worship at the chapel below the Doupiani Rock.

In the 11th century, monks settled in the caves. But construction of monasteries began in the 14th century. As Ottoman Turkish invasions intensified, places of refuge were needed.

**Athanasius the Meteorite (1302–1380)**. Born in Nea Patra (present-day Ypati). Orphaned young and entrusted to an uncle. In 1319, when the Catalan Company invaded the city, he fled to Thessaloniki. There he studied and became a monk.

After passing through Crete and practicing at Mount Athos, in 1344, he led his followers to the top of the "Broad Rock" at Meteora and founded the Great Meteoron Monastery. Elevation 534 meters. He named it "Meteoron" (that which is suspended in the sky) because the rock's summit had the largest flat area.

His patron was Simeon Uroš, the Serbian-Greek ruler of Trikala. Simeon's son John Uroš renounced the throne in 1373 and became a monk at Meteora.

## Twenty-four Monasteries

At their peak in the 16th century, 24 monasteries stood atop the rocks. Byzantine architectural style. Six remain today.

**Active monasteries:**
- **Great Meteoron** — The largest and oldest. Founded 1356. Dedicated to the Feast of the Transfiguration. Elevation 534 meters.
- **Varlaam** — The second largest. In 1350, a monk named Varlaam laid the foundation; after 200 years of abandonment, the brothers Theophanis and Nektarios rebuilt it in 1517. Materials were hauled up over 22 years, but the building itself was completed in 20 days.
- **Rousanou** — Built in the 14th century. Currently a convent (women's monastery). Elevation 484 meters.
- **St. Nicholas Anapausas** — On a narrow rock. About 80 meters high. "Anapausas" means "rest."
- **Holy Trinity** — Built in the 14th–15th century. Filming location for the 1981 James Bond film *For Your Eyes Only*.
- **St. Stephen** — The only one situated on a flat rock. Accessible by bridge. Currently a convent.

## How They Ascended

At first they climbed on wooden pegs hammered into the rock. Or set up long ladders that were removed afterward. Access was made deliberately difficult.

Until the 1600s, the primary means of transport was net and rope. A person or cargo was placed in a large net and hauled up by pulley. The process took half an hour.

When asked when they replaced the ropes, the monks said — "When the Lord lets them break."

In the 1920s, steps were carved into the rock. Only then did visiting become easier.

## UNESCO

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988. Recognized for the combination of natural landscape and religious architecture. The second most important monastic complex of the Greek Eastern Orthodox Church, after Mount Athos.

## Other Facts

- In 1881, under the Convention of Constantinople, Thessaly was transferred from the Ottoman Empire to the Kingdom of Greece.
- In 1921, Queen Marie of Romania visited. She was the first woman permitted entry to these monasteries.
- During World War II, German and Italian forces occupied and looted the monasteries.
- Currently, fewer than 10 monks or nuns reside in each monastery.
- Four monasteries are for men, two are for women.
- The area is in a seismic risk zone. The threat of rockfall is ever-present.
