# Wieliczka — The Underground Kingdom Built of Salt

## Location

Southern Poland, the Małopolska (Lesser Poland) region. Approximately 14 kilometers southeast of central Kraków. Beneath the small city of Wieliczka lies a kingdom.

## Scale

Nine levels extend to a depth of 327 meters underground. The total length of horizontal tunnels and chambers is 287 kilometers. The number of chambers exceeds 2,000. The distance from the deepest floor to the surface is nearly twice the height of the Eiffel Tower.

## History

Since the Neolithic era, brine from this area was evaporated to produce salt. Rock salt mining began in the 13th century and continued without interruption for approximately 700 years until 1996. One of the longest-operating salt mines in the world.

In the 14th century, Kazimierz III — known as Kazimierz the Great (Kazimierz Wielki) — incorporated the mine as a royal asset. At one point, a third of the Polish crown's revenue came from this mine. An era when salt was national power.

## The Legend of Saint Kinga

The Hungarian princess Kinga was to marry Duke Bolesław V of Kraków. She asked her father, Béla IV, for salt as her dowry — for salt was scarce in Poland.

When her father took her to the Máramaros salt mine in Hungary, Kinga threw her engagement ring into the mine shaft. Then she departed for Poland.

After arriving in Kraków, Kinga ordered miners to dig deep into the earth. The miners struck rock and split it open; inside they found a lump of salt, and when they broke the salt in two, Kinga's ring appeared.

This is the origin legend of the Wieliczka salt deposit. Kinga became the patron saint of Polish salt miners, was beatified in the late 17th century, and was canonized by Pope John Paul II in June 1999.

## The Chapel of Saint Kinga (Kaplica św. Kingi)

Located 101 meters underground. 54 meters long, 18 meters wide, 12 meters high. The floor, the walls, the ceiling, the chandeliers, the altar, the bas-reliefs — all salt.

Three miners created this. The brothers Józef Markowski and Tomasz Markowski, and Antoni Wyrodek. Self-taught miner-artists. From the late 19th century to 1963, they spent approximately 68 years completing it.

Bas-reliefs of biblical scenes are carved into the walls. Chandeliers made of salt crystals hang from the ceiling. On the altar stand sculptures of Saint Kinga, Saint Joseph, and Saint Clement. All of it is salt.

## Underground Lakes

Several brine lakes exist inside the mine. Water pooled between tunnels, saturated with salt, takes on a greenish hue. When lights reflect off the surface of the lakes along the tourist route, the water looks not like water but like an entrance to another world.

## The Healing Properties of Salt

At a depth of 135 meters, there is a sanatorium. The microclimate unique to salt mines — constant temperature, high humidity, air filled with suspended salt particles — is known to be effective for chronic allergies and respiratory conditions. It is the only facility in Poland to have been granted underground health resort status.

## UNESCO

In 1978, it was selected as one of the first 12 sites inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List. This means it was among the first places humanity collectively agreed: "This must be protected." It is also an official Polish Historical Monument (Pomnik Historii).
