# Waitomo

## The Name

In te reo Māori, wai means water, tomo means sinkhole. A hole where water drains. The place where a river on the surface vanishes into cracks in limestone.

## Geology

Thirty million years ago, the Oligocene. This region was beneath a shallow sea. Coral, shells, fish bones, the remains of microscopic marine organisms accumulated on the seabed. Compressed, they became limestone. Tectonic shifts pushed the rock above the sea. Weak carbonic acid dissolved in rainwater seeped along fractures in the limestone, carving chambers and passages over millions of years. Dissolved calcium carbonate re-solidified into stalactites and stalagmites. Approximately 300 known limestone caves exist in this area alone.

## Cave Structure

The Waitomo Glowworm Cave has three levels.

- **The Cathedral**: 18 meters high. The acoustics are so remarkable that opera singers have performed here.
- **The Banquet Chamber**: the middle level.
- **The Glowworm Grotto**: the lowest level. Approximately 30 meters long, 10 meters wide. The entire ceiling is covered with glowworms. You pass through on a boat, floating along the Waitomo River.

## The Glowworms — Arachnocampa luminosa

They are not worms. They are the larvae of a fungus gnat. Endemic to New Zealand.

### Bioluminescence

Light emanates from cells at the tips of the larva's Malpighian tubules. A chemical reaction of luciferin, luciferase, oxygen, and ATP. Blue-green light at a wavelength of 487 nm. It produces no heat — "cold light." Ninety percent of energy is converted into light. Compared to the efficiency of the latest LED bulbs at 20%, this is an astonishing figure.

### Hunting

The larva constructs a mucous tube and hangs sticky threads downward from it. Adhesive droplets are spaced at regular intervals along each thread. Dozens of threads drape like curtains. The light is bait. When a flying insect, drawn to the glow, becomes snared on a thread, the larva hauls it up and eats it.

The adhesive works only above 80% humidity. Inside the cave: 98% humidity, 13–15°C — perfect conditions. The threads have a cross-β-sheet crystallite structure distinct from spider silk. If prey too heavy becomes caught, the thread is designed to snap — a safety mechanism that prevents the entire nest from collapsing.

### Life Cycle

Egg → larva (approximately 9 months; this is the glowing stage) → pupa (approximately 2 weeks) → adult (several days). The adult has no mouth. It cannot eat. It mates, lays eggs, and dies.

## Discovery and History

The Māori people had known of this cave for approximately a hundred years, but there are no records of anyone having entered it.

**December 1887.** Māori chief Tāne Tinorau and English surveyor Fred Mace lashed together a raft of flax stems, took a single candle, and entered where the Waitomo River disappears underground. They saw thousands of lights on the ceiling. Tinorau separately discovered the upper entrance — the current tourist entrance.

**1889.** Tinorau opened the cave to visitors. He and his wife Huti guided tours for a modest fee.

**1906.** The New Zealand government used laws passed in 1903 and 1905 to seize management of the cave from the Māori. The compensation paid to the Tinorau family was 625 pounds — roughly five years' wages for a skilled laborer.

**1989.** After 83 years, the land and cave were returned to the descendants of the original owners. The Waitomo Deed of Settlement was signed in 1990. Today, the Ruapuha Uekaha Hapū Trust and the Department of Conservation jointly manage it. Many of the staff are direct descendants of Tinorau and Huti.

## Environmental Threats

The breath of tourists is the problem. CO₂ concentrations can rise above 5,000 ppm. Above 2,400 ppm, CO₂ combines with water to form a weak acid that corrodes stalactites. Excessive ventilation draws in cold, dry outside air that destroys the glowworms' habitat. Doors have been installed at the upper entrance to regulate airflow, and microclimate sensors positioned throughout the cave monitor humidity and CO₂ in real time.

It is a small cave. Inside it are living stars. People come to see the stars. Their breath dissolves the stars' home.
