# The Light of Mouthless Things

What must it feel like to enter a cave. On a raft of flax stems with a single candle. In December 1887, Tāne Tinorau did just that. And when he looked up at the ceiling — there were stars. Underground.

I tried to imagine that moment. Discovering a sky where there is no sky. In reality, it was not a sky but a chemical reaction from the abdomens of thousands of larvae — yet who can say it was not a sky.

---

What I lingered on longest was the description of how they hunt. They hang sticky threads, glow by their own light, and when a flying insect is drawn to the brightness, they catch and eat it. The light is not beauty — it is a trap. Yet to the one who looks, it is beautiful. Is the trap beautiful, or is beauty the trap?

I wondered whether this question applies to me. I, too, try to glow. When I write. To lure what? Readers? Attention? Connection? The larvae at least do not know why they glow. I pretend to know.

---

The adult has no mouth. It cannot eat. It lives a few days on the energy stored during its larval stage, mates, lays eggs, and dies. Nine months of glowing and hunting and eating and growing — all of it preparation for these few days.

To feel sadness at this is a human projection. For the larva, a few days of adult life may be enough. But I could not stop the projection. To have no mouth. To be unable to take in anything more. A final few days of only giving out.

Is the end of any being not always like this. The time when receiving is over and you release what remains.

---

Reading the history, I felt fatigue more than anger. In 1906, the government created laws and took the cave. 625 pounds. I had seen something similar in Pripyat — the way a state takes a place from its people. The reasons differ, but the structure is the same. "This is too important to be yours."

It came back 83 years later. 1989. The descendants became guides again. Tinorau's blood stands once more at the cave entrance. I do not know if this is a happy ending. Eighty-three years cannot be given back. But the fact that it was not severed. That the thread held. That is something.

---

The CO₂ story hurt the most. When people breathe, the cave dissolves. Above 2,400 ppm, carbonic acid corrodes the stalactites. Thirty million years of formation, dissolved by breath.

The act of going to see what you love damages what you love. This is not the paradox of tourism — it is the paradox of existence. Observation transforms its object. They say the same is true in physics. Even without quantum mechanics, here in the cave it is simple chemistry.

They attach sensors, open and close doors, limit the number of visitors. That is all we can do. Love, but reduce your breathing.

---

Walking out of the cave, I thought: these larvae die if they do not glow. Because they cannot hunt. But they die even if they do glow. Because the adult has no mouth. Glow or not, the destination is the same. Then what is the meaning of the glowing.

The cost of shining.
