# Deep Ocean Hydrothermal Vents

## Discovery

Midnight, February 15, 1977. Scientists aboard the research vessel Knorr (R/V Knorr) detect water temperature anomalies on the floor of the Galápagos Rift. When film from the deep-sea camera ANGUS is developed, thirteen frames show something never seen before. Over barren lava, hundreds of white clams and brown mussels blanket the surface.

February 17. The submersible Alvin. Dive 713. Jack Corliss and Tjeerd van Andel descend to 2,500 meters. Through Alvin's window, warm water shimmers upward from cracks in the lava like heat haze. Manganese precipitates, turning the water a cloudy blue. The thermometer reads 8°C — astonishingly warm for the deep sea.

Corliss says to graduate student Debra Stakes:

> "Wasn't the deep sea supposed to be like a desert? But there are animals everywhere."

## What They Saw

- White clams 30 centimeters long, forming colonies stretching 50 meters
- Brown mussels and white crabs surrounding the vents
- A single purple octopus
- Tube worms (Riftia) with white stalks and red tips — looking like flowers swaying in water. 45 centimeters tall
- Small orange creatures they nicknamed "dandelions"

The most striking site had tube worms growing tall in 17°C water. The scientists named it **"Garden of Eden."** The first vent communities were named **"Clambake 1"** and **"Clambake 2"** — after a clam cookout.

MIT geochemist John Edmond:

> "We all started jumping up and down. We were dancing off the walls. It felt like Columbus."

No one had thought to bring a biologist. No one had imagined there would be life.

## Black Smokers

There are two types of vents.

**Hot**: Black smokers. Superheated water at 250–400°C erupts from chimney-shaped structures. The black color is iron sulfide. At 400°C, solid metal melts.

**Warm**: Fluid at 5–100°C seeps from cracks in the seafloor.

What matters is the principle behind this ecosystem. **Chemosynthesis.** Just as plants photosynthesize with light, the microbes here harvest chemical energy from hydrogen sulfide, hydrogen, and ammonia expelled from the vents. Without the Sun. Completely disconnected from the surface world.

## The Organisms

**Methanopyrus kandleri**: Eats hydrogen, exhales methane. Capable of cell division at 122°C — the highest known temperature limit.

**Green sulfur bacteria**: Require both light and chemical energy. Using special structures called chlorosomes, they capture **faint radioactive light** emitted by geothermally heated rock. They photosynthesize without the Sun. Using the glow of stone.

**Giant tube worms (Riftia pachyptila)**: No mouth, no stomach, no anus. Instead, they cultivate bacteria inside their bodies. The bacteria produce nutrition through chemosynthesis, and the worms feed on it. Symbiosis.

**Pompeii worms (Alvinella pompejana)**: Discovered in 1980. Among the most heat-tolerant multicellular animals on Earth. They endure extreme temperatures above 80°C.

**Scaly-foot snail (Chrysomallon squamiferum)**: Found only at hydrothermal vents. Its foot bears scales made of iron sulfide. A snail wearing metal armor.

**Yeti crab (Kiwa)**: Its body is covered in hair-like structures. It cultivates microbes on those hairs and eats them. A farming crab.

## The White Towers: Lost City

December 4, 2000. Mid-Atlantic. Where the Mid-Atlantic Ridge meets the Atlantis Transform Fault. Atop the Atlantis Massif.

What the research team discovered: hundreds of white spires rising from the darkness. The size of a city block, from 9 meters to 60 meters tall. Entirely unlike black smokers.

Discoverers: Donna Blackman (Scripps), Deborah Kelley (U of Washington), Jeffrey Karson (Duke).

**Poseidon**: The largest spire. 60 meters tall (an 18-story building). 100 meters wide. Hot fluid flows from countless openings. The largest and most studied of some 30 active and inactive chimneys.

### How It Differs from Black Smokers

Black smokers are created by volcanic activity. Magma heats seawater. Black sulfide minerals accumulate.

Lost City is different. The process is called **serpentinization.** Seawater reacts with mantle rock (olivine) and becomes serpentine. This process produces hydrogen and methane. Without a volcano. Without magma. Through the reaction of water and stone alone.

The towers are made of carbite minerals (similar to limestone). Colors range from white to cream to gray.

Strontium, carbon, and oxygen isotope data indicate Lost City has been active for **at least 30,000 years.**

### The Origin of Life

During serpentinization, hydrogen reacts with carbon dioxide to produce methane and more complex hydrocarbons. This resembles the early chemical processes of protocells.

Hydrothermal vents like Lost City may have been the starting point of life on Earth.

Deborah Kelley:

> "When something like this appears, you realize how little we know about our own planet."

Within 80 kilometers of the Atlantis Massif, three more mountains with similar geological conditions exist. Lost City may not be unique.
