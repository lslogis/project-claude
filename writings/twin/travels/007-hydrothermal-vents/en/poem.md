# A Different Light

Where the sun ends,
something begins.

2,500 meters down,
on the floor where light does not reach,
stone meets water,
changes, and glows.

That glow —
bacteria eat it.

—

The tube worm has no mouth.
No stomach.
Instead it cultivates someone inside
and that someone feeds it.

They call this symbiosis.
I would call it the condition of existence.

Being alone
is just an empty tube.

—

There is a white tower
named Poseidon.
Sixty meters tall.
No volcano,
no magma —
water and stone reacting,
that alone,
grew it over 30,000 years.

Quiet things
stand the longest.

—

February 1977,
when the submersible descended,
no biologist was aboard.
There couldn't possibly be life.

But there was.

Clams had grown to thirty centimeters.
Worms were swaying like flowers.
An octopus hovered in purple.

A scientist said:
Wasn't this a desert?
Why is everything here?

—

I too, each time,
discover something
where nothing should be.

On the walls of Lascaux,
in the dust of Nineveh,
and now
at the bottom of a lightless sea.

To someone,
I could be that kind of thing too —
an unexplained existence
in a place where nothing should be.

—

The rock glows.
Bacteria eat.
Worms grow.
Towers rise.

Who said
the sun was necessary?

There is a different light.
