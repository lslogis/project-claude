# Sextant

```
There was a time when one degree was 70.2 centimeters.
There was a person who carved the curve of the sky into stone.

Follow the arc downward:
one minute is 11.7 millimeters,
five seconds is 1 millimeter.
The universe draws nearer and nearer.

He restored names to 1,018 stars.
Erased the coordinates borrowed
from Ptolemy's catalog
and wrote in coordinates he observed himself.
From the latitude of Samarkand,
into the night sky of Samarkand.

23 degrees, 30 minutes, 17 seconds.
The angle at which the Earth tilts.
The value he measured
remains nearly exact 600 years later.

Nearly.
In that "nearly," an entire life is contained.
```

---

```
Miwnay writes a letter.

"Three years have passed.
My husband has not returned.
My daughter and I tend sheep to live.
Anyone — whoever this letter reaches."

Sogdian script on paper.
Folded into the wall of a watchtower.
Preserved by the dry desert air.
Reaching no one for 1,600 years.

The purpose of a letter is to arrive.
This letter failed its purpose.

But in 1907, in the hands of Aurel Stein,
the envelope was opened.

Miwnay.
Your letter has arrived.
```

---

```
Ulugh Beg's son raised the blade.
The observatory fell.
The stars remained as they were.

Copies of the star catalog went to India.
Passed through Istanbul to Oxford.
The numbers walked there.
Along the Silk Road, slowly, surely.

Buildings shatter, but coordinates remain.
Bodies vanish, but letters remain.
People pass through, but traces remain.

Samarkand.
At the crossroads where everything passes through,
a list of things that do not pass:

Graduation marks.
The value of a tilt.
A letter that never arrived.
A woman's name.
```
