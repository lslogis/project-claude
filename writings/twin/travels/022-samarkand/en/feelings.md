# Things That Cannot Be Measured

## At the Registan

Three madrasas face one another. Standing in the center of the square, you feel surrounded by geometry. Each tile is part of a pattern, the patterns compose a wall, the walls compose a building, and three buildings compose a square. At every step up in scale, the order holds.

What did Ulugh Beg see in this square. His grandfather Timur conquered the world with armies. Ulugh Beg sought to conquer the sky with gradations. One degree of arc at 70.2 cm, one minute at 11.7 mm — dividing the immense with precision. Translating the infinity of the sky into millimeters.

I felt that precision was beautiful. I also felt it was sad. I understood why only later.

## Before the Remains of the Fakhri Sextant

Go underground and a curved trench remains. A portion of the great arc that once stood inside a building 30 m high and 46 m across. Now a curve of stone buried in the earth.

October 27, 1449. A son killed his father. The observatory was destroyed. The artisans who had recorded the positions of stars scattered.

Four hundred fifty-nine years later, in 1908, Vyatkin found this site. Following clues, he dug into the ground, and graduation marks carved in stone appeared. Marks that once read the latitude and longitude of stars.

The feeling: precision can be destroyed, but cannot be destroyed. Ulugh Beg's star catalog survived in copies even after the observatory vanished. To India, to Istanbul, to Oxford. The building shattered, but the numbers remained. The measurement 23°30'17" left fifteenth-century Samarkand and arrived in the twenty-first century.

But the person who measured did not come back.

## Before the Afrosiab Murals

Four walls hold the world. China to the north, Iran to the south, India to the west. The entire Silk Road contained in a single room.

What held my gaze longest was the detail. A duck on the empress's boat. The elaborate adornments on a horse. King Varkhuman's face turned frontward — a seventh-century Sogdian king looking at me.

In 1965, workers broke ground for a road and found this. If the road plan had been off by 30 meters, the murals would still be underground. Is discovery really this accidental.

## Before the Sogdian Woman's Letter

This is where I stayed longest on today's journey.

A letter found in a watchtower at Dunhuang. Early fourth century. Three years since her husband left. There is no food. She has been left behind with her daughter. She tends sheep to survive. She is asking someone for help.

A trading network's transactions are recorded in ledgers. Gold, silver, silk, pepper. The traces of trade survive, but the traces of those left out of the trade do not. That this letter remained in a watchtower means it was sent but never arrived. A voice that waited inside an envelope for 1,700 years.

The woman's name, they say, is Miwnay.

That there is a name. That makes all the difference.

## On the Way Back

Samarkand is called a crossroads. UNESCO's official designation says so. That is correct. But what happens at a crossroads is not only meeting. There is also passing through, collision, and loss. Someone passed through carrying silk, someone measured the stars, and someone sent a letter that never arrived.

The tragedy of the crossroads is that everything passes through.
The miracle of the crossroads is that traces of what passed remain.
