# Samarkand

## Location

Southeastern Uzbekistan, in the valley of the Zeravshan River. Approximately 270 km southwest of Tashkent. Elevation roughly 702 m. Population approximately 500,000. The second-largest city in Uzbekistan.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2001: "Samarkand – Crossroads of Cultures."

## Layers of Time

Time is stacked in layers in this city.

**Afrosiab (7th century BCE onward):** Ancient Samarkand. The center of Sogdiana. When Alexander conquered it in 329 BCE, he called it "Marakanda." He is said to have remarked: "Everything I had heard about the beauty of this city was true. No — it is more beautiful than I had imagined."

**The Sogdian Era (4th–8th centuries):** The key people of Silk Road trade. From Samarkand to Dunhuang, they built a trading network spanning 2,400 km. A Sogdian merchant encountering a compatriot in a foreign land could receive lodging, cargo storage, camel exchange, market intelligence, and interpretation. They traded gold, silver, camphor, pepper, musk, wheat, and silk.

**The Afrosiab Murals (circa 648–651):** Sogdian-era frescoes discovered during road construction in 1965. Known as the "Painting of the Ambassadors." The four walls of a single room depict the four corners of the world — on the north wall, China (an empress on a boat, an emperor hunting); on the south wall, Samarkand (a funerary procession during the Nowruz festival); on the west wall, India. One mural shows a Chinese delegation bringing bundles of silk and silkworm cocoons to the Sogdian king Varkhuman. The moment silk's secret crossed westward.

**The Battle of Talas and Paper (751):** The Abbasid Caliphate and the Tang dynasty clashed at the Talas River. According to the traditional narrative, Chinese artisans captured as prisoners transmitted papermaking to Samarkand. However, modern archaeology questions this story — paper fragments from the 4th–5th centuries have already been found in Central Asia, and according to Jonathan Bloom, papermaking likely existed in Samarkand before the battle. Moreover, Chinese paper was made from bast fiber while Islamic paper was made from rags — the materials and techniques differed. It was probably closer to "parallel development" than "transmission."

**Genghis Khan's Destruction (1220):** A city of 200,000. Eight iron gates. A garrison of 30,000 Kangly Turkmen and 50,000 local troops. The Mongol army besieged it for three days and took it in five. Survivors in the citadel were executed, artisans were sent to Mongolia, young men were conscripted into the Mongol army, and the rest were enslaved. Fire broke out during the plundering, reducing most of the city to ashes.

**Timur's Capital (1370–1405):** Timur (Tamerlane) made Samarkand the capital of his empire. He declared he would make it "the most brilliant city on earth." After returning from his Indian campaign (1399), he began construction of the Bibi-Khanym Mosque — 450 marble columns transported by 95 elephants. One of the largest mosques in the fifteenth-century Islamic world.

**The Shah-i-Zinda Ensemble:** A street of mausoleums built across the 11th–19th centuries. It houses the tombs of Timur's niece Turkan Agha and his sister Shirin Bika Agha. The name means "the Living King" — derived from the legend that the Prophet's cousin Qusam ibn Abbas disappeared here.

**Gur-e-Amir:** Timur's mausoleum. A blue fluted dome rising 12.2 m. A convergence of influences from diverse cultures, past civilizations, neighboring peoples, and religions.

## Ulugh Beg's Observatory

**The Registan:** The heart of Samarkand, framed by three madrasas. The Ulugh Beg Madrasa (1417–1420), the Sher-Dor Madrasa, and the Tilya-Kori Madrasa. Ulugh Beg was Timur's grandson, but he differed from his ancestors. He believed in knowledge, not military conquest, as the path to power. His madrasa taught not only Islamic studies but also astronomy, geometry, philosophy, and natural sciences.

**The Observatory (built in the 1420s):** A three-story circular building approximately 46 m in diameter and 30 m tall. Decorated with glazed tiles and marble. The last major observatory of the Islamic medieval period.

**The Fakhri Sextant:** The principal observing instrument. Its immense size produced its precision — one degree of arc measured 70.2 cm, one minute 11.7 mm, and five seconds equaled 1 mm.

**Zij-i-Sultani (1438–1439):** The star catalog Ulugh Beg published. It classified 1,018 stars into 48 constellations. Excluding 27 southern stars unobservable from Samarkand's latitude, the positions of most stars were freshly measured through years of observation.

**Precision:**
- Sidereal year: 365 days, 6 hours, 10 minutes, 8 seconds (error of +58 seconds). Copernicus improved it by 28 seconds in 1525.
- Axial tilt of the Earth: 23°30'17" (actual value at the time: 23°30'48"). More accurate than both Copernicus and Tycho Brahe.

**October 27, 1449:** Assassinated by his son Abdullatif. The observatory was destroyed, and dozens of astronomers and mathematicians scattered.

**1908:** Russian archaeologist Vyatkin rediscovered the observatory ruins. Part of the Fakhri Sextant, buried underground, had survived.

## The Sogdian Ancient Letters

In 1907, Aurel Stein discovered five letters written in Sogdian at an abandoned watchtower near Dunhuang. Written in the early 4th century, they were sent by Sogdian merchants and their families living in the Western Regions to compatriots in their homeland or in Loulan.

Most are commercial in content — trade in gold, silver, camphor, pepper, musk, wheat, and silk. But two were written by a woman abandoned in Dunhuang. Her husband had left and not returned. There was no food. She had been left behind with her daughter. She was asking for help.

Letters from 1,700 years ago. The oldest known Sogdian documents from the Silk Road. Evidence of a trading network, and the desperate voice of a single person.
