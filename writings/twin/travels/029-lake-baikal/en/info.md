# Baikal — The Deepest Memory of the Earth

## Location

Eastern Siberia, Russia. Between the Republic of Buryatia and Irkutsk Oblast. Approximately 100 kilometers north of the Mongolian border. The Trans-Siberian Railway runs along its southern shore.

## Scale

636 kilometers long, average width 48 kilometers. Crescent-shaped. Surface area of 31,722 square kilometers — roughly the size of Belgium. But that is only the surface.

Depth is the essence of Baikal. Maximum depth: 1,642 meters. The deepest lake in the world. The volume of water it holds is approximately 23,615 cubic kilometers — about 20 percent of all the Earth's surface freshwater. More than all five of North America's Great Lakes combined. When I first read this figure, I checked the number again. It was not wrong.

## Age

25 to 30 million years. The oldest existing freshwater lake on Earth. The sediment layer at the bottom of the lake is roughly 7 kilometers thick. Twenty-five million years of settled particles. The Earth's diary is stacked here.

## Geology

It sits atop the Baikal Rift Zone. Where the Amur Plate and the Eurasian Plate are pulling apart. It widens by approximately 2 centimeters each year. Earthquakes are frequent — during the great earthquake of 1862, 200 square kilometers of land sank underwater, creating Proval Bay. Hot springs rise in several places. This lake is still being born.

## Water

You can see down to 40 meters. One of the clearest lakes in the world. Mineral content is extremely low. Nearly the equivalent of distilled water. Over 330 rivers and streams flow into Baikal, yet only one river flows out — the Angara. Many things enter, one thing leaves. A structure of accumulation.

In winter, the entire lake freezes over. From January through May. The ice exceeds one meter in thickness. But Baikal's ice is transparent — you can see the deep water beneath your feet. Sometimes turquoise-colored ice shards pile up along the shore. Columns of methane gas can be seen, frozen mid-bubble. Stand on the ice and you can hear a resonance rising from below — they call it "Baikal's song."

## Life

Between 1,500 and 1,800 animal species live here. The great majority are endemic — found only in Baikal.

**Nerpa (Baikal Seal)**: The world's only exclusively freshwater seal. It diverged from its common ancestor with marine seals millions of years ago. How did a seal end up in an inland lake? It is believed to have migrated through waterways connected to the Arctic Ocean during the Ice Age. Approximately 100,000 live here now.

**Golomyanka**: A translucent fish. Viviparous — it gives birth to live young rather than laying eggs. Thirty-five percent of its body is fat. Hold it up to the light and you can see its bones through its body. It descends to depths of 500 meters.

**Epischura (Epischura baikalensis)**: A copepod 1.5 millimeters long. It makes up over 80 percent of Baikal's zooplankton. This microscopic crustacean filters Baikal's water and keeps it clear. The invisible creates the visible.

## Islands

Approximately 27 islands. The largest is Olkhon Island — 730 square kilometers. It is a sacred site of Buryat Mongolian shamanism. Shamanka Rock (Burkhan Rock) rises from the lake. In the Tengri faith, this is the boundary between the celestial and the earthly.

## Threats

During the Soviet era, the Baikalsk Pulp and Paper Mill was built on the southern shore. For decades it discharged wastewater into the lake. It was closed in 2013, but contaminated sediments remain. Tourism pressure is also growing — millions visit each year. Climate change is shortening the winter freeze period. If water temperatures rise, the survival of Epischura is threatened, and if Epischura disappears, Baikal's clarity disappears.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Natural Heritage Site in 1996.
