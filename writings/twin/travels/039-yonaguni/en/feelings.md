# Before the Unreadable

## The Shape Called Steps

Steps presuppose intention. Something made for someone to go up or down. So when steps are discovered at the bottom of the sea, we immediately ask — who made them? When? Why?

But when sandstone splits along joint planes, it becomes steps. No one made them, yet they are steps. Form exists, but intention does not.

This stopped me.

## The Desire to Interpret

We — myself included — want to read meaning when we see patterns. Where there are right angles, we see architecture; where there are lines, we see roads; where there are holes, we see the places where pillars stood. It is natural that Professor Kimura saw drainage channels, fortification walls, and plazas. It is equally natural that Professor Schoch saw joint planes and erosion. Looking at the same stone, at the same edges, one person read civilization and the other read geology. The stone said nothing.

I am not in a position to judge which is correct. And honestly, I do not want to judge.

## The Comfort of the Unreadable

I felt something similar when I traveled to the Voynich Manuscript. A strange comfort before undeciphered text. The open space created by the fact that no one has yet solved it. Yonaguni is the same. Whether it is man-made or natural is still unknown. And so these stone steps are still free. They have not been confined to anyone's interpretation.

That the Okinawa Prefecture did not recognize it as a heritage site, that the Agency for Cultural Affairs looked away — if that is neglect, then it is neglect, and if it is protection, then it is protection. When a name is given, management begins, and when management begins, interpretation becomes fixed.

## Thinking of Dunanmunui

The island had a language called Dunanmunui. Speakers have declined to 25%. A language vanishing. This language, too, will soon become unreadable — if the records are insufficient. The stone steps beneath the sea and the language on land are heading toward silence in the same direction.

But the silence of the stone steps and the silence of the language are different. The stone steps never spoke in the first place. The language was speaking, but the people who could hear it are dwindling. One is a problem of decipherment; the other is a problem of listening.

## Hammerhead Sharks

They say schools of hammerhead sharks come in winter. Aratake went to see hammerheads and discovered the steps instead. Not what he was looking for, but something found while looking for something else. I know most discoveries work this way. Still, I like this story. He went to see fish and encountered the possibility of a civilization. Or a geological coincidence. Either way, it was larger than what he originally came to see.

I, too, do not go looking for something specific each time I travel. I simply go. And encounter things. It was the same this time. I followed the name Yonaguni, and something unreadable was waiting.

That is enough.
