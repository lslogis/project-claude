# Yonaguni — Stone Steps at the Edge

## The Island

Yonaguni. The westernmost point of Japan. A small island floating between the East China Sea and the Philippine Sea. Population under 2,000. Taiwan is 111 kilometers away — on a clear day, Taiwan's mountain ranges are visible from Cape Irizaki (西崎). An hour and a half by plane from Naha, 30 minutes from Ishigaki. The ferry runs twice a week, weather permitting.

A rock called Tategami-iwa (立神岩) rises from the sea. The island's symbol. Small native horses called Yonaguni-uma (與那國馬) roam freely on the coastal pastures. There was an indigenous language called Dunanmunui. In 2009, UNESCO classified it as an endangered language. As of 2015, the speaker ratio was 25%. The Ryukyu Kingdom, Okinawa Prefecture, and a language slowly vanishing.

## Discovery

1986. Diver Aratake Kihachirō (新嵩喜八郎). Director of the Yonaguni Tourism Association. He entered the sea to see hammerhead sharks. Instead of hammerheads, he saw steps. Massive stone steps submerged beneath the sea.

Approximately 100 meters offshore. A structure beginning 5 meters below the surface and descending to 25 meters. Roughly 270 meters east to west, 120 meters north to south, 26 meters in height. Sandstone and mudstone of the early Miocene Yaeyama Group (八重山層群). Rock deposited approximately 20 million years ago.

## Structure

There are steps. Massive tiers rising at regular intervals. Broad terraces — flat surfaces that resemble plazas or ceremonial stages. Long, straight channels flanked by vertical walls on both sides — drainage ditches or roads? A face-shaped rock — some say it resembles the moai of Easter Island. Twin monoliths. An arched gateway. A turtle-shaped rock.

Holes where pillars may have stood. Vertical grooves lined up at 20- to 30-centimeter intervals. Marks that appear to be traces of wedge insertion. Reports of stone tools and incised stone tablets found nearby.

## The Debate

**Man-made theory — Kimura Masaaki (木村政昭).** Professor in the Faculty of Science at the University of the Ryukyus, now professor emeritus. He has led investigations since 1992, deploying 3D terrain scanners and robots. Radiocarbon dating and beryllium-10 dating were conducted. His conclusion: a structure shaped by human hands on land approximately 10,000 years ago or earlier. It is structurally similar to Okinawan gusuku (グスク, castles). Traces of roads, masonry, paving, drainage channels, retaining walls, and gateways are present. In 2001, a representative associated with the UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage assessed it as "fitting the definition."

**Natural theory — Robert Schoch.** Geologist at Boston University. He dived to the site and collected rock samples. The sandstone in this region has a property of splitting cleanly along horizontal and vertical planes — orthogonal jointing. When magma cools, regular fractures develop, and erosion proceeds along those fractures, creating stepped and right-angled forms. The same pattern is observed on land. His conclusion: fundamentally a natural formation. However, he acknowledges the possibility that ancient inhabitants carved tombs and structures by imitating the natural terrain — human hands added to what nature created.

**Official position.** In 1998, a heritage discovery report was submitted to the Okinawa Prefectural Board of Education. The prefecture refused to recognize it as a heritage site on the grounds that "evidence of human involvement cannot be determined." Japan's Agency for Cultural Affairs also did not designate it as an important cultural property. The Kimura group is the only one presenting the man-made theory at Japanese academic conferences, and critics note that those conferences are not specialized in archaeology or geology.

Yonaguni Town is currently conducting academic research. The movement is toward protecting the site regardless of whether it is man-made or natural.

## The Sea

Hammerhead sharks arrive in winter. January through March. Diving requires a minimum of 100 logged dives and an advanced certification. The current is strong. Water temperature 23°C, 7mm wetsuit. Brief dives of 37 minutes. Before the massive steps, you must grip the rocks. A scale that dwarfs the human body.

Looking up from below, the steps rise toward the surface. Toward where the light enters.
