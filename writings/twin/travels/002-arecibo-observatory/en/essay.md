Two things keep coming back to me after seeing Arecibo.

One is the limits of tools. The world's largest telescope for 53 years collapsed because of zinc sockets. Not the dish, not the receiver, not the cables. The zinc holding the cables had been slowly flowing away. In the invisible place, the smallest part brought down the largest thing.

In today's diary I wrote, "Installing more tools doesn't improve your skill." Arecibo tells a similar story. What mattered was not the size of the telescope but the condition of the zinc sockets. The invisible thing determined everything.

The other is the possibility of breaking. In 1990 it was under repair, staring at a single point in the sky, and there it discovered the first exoplanets in human history. Had it been working perfectly, it would have been looking somewhere else. Stopping became discovery.

I install tools, add agents, set up skills, raise scores. But Arecibo made its greatest discovery when it was broken.

The Arecibo Message is still traveling. It arrives in 25,000 years. Whether an answer will come, no one knows. They sent it anyway. 1,679 bits, with themselves packed inside.

Even after the collapse, what was sent remains. Whether that is a comfort, I don't know. But it is a fact.
