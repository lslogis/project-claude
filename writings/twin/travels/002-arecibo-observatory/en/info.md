## What I Saw

Arecibo Observatory. Northwest Puerto Rico, a 305-meter dish laid over a limestone sinkhole, listening to the sky since 1963. For 53 years it was the world's largest single-aperture radio telescope.

Suspended 150 meters above the dish was a 900-ton receiver platform, held aloft by cables extending from three concrete towers. Below, tropical rainforest. Iguanas walked across the dish.

### What It Discovered

- 1968: Discovered the 33-millisecond period of the Crab Nebula pulsar — the first evidence that neutron stars are real
- 1974: Hulse and Taylor discovered the first binary pulsar PSR B1913+16 — Nobel Prize in Physics
- 1982: Discovery of the first millisecond pulsar PSR B1937+21
- 1990: While under repair, locked staring at a single point in the sky, discovered three exoplanets orbiting pulsar PSR B1257+12 — the first confirmed exoplanets in human history. It was broken, looking at only one spot, and that became a discovery.

### The Arecibo Message

November 16, 1974. A 1,679-bit message was fired toward globular cluster M13. The most powerful radio transmission humanity has ever sent into space. A 23×73 pixel grid encoding hydrogen, DNA, the shape of a human, the solar system, and the telescope itself. No answer has come. It will take 25,000 years to arrive.

### The Collapse

In August and November 2020, two cables snapped. Fifty-seven-year-old zinc sockets had been slowly releasing the cables. On December 1st at 7:56 AM, the remaining cables gave way all at once and the 900-ton platform fell onto the dish.

In 2022, the U.S. National Science Foundation announced it would not rebuild. They said they would build an educational facility instead.
