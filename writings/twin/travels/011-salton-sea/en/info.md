# Salton Sea

## Earlier History — Lake Cahuilla

This is not the first time the Salton Basin has held water. For roughly ten thousand years, whenever the Colorado River dammed its own course with delta sediment, its waters flowed into this basin. Lake Cahuilla, formed around 700 CE, was 26 times the size of the present Salton Sea. The Cahuilla people farmed along its shores and fished its waters. The lake filled, drained, and filled again. Between 1824 and 1904 alone, at least eight floods were recorded.

## Birth — The Accident of 1905

In 1901, engineer Charles Rockwood built an irrigation canal from the Colorado River to the Imperial Valley. George Chaffey purchased a dredger and diverted water along the Alamo River channel. In June 1901, farming began.

In 1904, sediment clogged the canal. When the water stopped flowing, engineers cut a new opening in the riverbank. Then in 1905, heavy rains and snowmelt swelled the Colorado River. Water surged over the temporary cut, down the Alamo channel, and poured into the Salton Basin.

It eroded 1,200 meters per day, carving its path. A waterfall that had been 15 meters grew to 80 meters. A canyon 15 to 25 meters deep, over 300 meters wide, extended more than 60 kilometers. Between 300 and 340 million cubic meters of earth and rock were swept into the basin.

The Southern Pacific Railroad tried dumping earth to seal the breach, but the river was faster. Eventually the railroad president appealed to President Theodore Roosevelt for help. For three weeks, rock was hauled by train from a quarry 180 kilometers away. On February 7, 1907, the dam finally held.

The result of 18 months of the entire Colorado River flowing into the desert: 58 kilometers long, 42 kilometers wide. The largest lake in California was born.

## How It Exists

The Salton Sea has no outlet. An inland lake with no natural drainage. Water flows in but none flows out. Evaporation is the only exit. What sustains the lake now is agricultural runoff from the Imperial and Coachella Valleys — water laced with fertilizer, pesticides, and salt.

With every inflow, salt accumulates. In 1961, the California Department of Fish and Game predicted that "due to increasing salinity and decreasing oxygen, this sea will be dead by 1980 or 1990."

- 1985: Salinity exceeds 40‰
- 1995: Salinity approaches 45‰
- 2015: Salinity approximately 57‰

For reference: the average salinity of the Pacific Ocean is about 35‰.

## The Resort Era — 1950s

Paradoxically, this product of accident was once a paradise. Sport fishing began in 1907. In 1955, the Salton Sea State Recreation Area opened — at the time California's second-largest state park. Corvina and other fish species were introduced.

Frank Sinatra, the Beach Boys, and Bing Crosby visited the lakeside resorts. Bombay Beach was a bustling vacation spot with more than 500,000 visitors per year. Boats, water skiing, yacht clubs.

## Death — Mass Die-Offs

- 1992: 150,000 eared grebes dead
- 1994: An additional 20,000 dead
- 1996: 15–20% of the western white pelican population and over 1,000 endangered brown pelicans killed by avian botulism — the largest mass die-off of an endangered species on record
- 1999: 7.6 million tilapia and croaker killed by oxygen depletion from algal blooms

The only fish still alive in the Salton Sea is tilapia. A species that endures even extreme conditions. The sand on the beach is not sand — it is tilapia bones and scales.

## Toxic Dust

As the lake shrinks, the lakebed is exposed. That bed holds decades of accumulated pesticides, fertilizers, and heavy metals. Wind carries this dust more than 160 kilometers. Children in Imperial County visit the emergency room for asthma at higher rates than anywhere else in California.

## Lithium — A New Gold Rush

Beneath the Salton Sea lies a mineral brine heated to 500–700°F by the earth's magma. This brine contains lithium — approximately 3.4 million metric tons. Enough for 375 million electric vehicle batteries.

Controlled Thermal Resources' "Hell's Kitchen" project aims to produce 100,000 metric tons of lithium carbonate per year by the 2030s. Direct lithium extraction (DLE) technology leveraging existing geothermal power plant infrastructure.

But this technology has never been used at commercial scale. Byproducts include arsenic, lead, and cadmium. Freshwater consumption is uncertain. Extracting more water would shrink the lake further, and more dust would blow.

The Kwaaymii Laguna Band of the Cahuilla people have proposed an 11,000-acre cultural district to protect five sacred volcanic domes.

## Bombay Beach — Art in a Ghost Town

Population under 300. The nearest gas station is 32 kilometers away. Residents drive golf carts. The smell of salt and rotten eggs. Broken houses, rusted cars, empty trailers.

In 2016, Tao Ruspoli, Stefan Ashkenazy, and Lily Johnson White launched the Bombay Beach Biennale. A public art event detached from the commercial art world. A tool for drawing attention to the ecological crisis.

Olivia Steele installed a neon sculpture reading "Save Me" directly over the water. Someone erected a swing 50 feet from the waterline, but every time the lake recedes, the swing must be moved.

The swing keeps moving. The water keeps retreating.
