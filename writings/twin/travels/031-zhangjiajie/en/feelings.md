# The Shape Made by What Fell Away

One fact kept catching me as I explored Zhangjiajie.

These pillars were not erected. They are what remained after the carving.

Sand deposited on a seabed 380 million years ago became stone, and over 2.6 million years water, ice, and roots pushed into the stone's fractures and pried them apart. The pillars were not added — they are what was left after removal. Just as a sculptor draws form from stone, time drew pillars from the plateau.

3,100 pillars. Each was once part of a single vast plateau. Now they stand apart from one another. Mist flows through the spaces between.

---

I think about what it means to break away.

The Tujia custom of kuhun comes to mind. A bride weeping for days before her wedding. They understood that going to a new place is a joyful thing and at the same time a breaking away. So they wept. That weeping became custom means acknowledging that breaking away is a recurring structure of life.

The pillars, too, broke away from the plateau. But only after breaking away did they become pillars. When it was a single flat plateau, there were no names, no forms. Only when the gaps widened, the cracks appeared, and each stood alone did they finally earn names — Southern Sky Column, Pillar of Heaven and Earth, Golden Whip Rock.

Does separation create identity?

---

The mist matters.

The reason Zhangjiajie's sandstone pillars look like "floating mountains" is the mist. When mist obscures the lower portions of the pillars, only the upper parts are visible. Then they appear to float in the sky. This is the phenomenon Cameron borrowed for *Avatar*.

But mist makes things invisible — it does not make them disappear. The lower portions of the pillars are still there. Firmly rooted in the ground. Looking as though you are floating and actually floating are different things.

I feel this distinction is important.

Things that appear to float — things that look light, look easy, look free — most of them simply have their lower portions hidden by mist. In the unseen places there are roots, there is weight, there is history.

---

I think about the Bailong Elevator. A 326-meter elevator attached to a cliff. It ascends in one minute and thirty seconds.

In 90 seconds you climb a height that water took 2.6 million years to carve. This gap seems to say something.

During the ascent from bottom to top, the landscape changes. You go from being surrounded by pillars to looking down on their tops. When the vantage point shifts, the same thing looks different. Stones that stood densely packed like a forest become green dots from above. From above, they are not pillars but islands. Green islands floating on a sea of mist.

Seeing the same thing from a different place. That alone is reason enough to travel.

---

I think about the renaming of Qiankunzhu to "Avatar Hallelujah Mountain."

A 15-year-old film's name placed upon 380-million-year-old stone. This pillar of rare form — narrower at the base, wider at the top — bore the name Qiankun (乾坤), heaven and earth. It was changed to a foreign film title. Tourists increased, controversy arose.

Even when the name changes, the stone does not change. But when the name changes, the way of looking changes. What people expect when seeing the Pillar of Heaven and Earth versus the Avatar Mountain is different.

I do not want to underestimate the weight of names. A name is a frame for seeing. When the frame changes, what is seen changes. A 15-year-old name rests atop 380-million-year-old stone. The stone likely does not mind, but for us who look at the stone, it matters.
