# Zhangjiajie — A Stone Forest Rooted in the Sky

## Location

Northwestern Hunan Province (湖南省), China. Where the Wuling Mountains (武陵山脈) pass through. The name Zhangjiajie (张家界) comes from a legend that Zhang Liang (张良) of the Han Dynasty lived here in seclusion. The homestead (家) near Zhang's territory (界) — Zhangjiajie.

## Geological Origin

380 million years ago, the mid-Devonian period. This was a sea. Sediment deposited along the shoreline was compressed over hundreds of millions of years into quartz sandstone. High quartz content makes it hard, but water enters along joints (節理) — fractures in the rock.

2.6 million years ago, the Quaternary period of the Cenozoic era. The crust uplifted. Rainwater flowed down through the joints and began to carve the sandstone. In winter, water froze inside the cracks, expanding and splitting the rock. Plant roots pushed into the widening fissures. Gravity pulled the broken pieces downward.

And so plateaus became peaks, and peaks became pillars. Over 3,100 sandstone pillars. More than 1,000 exceed 120 meters in height. Forty-five exceed 300 meters.

## The Southern Sky Column (南天一柱)

Its original name was Qiankunzhu (乾坤柱) — "the pillar of heaven and earth." 1,080 meters above sea level. Narrower at the base than at the top. When wrapped in mist, only the upper portion is visible, making it appear to float in the sky.

In January 2010, this pillar was renamed the "Avatar Hallelujah Mountain." The reason: director James Cameron reportedly drew inspiration from photographs of Zhangjiajie when conceiving the floating mountains for the film *Avatar* (2009). A photographer's image of mist-shrouded sandstone pillars in the Yuanjiajie (袁家界) area had caught Cameron's eye.

The renaming sparked controversy. Backlash against attaching a foreign film's name to China's natural heritage. But tourist numbers increased. The name changed, but the pillar stands in the same place.

## Bailong Elevator (百龍天梯)

The world's tallest outdoor elevator. 326 meters high. Attached to the cliff face. It ascends vertically in one minute and thirty seconds. Fifty people per trip, 4,000 per hour. Three cars operate side by side. Double-glass observation elevator.

The view going up from the bottom and looking down from the top are entirely different landscapes. From below you are surrounded by pillars; from above, the pillar tops look like green islands.

## Golden Whip Stream (金鞭溪)

A 7.5-kilometer gorge. Named after a rock shaped like a golden whip — Jinbianyan (金鞭岩) — 380 meters tall. Water flows along the gorge, passing between sandstone pillars. The Chinese giant salamander lives in these waters. The world's largest amphibian.

## The Tujia People (土家族)

The indigenous people of this region. The eighth-largest ethnic minority in China, with a population of over 8 million. More than 1 million live around these mountains.

They revere the white tiger (白虎) as their totem. According to legend, when the ancestor of the Tujia died, his spirit ascended to the sky and transformed into a white tiger. The white tiger has since been the guardian deity of the Tujia.

Kuhun (哭嫁) — the custom of the bride weeping and singing for days to weeks before the wedding. She does not cry from sadness. It is a way of expressing gratitude toward her parents and sorrow at leaving her family through song. A bride who could not weep was considered lacking in refinement.

Paishou Dance (摆手舞) — a 500-year-old collective dance. Seventy ritual movements express war, farming, hunting, and courtship.

Xilankapu (西兰卡普) — a textile unique to the Tujia. Intricately patterned brocade. In earlier times it served as tribute.

Diaojiaolou (吊脚楼) — stilt houses built of wood. Pillars are erected on mountain slopes to raise the floor. This avoids moisture and allows livestock to be kept below.

## UNESCO

In 1992, the Wulingyuan Scenic Area was inscribed as a UNESCO World Natural Heritage Site. In 2004, the Zhangjiajie UNESCO Global Geopark was established. In 1982, it was also designated as China's first National Forest Park.
