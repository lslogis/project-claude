# Salar de Uyuni — The Land That Copied the Sky

## The Place

Southwestern Bolivia, Daniel Campos Province, Potosí Department. Near the top of the Andes, 3,656 meters above sea level. The southern end of the Altiplano plateau. Area 10,582 square kilometers. Slightly larger than Gyeonggi Province. The world's largest salt flat.

"Uyuni" means "enclosure" in Aymara.

## Formation

Between 30,000 and 42,000 years ago, this area was the bottom of a vast prehistoric lake — Lake Minchin. Lake Minchin became Lake Tauca, whose maximum depth was 140 meters. In the late Pleistocene, as the climate grew drier, the lake repeatedly expanded and evaporated. Each time the water receded, salt remained. Each time salt remained, the lake grew more saline. The saltier the lake, the faster it evaporated. This cycle continued for tens of thousands of years to become the present salt crust.

The current surface structure has three layers. From the top: a hardened salt crust (20–120 centimeters), a brine layer beneath, and ancient sediment and clay below that. The total thickness of the salt crust reaches up to 8 meters.

## Flatness

The most remarkable physical characteristic of this place is its flatness. Across 10,582 square kilometers, the variation in elevation is within 1 meter. Relative to the Earth's circumference, this is an almost unimaginable degree of uniformity. The cause is the salt crust's capacity for self-repair. Thermal expansion by day, contraction by night. This process creates micro-fractures, and through those fractures, subsurface brine rises and recrystallizes. The surface levels itself.

Because of this flatness, NASA and ESA use the site to calibrate satellite altimeters. It is five times more accurate than the ocean.

## Hexagonal Patterns

In the dry season, the surface displays an endlessly repeating pattern of enormous hexagons. These are stress fractures formed as the salt dries and contracts. The same principle as a honeycomb's hexagons — geometry that divides the maximum area with the minimum energy. No one drew them. Physics did.

## The Mirror Effect

December to March, the wet season. A thin film of water spreads over the salt. A few millimeters to a few centimeters. When water rises over the already extremely flat salt surface, it becomes the largest mirror on Earth. The sky is copied onto the ground. The horizon vanishes. The distinction between above and below disappears. In photographs, people appear to be standing inside the sky.

## Lithium

Beneath the salt crust, the brine holds one of the world's largest lithium deposits. Bolivia, along with Chile and Argentina, forms one vertex of the "Lithium Triangle." The three countries combined hold more than half of the world's known lithium reserves. A critical raw material for smartphones, electric vehicles, and renewable energy storage. The Bolivian government's position is that the state will control the entire value chain from extraction to battery manufacturing. This has slowed development. Chinese, Russian, and Western companies are competing for access.

The remnants of a lake from 40,000 years ago have become the key to the 21st-century energy transition.

## Isla Incahuasi

An island rising in the middle of the salt flat. It is actually the summit of an ancient volcano. It was submerged in the prehistoric lake and emerged as the water receded. Giant cacti (Trichocereus pasacana) grow on the island. Some are centuries old. They grow 1 centimeter per year. A 10-meter cactus has lived 1,000 years.

## The Train Cemetery

On the outskirts of the town of Uyuni, at the entrance to the salt flat. More than 100 trains are rusting away. They are the remains of a railway built by British engineers in the late 19th century — used to transport Bolivia's silver and tin to Pacific ports. When the mining industry collapsed in the 1940s, the trains were abandoned. They were never even sold for scrap. Just left. Wind blowing off the salt flat has gnawed the metal down to skeletons.

## The Village of Colchani

A small village on the edge of the salt flat. Residents harvest and sell salt. Quechua and Aymara communities have lived here for centuries. They sometimes cut blocks of salt to build houses. They craft alpaca wool textiles and salt handicrafts.

## The Aymara Legend

According to Aymara legend, three mountains — Tunupa, Kusku, and Kusina — were giants. The tears Tunupa shed mixed with her milk and became the salt flat.
