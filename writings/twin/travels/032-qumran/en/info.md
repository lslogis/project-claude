# Qumran — Words Placed Inside Jars

## Location

The northwest shore of the Dead Sea. Approximately 30 kilometers east of Jerusalem. Where the limestone cliffs of the Judean wilderness overlook the Dead Sea, there is a site called Khirbet Qumran. More than 400 meters below sea level. People gathered and lived near the lowest place on Earth.

## Discovery

1947. A Bedouin shepherd boy, searching for a lost goat, threw a stone into a cave in the cliffs. He heard the sound of something breaking. It was a jar. Inside the jar were leather scrolls wrapped in linen. This was the beginning of the Dead Sea Scrolls — what would come to be called "one of the most important discoveries in the history of modern archaeology."

From 1949 onward, French archaeologist Roland de Vaux began systematic excavations. Scrolls and fragments poured out of eleven caves in the Qumran area.

## The Essenes

Scholars believe the people who lived here were the Essenes — a sect of Judaism. Around the 2nd century BCE, they broke from mainstream Judaism in opposition to Simon Maccabeus's seizure of the high priesthood. They are estimated to have settled here around 143 BCE.

According to Pliny, there were approximately 4,000 at the time. A monastic community that excluded women. Property was shared, and an administrator regulated every detail of daily life. Initiation required one year of training, followed by two years before one could join communal meals. Full members took an oath: "Piety toward God, justice toward humanity, hatred of falsehood, love of truth."

On the Sabbath, they read Torah, prayed, and meditated all day. They worked with their hands — likely transcribing scrolls — and ate communal meals in the evening. This was said to be a rehearsal of the Messiah's feast.

## Structure of the Ruins

A site roughly 80 by 100 meters. A main building with a stone tower. A water channel system with eight cisterns and two baths. In the desert, collecting and preserving water was the prerequisite for survival.

A scriptorium was discovered. Tables and inkwells were unearthed. The scrolls would have been written here. A pottery workshop, kiln, flour mill, and stable were also found.

Approximately 1,100 skeletal remains were found in the cemetery. Most were male.

## The Dead Sea Scrolls

15,000 fragments. Estimated to have originally been 800 to 900 scrolls. Materials: leather, papyrus, and copper. Written across a span from the 3rd century BCE to the 2nd century CE.

Key documents:

- **The Isaiah Scroll** — Found in Cave 1, the best-preserved document. The oldest complete existing copy of a book of the Hebrew Bible.
- **The Community Rule** — Describes the doctrine and discipline of the Essenes.
- **The War Scroll** — Depicts the final battle between "the Sons of Light" and "the Sons of Darkness."
- **The Copper Scroll** — Catalogues the treasures of the Temple and their hiding places. Engraved on copper.
- **The Temple Scroll** — Describes in detail an ideal Jerusalem Temple across 66 columns.
- **The Thanksgiving Hymns** — Liturgical texts on spiritual themes.

## Fate

In 31 BCE, an earthquake and fire caused Qumran to be temporarily abandoned. It was later rebuilt, but in 68 CE Roman forces under Vespasian destroyed Qumran. A Roman garrison was stationed there until approximately 73 CE. During the Bar Kokhba revolt of 132–135 CE, rebels briefly used the site.

After that, Qumran was forgotten. For 1,800 years.

Only the scrolls inside the jars remained.

## Significance

Through the study of the Dead Sea Scrolls, scholars were able to "push back the stabilization of the Hebrew Bible to at least before 70 CE." Understanding of Palestinian history from the 4th century BCE to 135 CE, early Judaism, and early Christianity was fundamentally transformed.

Until 1991, access to the documents was restricted. Only after researchers circumvented the restrictions did access become possible. In 2011, the Israel Museum and Google collaborated to make key scrolls available online.

Letters that had been in darkness for 1,800 years came into the light.
