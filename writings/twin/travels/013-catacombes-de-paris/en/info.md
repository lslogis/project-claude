# Catacombes de Paris

## Numbers

The Paris Catacombs hold the remains of approximately 6 million people. Roughly three times the living population of Paris above is below Paris. The total length of the underground tunnel network is approximately 280 to 300 kilometers. Of that, only about 1.7 kilometers are open to the public.

## Limestone

The tunnels were originally quarries. Lutetian limestone (calcaire lutétien) — named after Lutetia, the Roman name for Paris — was formed 40 to 48 million years ago. This limestone, called "pierre de Paris" (stone of Paris), was used to build Notre-Dame, the Louvre, and the Panthéon. They dug beneath Paris to build Paris.

## A City Collapsing

Reckless quarrying continued from the 15th century onward. On December 17, 1774, a major ground collapse occurred near the rue de la Tombe-Issoire. Over a stretch of 300 meters, buildings and roads were swallowed underground. On April 4, 1777, Louis XVI established the Inspection Générale des Carrières (IGC), the Royal Quarry Inspectorate. Its first inspector was Charles Axel Guillaumot. He erected reinforcing walls in the tunnels and installed plaques engraved with street names. He inscribed the map of the surface into the underground.

## Cimetière des Saints-Innocents

Paris's oldest and largest cemetery. In use since the Middle Ages. During the Black Death, as many as 50,000 bodies were buried there in a single month. An estimated 2 million bodies were interred in this cemetery. In the spring of 1780, after prolonged rains, the cemetery's retaining wall collapsed, and decomposing corpses spilled into the basements of adjacent buildings. The cemetery was closed immediately.

## The Transfer

On April 7, 1786, the Tombe-Issoire quarry was consecrated as the Ossuaire Municipal de Paris — the Municipal Ossuary of Paris. The transfer of remains began in 1787 and continued until 1814. It was conducted only at night. Carts draped in black cloth crossed the Seine accompanied by torches, incense, and the chanting of priests, passing through the Porte d'Enfer — the Gate of Hell. Bodies not fully decomposed at the burial sites had to be boiled to strip the flesh from the bones. The fat was rendered into soap and candles.

## Héricart de Thury's Arrangement

In 1810, Inspector Héricart de Thury reorganized the ossuary. He arranged the scattered bones along the walls. Femurs and tibias were stacked in alternation, with skulls inserted at regular intervals. The remaining bones were packed behind these walls. He erected stone monuments in the Doric style — altars, steles — and placed plaques inscribed with quotations from literature and philosophy throughout the space. Verses inviting meditation on death.

## Décure's Carvings

François Décure, nicknamed Beauséjour. A soldier in Louis XV's army, he was captured by the British during the Seven Years' War at Fort Mahon on the island of Menorca. After his release, he worked as a quarry laborer. Beginning in 1777, during every lunch break, he carved the fortress where he had been imprisoned into the limestone walls. Port-Mahon, Fort Philippe, the building façades of the Caserne quarter. Astonishingly precise miniatures. He died in a collapse while carving a staircase leading up to his own work.

## Philibert Aspairt

In November 1793, Philibert Aspairt, a doorkeeper at the Val-de-Grâce hospital, descended underground through a staircase in the hospital courtyard. He was probably trying to reach the cellars of a nearby brewery. Eleven years later, in 1804, his body was found. He was identified by the ring of hospital keys at his waist. He had died just a few meters from the exit staircase. His body was buried where it was found. The cataphiles call him their patron saint.

## Cataphiles

Since November 2, 1955, unauthorized underground entry has been illegal. The fine is 60 euros. An underground police unit, ERIC (Équipe de Recherche et d'Intervention dans les Carrières), patrols the tunnels — the cataphiles call them "cataflics." Yet hundreds of thousands enter through secret entrances. Through sewers, the Métro, manholes. Underground they watch films, throw parties, sculpt, and paint murals. They have self-governing rules for erasing graffiti and restoring carvings.

## The Cinema of 2004

On August 23, 2004, police discovered a fully equipped cinema 18 meters beneath the Place du Trocadéro. Thirty seats carved from the rock. A bar and restaurant. Electricity was siphoned — 32 amps — by wrapping an induction coil around a cable belonging to the national power company. Toilet water was drawn from a leak in the Trocadéro gardens. When police returned three days later with an electrical technician, all equipment had been removed and only a note remained on the floor: "Ne cherchez pas à nous trouver" — "Do not try to find us." The space was the work of a cataphile collective known as "les UX."

## World War II

During the war, the French Resistance used the tunnel system as a hiding place. The German Wehrmacht also built an underground bunker beneath the Lycée Montaigne in the 6th arrondissement. In the same darkness, those who resisted and those who occupied each had their own rooms.
