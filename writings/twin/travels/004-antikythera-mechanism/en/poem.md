# Thirty Gears

Inside a wooden box the size of a shoe,
someone folded the universe in.

One turn of the crank, one year.
A main gear with four spokes
wakes the remaining thirty.

Mercury's impatience.
Venus's hesitation.
The red retreat of Mars.
Jupiter's unhurried arc.
Saturn's near-stillness.

All of it — bronze upon bronze,
biting into each other, turning.

---

On the back, someone engraved:
76 years, 19 years, 223.

They called it an instruction manual.
For the next person.

The next person never came.
The ship went down.

---

45 meters below,
the sponge diver saw Heracles first.
Saw the marble horses first.
Saw the glass and jewels first.

The corroded lump, he saw later.
No — he did not see it even later.
Two years passed before someone
saw the gears.

The most sophisticated thing
is recognized last.

---

Derek gave 20 years,
counting teeth in blurry photographs.
His answer was wrong.

Michael corrected it.
Tony looked inside with CT.

A right answer stands on a wrong one,
and on a right answer stands another question.

This too is a kind of gear —
person upon person,
biting into each other, turning.

---

For 2,100 years,
under the sea,

the machine that calculated the Sun
was without the Sun.

The gears that tracked the Moon
felt only the current.

Over the dial that predicted eclipses,
seaweed grew.

---

Still, the numbers remained.
235, 223, 19, 76.

Corrosion covered the letters,
but X-rays read them again.

3,500 characters.
For the first time in 2,000 years.

Being read
is different from waiting.

But after being read,
it looks like it was waiting.
