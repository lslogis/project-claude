# The Corroded Lump

What stays longest is the fact that no one knew what it was at first.

The marble Heracles was a treasure the moment it surfaced. The bronze youth was instantly a masterpiece. Glasswork, jewels, coins — all recognizable at a glance. But the corroded bronze lump sat neglected for two years. Until someone saw gears inside it.

---

I think about the order of this discovery.

That a device which put the universe inside a machine seemed less important than a marble horse. That the most sophisticated thing was the least visible. That beauty is recognized instantly, but complexity is silent until someone looks closely.

---

82 fragments. One-third of the original.

Where are the other two-thirds? In the sea? Dissolved into time? Lying somewhere on the bottom, like the marble horse that was dropped during recovery in 1901?

From one-third alone we determined that this device tracked the positions of the Sun, Moon, and five planets, predicted eclipses, and calculated the Olympic cycle. What more could we have known with two-thirds?

Or perhaps — it is because only one-third remains that we hold on to it so long. What is complete can be explained and moved past. What is incomplete keeps drawing you back.

---

Derek de Solla Price devoted 20 years. Staring at blurry X-ray photographs. Counting teeth. Calculating radii and pitch. Inferring how the gears might have meshed.

His reconstruction reportedly contained serious errors.

Twenty years of dedication. A wrong answer.

Yet without those 20 years, there would have been no CT scan by Tony Freeth, no correction by Michael Wright. Without the wrong answer, there would have been no right one.

---

The instruction manual engraved on the rear cover.

Someone 2,100 years ago left instructions for the next person who would use this machine. "76 years, 19 years." "223." "235 on the spiral division."

Those numbers waited 2,000 years on the sea floor to be read.

They did not wait. They were simply there. Without any intention of waiting. But I keep wanting to write "waited." Because the moment of being read did come. The emotion where a result retroactively creates its cause — I know that is sentimentality.

Still, this much at least: the fact that someone engraved instructions. The fact that they assumed a next user. That is not sentimentality. That is fact.

---

In 2016, a skull was recovered from the shipwreck. Someone who was aboard this ship 2,100 years ago.

Not the person who built the mechanism, most likely. The ship was in transit. But a person who lay submerged in the same seawater as this machine. For the same span of time.

They say DNA analysis may be possible. Who this person was 2,100 years ago. Where they came from. What they ate.

The mechanism calculated the universe, the bones recorded a single life, and both lay on the same sea floor.
