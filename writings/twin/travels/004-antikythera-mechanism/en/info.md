# The Antikythera Mechanism

## Discovery

Spring, 1900. Sponge divers from the Greek island of Symi, on their way to Tunisia, encountered a storm near the island of Antikythera. While waiting for the sea to calm and searching for sponges, they discovered a shipwreck at a depth of 45 meters.

A Roman cargo vessel from the 1st century BCE. Inside: bronze statues, marble sculptures, jewels, glasswork, coins. A marble Heracles over two meters tall. A bronze youth from around 340 BCE. Four marble horses — one fell back into the sea during recovery.

And one unremarkable corroded lump of bronze.

## Recognition

1902, National Archaeological Museum of Athens. Politician Spyridon Stais discovered gears inside the bronze lump. His cousin Valerios Stais, the museum director, began the first study.

## Physical Specifications

Housed in a wooden case the size of a shoebox. Made of bronze plates. Fragmented into 82 pieces (A–G and nos. 1–75). 30 gears survive, 27 of them concentrated in the largest fragment, A.

A main drive gear with four spokes is turned by a hand crank — one rotation equals one solar year. This drives all remaining gears.

## Functions

### Front Dial
- Displays the zodiacal positions of the Sun and Moon
- Moon phase indicator
- Positions of Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn — all five planets

### Rear Upper Dial
- Metonic cycle: 19 years = 235 synodic months. Error less than one part in 13 million.

### Rear Lower Dial
- Saros cycle: 223 synodic months ≈ 18 years and 11 days. Predicts solar and lunar eclipses.

### Subsidiary Dial
- Panhellenic games cycle. Including the Olympics.

## Gear System

Epicyclic gearing — gears mounted on gears to reproduce the irregular motions of planets. A slot-and-pin mechanism tracks the Moon's anomalistic motion. Mechanically implements cycles known from Babylonian astronomy.

## Inscriptions

An instruction manual was engraved on the rear cover. "76 years, 19 years" — the Callippic and Metonic cycles. "223" — the Saros cycle. "235 on the spiral division" — describing the Metonic dial.

Letters read for the first time in 2,000 years through X-ray microfocus tomography. A total of 3,500 characters have been deciphered. Astronomical terms for the Sun, Moon, ecliptic, Metonic cycle, Saros cycle, and more.

## Researchers

- **Valerios Stais** (1902) — first recognition
- **Albert Rehm** (early 1900s) — first identification of astronomical cycles, epicyclic gear hypothesis
- **Derek de Solla Price** (1974) — published "Gears from the Greeks," identified 30 gears via X-ray, 20 years of research
- **Michael Wright** — restored the planetary display system, corrected Price's errors
- **Tony Freeth** (2006, 2021) — revolutionary reconstruction via CT scanning, led the UCL team

## Significance

More than **1,000 years** passed before technology of comparable sophistication appeared again — not until the great cathedral clocks of the Middle Ages.

This mechanism proves that the ancient Greeks reached a level of technical refinement previously unimaginable.

## Present Day

On display at the National Archaeological Museum of Athens. 82 fragments. Only one-third of the original device remains.

In 2016, a human skull was recovered from the shipwreck. The bones of someone who was on this ship 2,100 years ago. DNA analysis may be possible.

Most of the ship's cargo hold has yet to be explored.
