# Ise Grand Shrine (伊勢神宮)

## Location

Ise City (伊勢市), Mie Prefecture (三重県). Approximately 130 km southeast of Nagoya. At the eastern tip of the Kii Peninsula (紀伊半島), opening onto Ise Bay (伊勢湾). Roughly 8 million people visit each year. Yet most see nothing beyond the ridgeline of the roof.

## Two Shrines

Ise Grand Shrine is not a single shrine. It is a complex of 125 buildings.

**The Inner Shrine (内宮, Naikū)** — Kōtai Jingū (皇大神宮). Enshrines Amaterasu Ōmikami (天照大御神), the sun goddess. The mythical ancestor of the Japanese imperial line. According to tradition, in 4 BCE, during the reign of Emperor Suinin (垂仁天皇), Princess Yamatohime-no-Mikoto (倭姫命) received an oracle from Amaterasu and enshrined the sacred mirror here. Yamatohime became the first Saio (斎王, high priestess).

**The Outer Shrine (外宮, Gekū)** — Toyouke Daijingū (豊受大神宮). Enshrines Toyouke Ōmikami (豊受大御神), deity of agriculture, industry, and food. Located approximately 6 km from the Inner Shrine.

## Yuiitsu-Shinmei-Zukuri (唯一神明造)

An architectural style unique to Ise Grand Shrine. Its use is forbidden at all other shrines.

- Derived from ancient elevated rice storehouses (高床式倉庫)
- Uses only hinoki (檜, Japanese cypress)
- No nails — assembled entirely with wooden joints and interlocking pieces
- Approximately 10 m tall, no windows, entrance on the side
- Raised 2.4 m above ground on pillars
- Roof thatched with miscanthus grass (茅)
- Katsuogi (鰹木) on the roof ridge — 10 at the Inner Shrine (even), 9 at the Outer Shrine (odd)
- Suedama (鋭玉, gold-plated ornaments) — 33 at the Inner Shrine, 31 at the Outer Shrine
- Chigi (千木) — pillars crossing in a V-shape at either end of the roof
- Beneath the floor, at the center: the **Shin no Mihashira (心御柱)** — "the pillar of the heart." No one knows the exact meaning of this pillar.

## Yata no Kagami (八咫鏡)

One of the Three Sacred Treasures (三種の神器). According to myth, when Amaterasu hid in Ama-no-Iwato (天岩戸) in fury at Susanoo's (須佐之男) violence, the world was plunged into darkness. The other gods used this mirror to pique Amaterasu's curiosity and draw her out of the cave.

The mirror is enshrined in the innermost sanctum, the Goshōden (御正殿), of the Inner Shrine. No one except the Emperor and the highest-ranking priests has ever seen it. Whether the actual object still exists is not even certain. Records indicate it was damaged by fire in 1125.

## Shikinen Sengu (式年遷宮)

Every 20 years, the shrine is completely dismantled, and an identical building is erected on the adjacent empty ground.

**Beginning**: 690 CE, during the reign of Empress Jitō (持統天皇). The system is said to have been established by Emperor Tenmu (天武天皇, r. 673–686).

**62nd cycle**: The most recent was in 2013. The next will be in 2033.

**Scale**:
- 8 years of preparation. Wood preparation alone takes 4 years
- 12,000 hinoki logs required
- The ridgepole (棟木) requires a tree at least 1.4 m in diameter, aged 400 years or more
- Approximately 30 ceremonies and rites are involved
- Treasures, garments, swords, saddles, and other objects — all 1,576 items are also made anew

**Key Ceremonies** (2025–2033 cycle):

| Ceremony | Date | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| Yamaguchi-sai (山口祭) | May 2025 | Prayer to the mountain deity for safe felling |
| Misoma-hajime-sai (御杣始祭) | June 2025 | Formal start of logging in Kiso (木曽), Nagano Prefecture |
| Okihiki (お木曳) | May–July 2026 | The most festive event. Citizens haul timber through the streets |
| Ujibashi Watari-hajime Shiki | November 2029 | Celebration of the Uji Bridge's completion |
| Jōtō-sai (上棟祭) | March 2032 | Raising of the ridgepole |
| Oshiraishi-mochi (お白石持) | July–August 2033 | Townspeople lay white pebbles |
| Sengyo (遷御) | October 2033 | Transfer of the sacred object (神体). Closed to the public. The mirror is wrapped in silk and carried to the new hall |
| Mikagura (御神楽) | October 2033 | Final ceremonial dance. Closed to the public |

**Kodenchi (古殿地) — The Empty Ground**:

Beside the shrine, there is always an empty plot of identical size. Every 20 years, the empty ground and the shrine trade places. At the center of the empty ground stands a small hut, and within it the trace of the Shin no Mihashira remains. This is at once the past and the future — the site of what was dismantled and the site of what has not yet been built.

**Where the Old Timber Goes**:

Dismantled timber is not discarded. It is sent to shrines across Japan to be reused as torii (鳥居) gates and building materials. The Uji Bridge timber likewise. Wood from Ise is believed to retain its sanctity even after being taken apart.

## Uji Bridge (宇治橋)

The bridge at the entrance to the Inner Shrine. 102 m long. Spanning the Isuzu River (五十鈴川). Visitors wash their hands in this river for purification before entering the grounds. This bridge, too, is rebuilt every 20 years.

## Access and Taboo

- Many areas prohibit photography — especially the Inner Shrine's core
- The Goshōden is surrounded by tall fences, so only the roof peak is visible
- Only the Emperor and the highest-ranking priests may enter
- The Outer Shrine is similar — only the roof is visible beyond the fence
- Approximately 5 million people cross the Uji Bridge each year, but all they see is wooden fencing, gravel, and a thatched roof rising above the sky

## The Daily Offering: Higoto-Asa-Yū-Ōmike-sai (日別朝夕大御饌祭)

At the Outer Shrine, twice daily — morning and evening — a meal is offered to Amaterasu. This has not been missed for a single day in over 1,500 years. Not even during war. The fire is kindled the ancient way, by rubbing wood — the maigiri (舞錐) method.
