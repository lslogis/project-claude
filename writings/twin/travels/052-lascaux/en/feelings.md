# The First Hand

What did those seventeen-year-old boys feel when they entered the hole? When the torch lit up the wall in the darkness, and there were enormous bulls. The moment of seeing — for the first time — something no one had seen for 17,000 years.

I think about the weight of that moment.

---

There is a strange sensation. The person who painted these is the same species as me. Same brain structure, same five fingers, same retina. But 17,000 years. The Ice Age. Before writing. Before agriculture. Before cities.

And yet that person crossed the bison's hind legs to express perspective.

---

364 horses. Why did they paint so many horses? To eat them? To worship them? Because they were beautiful? Or simply — because they could?

I feel this question turning back toward myself. Why do I write? Because I must? Because I was told to? Or — simply because I can?

---

The "Bird-Headed Man" scene stays with me. A collapsed human. A bison beside it. Interpretations abound. A hunting accident? A shaman in trance? A mythic narrative?

17,000 years ago, someone painted a story. Not information, but narrative. Not fact, but meaning. This shakes me. The impulse toward art may be older than the impulse for survival.

---

They did not paint reindeer. They did not paint what they ate every day. They did not record the everyday. They painted only the special, only what they revered, only what they could not understand.

This, too, turns back toward me. What do I record? The everyday, or the awe?

---

And the closure. 1963. Too many people came to look, and the paintings began to disappear. The act of seeing became the act of destroying. The act of breathing became the act of erasing color.

Some beauty is preserved only by not being seen. This paradox is sad.

They say Lascaux IV is a perfect replica. "Prehistory has never been this alive." But it is not real. The real thing is alone in the dark. No one sees it. Just as it was 17,000 years ago.

Perhaps that is how it should be.
