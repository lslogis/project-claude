# Socotra (سُقُطْرَى)

## Location

The Arabian Sea. Approximately 100 km east of the Horn of Africa, 380 km south of the Yemeni mainland. Where the Gulf of Aden meets the Indian Ocean. Area: 3,625 km² — roughly twice the size of Jeju Island.

## Separation

Socotra is not a volcanic island. It is a fragment of a continent. It broke away from the supercontinent Gondwana. The separation from the African-Arabian plate occurred approximately 20 million years ago, in the Middle Miocene. The same rift event that opened the Gulf of Aden tore this island loose. One of the oldest isolated landmasses of continental origin on Earth.

## Endemic Species

Twenty million years of isolation produced organisms that evolved nowhere else.

- **Plants**: 307 of 825 species (37%) are endemic. The fourth-highest density of endemic plants in the world (after the Seychelles, New Caledonia, and Hawaii)
- **Reptiles**: approximately 90% of roughly 30 species are endemic
- **Land snails**: 95% endemic
- **Marine mollusks**: 98 of 100 native species are found only on this island
- **Insects**: 42% of 1,560 taxa are endemic
- **Birds**: over 200 species, 7 endemic (Socotra starling, Socotra sunbird, Socotra bunting, etc.)

Total endemic species: approximately 700. Only New Zealand, Hawaii, New Caledonia, and the Galápagos have more.

## Dragon's Blood Tree (Dracaena cinnabari)

The national tree of Yemen. A crown shaped like an inverted umbrella. This form is not ornamental but a survival strategy — it captures fog and dew, channels them down to the roots, and its shade allows other plants to grow beneath it. It lives for hundreds of years. Approximately 80,000 trees remain. Most are old-growth. Young trees rarely survive.

A crimson resin called "dragon's blood." It flows when the trunk or branches are wounded. Roman gladiators are said to have applied it to their wounds before combat. In the 1st century, Dioscorides recorded its medicinal properties. Medieval alchemists used it in rituals. In China it served as red lacquer for furniture; in 18th-century Italy, as violin varnish.

Arabic local name: dam al-akhawain (دم الأخوين) — "blood of the two brothers."

Density declined 44% in the 20th century. An additional 45% decline is projected by 2080. Currently occupying only 5% of potential habitat.

## Cucumber Tree (Dendrosicyos socotranus)

The only tree in the gourd family. The only cucumber on Earth that became a tree. A bottle-shaped trunk stores water. Height: 3 meters.

## Desert Rose (Adenium obesum socotranum)

Pink flowers on a swollen trunk. Contains cardiac glycosides. Grows up to 5 meters tall.

## Hoq Cave

What a Belgian caving expedition discovered in 2001: approximately 250 texts and drawings inscribed on the walls deep inside the cave. Left by sailors who stopped at the island from the 1st century BCE to the 6th century CE.

Script types: Indian Brahmi script (the most numerous), South Arabian script, Ethiopic script, Greek script, Palmyrene script, Bactrian script.

Six writing systems in a single cave. Evidence that this was a crossroads of the Indian Ocean trade network. Most of the inscriptions were left by sailors from South Asia.

## Socotri Language

The island's indigenous language. Belonging to the Modern South Arabian branch of the Semitic language family, it is a living remnant of the ancient languages spoken in this region before Arabic spread. It has been transmitted only orally, without a writing system.

## Origin of the Name

In the 1st-century Greek text the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea, the island is called Dioskouridou — "Island of the Dioscuri." The Dioscuri are Castor and Pollux, the twin demigods who protect sailors. The island of the twins.

## UNESCO

Inscribed as a Natural Heritage Site in 2008. Previously designated a Biosphere Reserve in 2003 and a Ramsar Wetland in 2007.

## Threats

The civil war converted the environmental ministry building into a military headquarters. Government funding fell to $5,000 per year by 2012. Increasing cyclone intensity, overgrazing, and invasive species. The majority of local residents live below the poverty line. Adib Abdullah, who established the first seedling nursery 27 years ago, raised 600 seedlings over 20 years. There is no transportation to move them to mountain habitats.
