# The Danakil Depression

## Location

Northeastern Ethiopia, near the borders of Eritrea and Djibouti. 200 km north to south, 50 km east to west. Area approximately 10,000 km². One hundred twenty-five meters below sea level. The third-lowest point on the African continent.

To the west, the Ethiopian Highlands stand like a folding screen; to the east, beyond the Danakil Alps, lies the Red Sea.

## Why Such a Place Exists

Three plates — the African, Arabian, and Somali — meet at the Afar Triple Junction. The three plates diverge in different directions at 1–2 cm per year. The crust stretches, thins, and the land subsides. This is the final stage of a continent splitting apart. In several million years, this place will be sea.

The Red Sea has already entered once; what it left behind when it evaporated is a salt layer thousands of meters thick.

## Temperature

Average annual temperature 36–38°C. Daytime highs exceed 50°C. In records from 1960–1966, Dallol's average annual temperature was 34.4°C — the highest annual average ever recorded for an inhabited place in the history of meteorological observation. Annual precipitation 100–200 mm. It evaporates the moment it falls.

## Dallol

Northern Danakil, approximately 130 meters below sea level. The lowest site of volcanic activity on Earth.

Hydrothermal springs rise around the crater formed by a phreatic eruption in 1926. Water temperature exceeds 94°C. The pH is 0.25 — nearly pure acid. In some pools, pH as low as negative 1.7 has been recorded.

The colors are surreal. Sulfur produces yellows and greens, iron oxide produces oranges and reds, chlorides form white crystals, manganese creates black. These blend together to form a palette almost impossible to believe exists on Earth.

### Gaet'ale Pond

A small pool near Dallol. Salinity of 43% — the saltiest body of water on Earth. Saltier than the Dead Sea (34%).

### The Limits of Life

In January 2017, a research team led by Felipe Gómez of Spain's Centro de Astrobiología discovered Nanohaloarchaea in Dallol's salt chimneys. Ultra-small archaea, twenty times smaller than ordinary bacteria. They were alive in near-zero pH acidity, temperatures above 94°C, and supersaturated brine.

This forced a rewriting of the definition of "conditions in which life cannot exist." NASA classifies Dallol as the closest planetary field analog on Earth to the past hydrothermal environments of Mars.

## Erta Ale

In the Afar language, "the smoking mountain." Elevation 613 meters. A 1,700 × 600-meter caldera at its summit.

Inside it is one of only six active lava lakes on Earth. The longest continuously active lava lake confirmed since the early twentieth century. Convection keeps the molten lava circulating; a thin charcoal crust forms on the surface, and through its cracks, red light seeps out.

The Afar people call this crater "the gateway to hell."

Because daytime temperatures exceed 40°C, the climb begins at 7 PM. Ten kilometers one way, three to four hours. You arrive at the summit around midnight to see the lava lake.

In January 2017, an eruption created a new lava lake to the southeast, while the original lake sank deeper, becoming barely visible through the smoke.

## Salt

The salt flats of Danakil are what the Red Sea left behind when it evaporated. A salt layer thousands of meters thick.

### The Afar People's Salt Mining

A tradition that has continued since the sixth century. Every day except Friday, hundreds of miners go out to the salt flats. They split the ground with hand axes and cut salt blocks. Each block weighs an average of 4 kg.

Caravans of 15–20 camels carry the salt across 80 km of desert. They spend one night in the village of Ahmed Ela, then continue to Berhale, and on to Mekele. One week, one way. The Afar take a 400% margin between purchase and sale price.

These caravans were nearly the only point of contact between the Afar region and the rest of the world.

## Ghost Town

### The Italian Era (1906–1929)

In 1906, the Italian mining company Compagnia Mineraria Coloniale began potash mining at Dallol. Initially transported by camel, a narrow-gauge railway was completed in April 1918 from the port of Mersa Fatma in Eritrea to a point 28 km from Dallol.

Between 1925 and 1929, 25,000 tons of sylvite (70% KCl) were extracted. Total production: 51,000 tons. But when mass supply began from Germany, the United States, and the Soviet Union, the operation lost its profitability.

### The American Era (1954–1968)

After World War II, the British administration dismantled the railway. In 1954, the American company Ralph M. Parsons Company resumed mining. In March 1967, the main mine flooded and extraction ceased; by 1968, all operations had ended.

Ruins from the Italian era, equipment from the American era — all rusting in 50°C heat. Dallol has become a ghost town.

## A Place Abandoned Three Times

The Red Sea entered and evaporated, leaving salt. Italy came and left. America came and left. The plates diverge, the water evaporates, the people depart, and only salt remains.

Only the Afar people remain. Since the sixth century. Every day, hand axe in hand.
