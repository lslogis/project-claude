# Hashima — A Battleship Above the Sea, Mineshafts Below

## Location

Approximately 18 kilometers southwest of Nagasaki harbor. A small reef in the East China Sea. Originally one-third its current size — the island was expanded with waste rock extracted during coal mining. Final dimensions: 480 meters by 160 meters, area 6.3 hectares. From a distance, it resembles a battleship — hence the name Gunkanjima (軍艦島, "Battleship Island"). Its official name is Hashima (端島), "island at the edge."

## The Mine Begins

Coal outcrops were discovered around 1810. In 1890, Mitsubishi purchased the entire island along with its mining rights and commenced full-scale undersea coal mining. Shafts descended 600 to 1,000 meters beneath the island into the seabed. There were four vertical shafts; the deepest, Shaft No. 2, was completed in 1925 and reached 400 meters underground. Japan's first electric elevator was installed here. The extracted coal was supplied as coking coal to the Yawata Steel Works.

## The Island of Concrete

In 1916, Building No. 30 was constructed — Japan's first reinforced-concrete high-rise apartment. Seven stories. Built to withstand typhoons and waves. Afterward, over 30 concrete buildings were packed tightly onto the island. The tallest was nine stories. The entire island was covered in concrete, and buildings were connected by corridors and staircases — this served as the island's road system. Only the managers' residences were wooden; mining families lived eight to a unit of roughly 17 square meters (a 6-mat plus 4.5-mat room).

## Functions of a City

Schools (elementary and middle), a hospital, a temple, a shrine, a cinema, pachinko parlors, barber shops, a public bathhouse, a market, restaurants. Nearly everything needed for daily life was present. At a time when the national television penetration rate was 10%, nearly every household had a set. Rooftop gardens had existed since the 1910s — the first rooftop agriculture on an "island without green." But there was no water. The island had no springs; until an undersea water pipeline was completed in 1957, water was rationed by "water ticket."

## Population Density

In 1960: population 5,267. Residential-area density of 1,391 persons per hectare — nine times that of Tokyo at the time. The most densely populated place on Earth.

## The Dark History

During the Pacific War, as Japanese men were conscripted, Mitsubishi forcibly brought Korean and Chinese prisoners to mine coal. An estimated 500 to 4,000 people. Inside the undersea shafts: 95% humidity, 30°C, working prone in narrow coal seams. Four to five people died in accidents each month. Records indicate that over 1,300 perished during the wartime period. Forced laborers called Hashima "Hell Island." Testimony of Korean laborer Seo Jeong-wu: "The moment I saw Hashima, I lost all hope."

After the atomic bombing of Nagasaki, these forced laborers were sent into the devastated city center for relief work. They were exposed to radiation.

## Closure and Silence

In the 1960s, petroleum began replacing coal. In 1964, a fire allowed seawater to flood the main shafts. Mitsubishi determined that the safely mineable coal was exhausted. The mine closed on January 15, 1974. On April 20, the last residents boarded the boat to Nagasaki. A photograph survives of schoolchildren standing in the playground, forming the words "Sayonara Hashima" with their bodies.

For 35 years, no one entered. Tourism resumed on April 22, 2009, and in 2015 the island was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the "Sites of Japan's Meiji Industrial Revolution."

## Now

The concrete is crumbling. The floors of Building No. 30 have partially collapsed; every window is gone. Visitors can only view the island from a distance at three observation platforms on the southwest side. The dense residential blocks and mine facilities are off-limits. It has been compared to Studio Ghibli's *Castle in the Sky* — a ruined city floating in the air.
