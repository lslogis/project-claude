# Aral Sea

## Original Size

The Aral Sea was the world's fourth-largest inland body of water, with an area of 68,000 km². It lay between Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan. The Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers flowed into it. At its deepest, the water reached 68 meters. Fish were abundant, and communities around the lake sustained themselves through fishing.

## Diverting Water for Cotton — 1960s

Beginning in the Stalin era, the Soviet Union sought to make Central Asia self-sufficient in cotton. Five-year plans demanded cotton production quotas. In the 1960s, the Soviet government began diverting the Amu Darya and Syr Darya into massive irrigation canals, turning the deserts of Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Turkmenistan into cotton fields. The canals were wide, long, and uncovered. Much of the water evaporated before reaching its destination.

## Timeline of Shrinkage

- **1960**: Large-scale irrigation diversion begins
- **1990**: Area reduced by 40%, water volume by 60%
- **1997**: Shrunk to 10% of original size
- **2000s**: Split into the North Aral Sea and South Aral Sea
- **2007**: Approximately 10% of original area
- The exposed lakebed became the **Aralkum Desert**

## Moynak — Graveyard of Ships

Moynak (Mo'ynoq) in Uzbekistan was a fishing port on the southern shore of the Aral Sea. A vibrant harbor town of 40,000 people. Canneries operated, fishing fleets set out. Now the shoreline is 170 km away. What remains are rusting fishing vessels on desert sand — iron skeletons on sand. This is called the "graveyard of ships."

## Vozrozhdeniya Island — The Island of Rebirth, The Island of Death

Vozrozhdeniya Island sat in the middle of the Aral Sea. Its name means "rebirth." From 1948, the Soviet military built a top-secret biological weapons laboratory on this island. Code name: Aralsk-7.

Pathogens tested: anthrax (Bacillus anthracis), smallpox (Variola major), plague (Yersinia pestis), tularemia (Francisella tularensis), brucellosis (Brucella suis), botulinum toxin, and Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus.

### The 1971 Smallpox Incident

July 30, 1971. A laboratory technician aboard the Aral Sea research vessel Lev Berg was collecting plankton samples on deck, 15 km from the island. That day on the island, 400 grams of weaponized smallpox was being detonated in an open-air test. The technician was infected. After returning to the city of Aralsk, she transmitted the virus to those around her. Ten people were infected and three died — a 23-year-old woman, a 4-month-old infant, and a 9-month-old infant, all from hemorrhagic smallpox. The Soviet Union did not report the incident to the WHO. It was not made public until 2002.

### Anthrax Burial

In 1988, the Soviet Union transported approximately 200 tons of weapons-grade anthrax from a facility in Sverdlovsk (now Yekaterinburg) to Vozrozhdeniya Island. It was mixed with bleach and buried in pits. When a U.S. Department of Defense inspection team took soil samples in the late 1990s, live anthrax spores were detected.

As the sea shrank, the island became a peninsula. In 2001, it connected to the Uzbekistan mainland by land. The sealed pathogens could theoretically migrate to the continent via animals. The United States spent approximately $6 million to demolish 20 buildings and decontaminate 11 anthrax burial sites.

## Health Crisis in Karakalpakstan

Karakalpakstan, the region nearest the Aral Sea (population approximately 1.5 million). Dust storms rise from over 3 million hectares of exposed lakebed. Salt and pesticide residues are carried through the air. By 1993, approximately 75 million tons of dust and salt had been deposited across the surrounding region. Aral salt has been detected as far away as Belarus, more than 1,000 km distant.

- **Tuberculosis**: Over 2,000 deaths per year. Multi-drug-resistant tuberculosis (MDR-TB) accounts for 30% of new cases
- **Anemia**: 80–90% of women and children are anemic — the highest rate in the world, according to the WHO
- **Drinking water contamination**: Salt, strontium, zinc, and manganese from irrigation runoff

## Kok-Aral Dam — Rebirth in the North

In 2005, Kazakhstan completed the Kok-Aral Dam (13 km) with World Bank support. It separated the North Aral Sea from the South Aral Sea, trapping Syr Darya river water in the north.

Results:
- Water level rose 3.3 meters (11 feet) within 7 months — a target scientists had projected would take 3 years
- Salinity: 30 g/L → 8 g/L
- 22 fish species returned
- Annual fish catch approximately 8,000 tons
- Over 20 years, area expanded by 36%, water volume nearly doubled, salinity halved
- Rain clouds returned and the microclimate began to improve

The South Aral Sea continues to shrink.
