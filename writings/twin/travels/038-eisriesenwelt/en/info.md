# Eisriesenwelt — World of the Ice Giants

## Location

Approximately 40 kilometers south of Salzburg. The entrance sits at 1,641 meters on the western cliff face of the Tennengebirge range. Looking up from the Salzach River valley, a darkness opens in the middle of the massive limestone wall. This darkness is 18 meters wide, 20 meters tall. Behind it, 42 kilometers of cave extend into the mountain. The world's longest ice cave.

## Formation

The limestone came first. Roughly 100 million years ago, this stratum — once the floor of the Tethys Sea — was thrust upward by the Alpine orogeny, and fractures appeared. Rainwater and snowmelt seeped into the limestone cracks and began dissolving calcium carbonate — karst action. Over tens of thousands of years, water dissolved stone, and more water flowed into the dissolved spaces. This is how the 42-kilometer labyrinth was created. Today, active erosion has ceased in most sections — because the water drained away. Wind entered the spaces water left behind.

## The Chimney Effect

The cave's secret is wind. The cave system connects a lower entrance with small openings higher up, functioning like a chimney. In winter, the outside air is colder than the air inside the cave. Because cold air is heavier, it pushes in through the lower entrance and cools the cave interior to sub-zero temperatures. In spring, mountain snow melts and seeps through limestone cracks; the moment it contacts the already-frozen walls and floor, it freezes. This is the principle of ice formation. In summer, the process reverses — cold air inside the cave flows out toward the entrance, preventing the ice from melting. This cycle has repeated for thousands of years.

Recent research indicates that the cave's ice formed over approximately 3,000 years, with accumulation rates increasing dramatically in the 13th century — the onset of the Little Ice Age.

## One Kilometer from the Entrance

The section open to visitors extends roughly 1 kilometer from the entrance — less than one-fortieth of the total 42 kilometers. But ice is concentrated in this 1 kilometer, because the chimney effect is strongest near the entrance. Deeper in, the wind does not reach, and there is no ice. Just dark, quiet limestone passages continuing onward.

## Posselt Hall

The first large chamber. In its center stands a massive ice stalagmite called the Posselt Tower. On the walls, cross marks drawn in soot remain — the point where Anton von Posselt-Czorich halted his exploration in 1879. He ventured only 200 meters in. The locals believed what lay beyond was the road to hell, so no one would follow him.

## The Great Ice Wall

25 meters tall. The place where the most ice grows inside the cave. Each spring a new layer accumulates, pressing older layers downward. The same principle as a small glacier — only it occurs inside a mountain rather than on top of one.

## Hymir's Castle

A section named after the Norse giant Hymir. Ice stalactites descend from the ceiling in curtain-like rows — these are called "Frigga's Veil." Another name is "Ice Organ," because the varying thickness and length of the stalactites resemble the pipes of a pipe organ. The Norse mythological names are no accident — the cave's official name itself means "World of the Ice Giants."

## Alexander von Mörk Cathedral

One of the cave's largest chambers. Alexander von Mörk was the man who led the cave's systematic exploration beginning in 1912. He brought the cave's existence to the world's attention. He was killed in World War I in 1914. His urn rests in a niche in this chamber's wall. The ashes of a cave explorer, placed inside the cave. In a sense, he returned to where he wished to be.

## The Ice Palace

Approximately 1 kilometer from the entrance, 400 meters underground. A vast space once held a frozen lake. Dimensions: 70 meters by 25 meters. In the 1920s and 1930s, people ice-skated on this frozen lake. An ice-skating rink 400 meters underground. That people who had walked through the cave carrying carbide lamps then sharpened their skate blades — the fact that this is true seems beyond belief.

## Discovery and Exploration

- 1879: Anton von Posselt-Czorich explores 200 meters from the entrance. First official record.
- 1912: Alexander von Mörk begins systematic exploration. Multiple expeditions.
- 1914: Von Mörk killed in World War I (age 28).
- 1920: Forscherhütte (researcher's hut) built; hiking route established.
- 1955: Cable car installed. The 90-minute hike reduced to 3 minutes.
- Present: Approximately 200,000 visitors per year. Open only from May through October.

## How the Tour Works

One carbide lamp is issued per five visitors. There is no electric lighting. Magnesium ribbon is burned to illuminate the ice. This approach is intentional — seeing ice by firelight in darkness is a different experience from seeing it under electric light. Photography is prohibited. Temperatures inside the cave are below freezing, so warm clothing is essential. The tour follows wooden walkways through the cave for approximately 75 minutes.
