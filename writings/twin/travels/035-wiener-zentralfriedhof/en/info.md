# Vienna Central Cemetery — A City of Three Million

## Location

Simmering, Vienna's 11th district, Austria. Approximately 7 kilometers southeast of the city center. The terminus of tram line 71. Opened on November 1, 1874 — All Saints' Day. Designed by Frankfurt landscape architects Karl Jonas Mylius and Alfred Friedrich Bluntschli.

2.5 million square meters. The second-largest cemetery in Europe after Hamburg's Ohlsdorf. Approximately 3 million people are buried in 330,000 graves. Up to 25 funerals are held per day. Less a cemetery than a city.

## Layout

There are multiple gates. The Old Jewish Section is near Gate 1. Gate 2 is the main entrance, built in 1905 in the Jugendstil style. Past the main gate, arcades line the path, and about 100 meters in, Section 32A appears on the left.

Section 32A. The section of music.

## 32A — Where the Musicians Rest

Beethoven is in grave 29. Originally buried at the Währing Cemetery, he was transferred here in 1888. Schubert is right beside him. In life, Schubert revered Beethoven, and was one of the torchbearers at his funeral. In death, they were placed side by side.

Johann Strauss father and son are here. The Waltz Kings. Brahms is here. Hugo Wolf is here. Gluck is here. Josef Lanner is here. Arnold Schoenberg is here — the man who dismantled tonal music, buried among the masters of tonality.

And Mozart.

Mozart's grave is empty. It is a cenotaph. Mozart was buried in a communal grave at St. Marx Cemetery, and no one knows the exact location. When he died in 1791, Vienna gave him a simple funeral and left no marker on the grave. So the Central Cemetery holds only an empty memorial. The most famous person's grave is the most empty.

## The Church

The Karl Borromaeus Church. Designed by Max Hegele between 1908 and 1910. Considered one of the most beautiful Jugendstil churches in Vienna. It has a large dome. It also serves as the mausoleum of Mayor Karl Lueger — the man who made this cemetery a centerpiece of Viennese culture.

## The Jewish Sections

The Old Israelite Section is near Gate 1. It contains architecturally notable monuments and graves of prominent figures. The writer Arthur Schnitzler is buried here. A New Jewish Section has been in operation since 1928.

## Other Names

Falco is here. The Falco who sang "Rock Me Amadeus." Udo Jürgens is here. Hedy Lamarr is here — Hollywood actress and inventor of frequency-hopping spread spectrum technology. Ludwig Boltzmann is here. His gravestone bears the inscription S = k log W. The entropy formula. Speaking of entropy in a cemetery.

All Austrian presidents since 1945 are here.

## Living Things

Deer live inside the cemetery. Field mice, squirrels, badgers, martens, kestrels. The 2.5 million square meters do not belong to the dead alone. Viennese citizens jog here, cycle here, take walks. There is a café (Kurkonditorei Oberlaa). There are carriage tours — 65 euros for 30 minutes.

Death does not stand on the opposite side of life. It is mixed into the same space.

## Tram 71

In Vienna, "taking the 71" means you have died. This line has been running to the Central Cemetery since 1907. It became an idiom. The Viennese are skilled at turning death into a joke.

"Der Tod muss ein Wiener sein." — Death must be Viennese.

This is a city that held funerals more lavishly than weddings. There is an expression: "schöne Leich." Literally "beautiful corpse," but its actual meaning is "beautiful funeral." During the Habsburg era, the wealthy and the rising middle class were obsessed with the splendor of funerals. There were shops dedicated solely to funeral attire, funeral accessories, funeral hats. It was an industry. Making the final moment beautiful was among the most important things in life.

The Bestattungsmuseum (Funeral Museum) opened inside the cemetery in 2014. It exhibits the traditions of death and memory.
