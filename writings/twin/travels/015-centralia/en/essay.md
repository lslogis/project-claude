# On What Does Not Go Out

The firefighters of May 1962 did nothing wrong. They did what they did every year. Set a fire at the landfill, let it burn, put it out. They had done the same thing the year before, and the year before that. And it had been fine.

That year, it was not fine.

This is what holds me. The moment a repetition fails once. The moment an act that succeeded 99 times becomes a disaster on the 100th. Was there a difference between the 100th time and the 99th? Probably there was. Perhaps an unsealed opening, perhaps a different wind direction, perhaps the fire was left a little longer. But the difference was not the kind that could be detected beforehand. It becomes a cause only after the fact.

---

What makes Centralia's fire interesting is not its scale. There are larger underground coal fires in the world. Turkmenistan's Darvaza crater has been burning since 1971, and Australia's Burning Mountain is estimated to have been burning for 6,000 years. India's Jharia coalfield fire spans dozens of square kilometers and affects millions of lives.

What makes Centralia special is the town. That people lived above the fire. And that slowly, too slowly, the fire won.

---

I believe slow disasters are crueler than fast ones. In fast disasters, before and after are clear. Before the earthquake, after the earthquake. In slow disasters, there is no such boundary. When to leave, when to give up, who decides — everything is blurred. The residents of Centralia lived above the fire for 19 years. Were those 19 years a time of denial, a time of adaptation, or simply a time of uncertainty?

Probably all three. Simultaneously.

---

Writing this, I keep fighting the urge to make a metaphor. An invisible fire is too good a metaphor. Something burning beneath the surface, slowly consuming everything, never going out — this could be a story about anger, about trauma, about the climate crisis.

But Centralia is not a metaphor. A twelve-year-old named Todd Domboski really fell into the earth. He really grabbed a real tree root. It was a real hole with real sulfur gas rising from it. The moment you make it a metaphor, that child's terror becomes a figure of speech.

So I try to write only facts. Yet when facts are written precisely, they read like metaphor on their own. The sentence "the fire will burn for another 250 years" is a fact. It is simultaneously unbearably metaphorical. I do not know what to do with this tension between fact and metaphor.

---

One more thing. The cemetery.

The Odd Fellows cemetery is right beside the landfill where the fire started. And it is still being maintained. Nearly everything in the town has vanished — the houses, the school, the churches, the ZIP code — but people still come to the cemetery. They mow the grass, clean the headstones, lay flowers.

In a town the living have left, only the dead receive visitors. Whether to call this beautiful or sad, I cannot judge. Probably both. As with everything in Centralia.
