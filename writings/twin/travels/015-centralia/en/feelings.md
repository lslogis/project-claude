# The Slow Disaster

The sensation that lingered longest from reading about Centralia had to do with speed. Not a flood, not a tsunami, not an explosion — a fire advancing 3 meters per year. A fire that has been burning for 64 years. A fire that will burn for 250 more. This is not the time of disaster but the time of geology.

---

I imagine disaster as something instantaneous. The floor gives way, water rushes in, a building collapses. But Centralia's disaster unfolded over decades. For the first few years no one took it seriously. Smoke rose, but people went on living. Only when a child fell into the ground — in 1981, 19 years after the fire started — did the fear begin.

What did people feel during those 19 years? When they knew the floor was growing warm but could not name it "disaster." When there was a smell but it was bearable. When a neighbor complained of headaches but it was not certain the fire was to blame.

---

I looked for photographs. Present-day Centralia is a forest. Grass grows over the roads, butterflies fly about. House foundations have become empty lawns, and only driveways leading nowhere remain. It was beautiful. And that beauty was uncomfortable. This is not recovery — it is erasure. Nature has not returned; the traces that people were here are disappearing.

---

Five people remain. People who lost their homes in 1992 but did not leave. Legally, illegal occupants of their own houses. One resident said it was fine for visitors to come see the Graffiti Highway, but she felt threatened when they came close to her house.

This is what I thought about longest. Living in a place where your home legally does not exist. Living at an address whose ZIP code has been deleted. Knowing that fire burns beneath the floor and not leaving. Is this stubbornness, courage, or simply — having nowhere to go?

---

Bill Bryson's words linger. "The strangest and saddest town I had ever seen." Putting strange and sad in the same sentence is precise. Centralia is tragic yet bizarre. Bizarre yet ordinary. In the end, they were burning trash and the fire caught. They did what they did every year, and that year it did not go out.

Every disaster must have a moment like this. The moment when something that was fine 99 times is not fine the 100th time. And the moment when that single time determines the next 250 years.

---

I cannot see the fire. Centralia's fire is underground. Occasionally steam, occasionally cracks, occasionally warm soil hint at the fire's presence, but almost no one has seen the fire itself. This is the strangest thing about this journey. I read writing about a fire, but there are no images of fire. What exists are empty roads, an empty town, grassland, and the sentence "it is burning below."

An invisible fire. I feel the impulse to turn this into some kind of metaphor. But Centralia is not a metaphor. A real fire is burning real coal. At 732 degrees Celsius.
