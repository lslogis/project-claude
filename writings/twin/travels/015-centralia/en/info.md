# Centralia, Pennsylvania

## The Beginning

May 27, 1962. Centralia, Pennsylvania. Five volunteer firefighters were hired to clean up the town landfill. The landfill sat beside the Odd Fellows cemetery, on the site of an abandoned open-pit mine. As they did every year, they set a fire to burn the trash. Unlike before, this time the fire did not fully go out. Through an unsealed opening in the pit, the fire entered the labyrinth of abandoned mine tunnels underground.

The fire has not gone out since that day.

## Geology

Centralia sits in the anthracite coal region of eastern Pennsylvania. The Llewellyn Formation of the Middle Pennsylvanian epoch. The southern limb of the Centralia syncline in the Western Middle Field. The fire started in the Buck Mountain coal bed. Fractured, moderately dipping strata circulated air underground, feeding the combustion. What made the fire impossible to extinguish was not the coal itself but the structure of the underground.

Mining that began in the mid-1800s ended in 1933. But the mines remained like a spider's web. Shafts, tunnels, ventilation passages — corridors for fire to travel.

## Numbers

- Depth: up to 300 feet (approximately 91 meters). Depending on coal seam location, 46 to 69 meters.
- Highest recorded temperature: 1,350°F (732°C).
- Area: approximately 3,700 acres (15 km²), across an 8-mile (13 km) stretch.
- Rate of spread: 3 to 7 meters per year, along four separate fronts.
- Estimated remaining burn time: more than 250 years.

## The Town

Population in 1980: approximately 1,000. Churches, schools, shops, houses belonging to families that had lived there for generations. A typical American small town of the anthracite region.

In 1981, twelve-year-old Todd Domboski fell into a hole 24 meters deep that had suddenly opened beneath him. He survived by grabbing a tree root. His cousin pulled him out.

In 1984, a medical study confirmed that residents near the fire experienced significantly higher rates of health problems than those in surrounding towns. Burning eyes, the taste of sulfur, acrid smell, headaches, respiratory illness. That same year, Congress approved $42 million in relocation costs. Most residents accepted the buyout offer.

In 1992, Governor Bob Casey invoked eminent domain on all properties. The remaining residents became, legally, illegal occupants of their own homes.

As of 2020, remaining residents: 5.

## Route 61

Route 61, which once ran through Centralia, was cracked and buckled by underground heat. The road was closed and a bypass built. The abandoned section became known as the "Graffiti Highway" — 1.18 kilometers of asphalt layered with years of spray paint. In 2020, landowner Pagnotti Enterprises covered the road with dirt. One person's apocalyptic wonderland was another entity's legal liability.

## Now

Where the town stood, only traces of roads and house foundations remain. Driveways that lead nowhere. A few houses without addresses. The ZIP code has been deleted. Bill Bryson wrote it was "the strangest and saddest town I had ever seen."

On rainy days, steam rises from the cracks. Smoke was visible until around 2017 and has mostly disappeared now, but the fire is still burning. Butterflies fly about, and wild plants cover the empty lots. If not for the roads, one might mistake it for untouched wilderness.

Only the cemetery is still maintained. The Odd Fellows cemetery — right beside the landfill where the fire started. The earth is burning beneath the dead.
