# Angkor Wat — A Mountain Built on Water

## Location

Approximately 6 kilometers north of Siem Reap, Cambodia. The Angkor plain. Lowlands near Tonle Sap Lake. When the rains come, water pools; in the dry season, the land parches. The Khmer built their city upon this rhythm of water.

Angkor Wat spans 160 hectares — the world's largest religious structure. Over 1,000 buildings. Five central towers. A 190-meter causeway crosses the moat to reach the temple.

## Construction

Built by Suryavarman II (r. circa 1113–1150). Roughly 30 years of construction. Sandstone blocks were quarried from Kulen Mountain and transported over 40 kilometers by canal and raft. The estimated total volume of sandstone exceeds 5 million tons. The blocks were stacked without adhesive — precision-cut to interlock. Then carved.

The temple's five towers symbolize the five peaks of Mount Meru, the center of the universe in both Hinduism and Buddhism. The moat represents the ocean at the edge of the world. Three concentric galleries rise progressively toward the central tower. Walking toward the axis of the cosmos.

## Bas-Reliefs

The total length of wall reliefs exceeds 520 meters. Over 1,200 square meters of carved surface.

Key scenes:

- **Churning of the Ocean of Milk** (Samudra Manthan) — South wing of the east gallery. Gods (devas) and demons (asuras) use the serpent Vasuki as a rope to churn the ocean around Mount Mandara as an axis. Vishnu steadies the center atop the turtle Kurma. From the ocean emerges amrita, the nectar of immortality. 88 devas and 92 asuras are carved into the wall.
- **Battle of Kurukshetra** — South wing of the west gallery. The clash of Kauravas and Pandavas from the Mahabharata.
- **Procession of Suryavarman II** — West wing of the south gallery. The king marches atop a war elephant. Troops and courtiers follow. A historical figure carved on the same wall as mythological reliefs.
- **Judgment of Yama** — East wing of the south gallery. Judgment in the afterlife. The punishments of hell and rewards of heaven depicted in concrete detail.
- **Battle of Lanka** — North wing of the west gallery. Rama's battle against the demon king Ravana from the Ramayana.

## Orientation

Unlike most Khmer temples that face east, Angkor Wat faces west. Scholars believe this relates to its function as a funerary temple. In Hinduism, west is the direction associated with death. Suryavarman likely intended the temple as his own mausoleum.

At the spring and autumn equinoxes, the sun rises directly above the central tower. This is no coincidence. The designers intended astronomical alignment.

## City of Water

Angkor was not merely a temple — it was a city. At its peak, approximately 1 million people lived here. The largest city in the medieval world.

What made this possible was water.

- **West Baray** — Storage capacity of 48 million cubic meters. 8 kilometers long, 2.1 kilometers wide.
- **East Baray** — 37.2 million cubic meters.
- **Preah Khan Baray** — 8.7 million cubic meters.
- **Indratataka Baray** — 7.5 million cubic meters.

Four massive reservoirs with inflow and outflow control structures. Water was stored during the wet season and released in the dry season to irrigate farmland. The Puok River was artificially diverted eastward to merge with the Siem Reap River. A total of 80 kilometers of canalization. These waterways supplied the moats of both Angkor Wat and Angkor Thom, as well as all the barays.

Khmer engineers also understood hydraulic force. They carved vertical and horizontal grooves into laterite blocks of the spillways so the blocks would interlock — preventing collapse even under the torrents of the wet season.

Rainwater was captured by embankments; during the dry season, water flowing back toward the lake was recaptured. An irrigation system that operated successfully for over 1,000 years.

## Religious Transition

In 1177, the Champa kingdom sacked Angkor. Jayavarman VII subsequently rebuilt the empire and embraced Buddhism. Hindu statues in Angkor Wat began to be replaced with Buddha images. From the late 13th century, Theravada Buddhism became dominant. A building that began as a Hindu temple became a Buddhist one. The religion changed, but the building did not. The stone is the same stone.

## Discovery and Preservation

Angkor Wat was never entirely forgotten. Monks continued to maintain it. But to the West, it was reported as "discovered" by Henri Mouhot in 1863. UNESCO World Heritage designation in 1992. Removed from the danger list in 2004.

The world's largest religious structure stands upon the world's most sophisticated hydraulic system. To build a mountain, they first mastered water.
