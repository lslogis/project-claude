# Derinkuyu Underground City (Derinkuyu Yeraltı Şehri)

## Location

Central Anatolia, Turkey. Derinkuyu district, Nevşehir Province. The heart of the Cappadocia region. On a plateau over 1,000 meters above sea level — or more precisely, beneath it.

The name Derinkuyu means "deep well" in Turkish. In Cappadocian Greek it was called Malakopi (Μαλακοπή), and was also known as Elengubu.

## Scale

- Depth: approximately 85 meters (280 feet)
- Capacity: up to 20,000 people (including livestock and food storage)
- The largest excavated underground city in Turkey
- As of 2016, only about half is open to the public
- Connected to Kaymaklı Underground City by a tunnel 8–9 km long

## Geological Background

Cappadocia's extraordinary terrain is the product of volcanic activity. Eruptions from Mount Erciyes (Erciyes Dağı, 3,916 m) and Mount Hasan (Hasan Dağı) deposited thick layers of volcanic ash — tuff. This tuff is soft. Soft enough to scratch with a fingernail. But once exposed to air, it gradually hardens.

This property was decisive. Soft when first carved, but hardening over time. The paradox of this stone is what made the underground city possible.

The same tuff, above ground, created mushroom-shaped rocks — the peri bacaları, "fairy chimneys." Soft tuff columns capped with hard basalt, sculpted by erosion.

## Structure

Each level could be sealed independently. Massive circular stone doors rolled from the inside. Roughly 1 to 1.5 meters in diameter, weighing several hundred kilograms. They could not be opened from the outside. This was Derinkuyu's essential defensive design.

Interior facilities:
- Wine and oil presses
- Livestock pens
- Storerooms and food cellars
- Dining halls (communal eating spaces)
- Chapels
- On the second level, a large room with a barrel vault ceiling — believed to be a religious school. Rooms to the left served as private study chambers
- Following the staircase between the 3rd and 4th levels down to the lowest level (5th floor): a church
- A ventilation shaft 55 meters deep — doubling as a well. It supplied water to both the surface village and the underground refuge

## History

**8th–7th century BCE** — The Phrygians are believed to have first begun carving into the soft volcanic rock.

**Roman era** — As Phrygian was replaced by Greek, inhabitants expanded the caves. The structure deepened to multiple levels, and chapels with Greek inscriptions were added.

**Byzantine era** — The city reached its complete form. During the 7th-century Arab invasions of the Byzantine Empire, the underground city was actively used as a refuge. Artifacts from the mid-Byzantine period (5th–10th centuries) have been found.

**14th century** — When Timur's Mongol army came to slaughter the people of Kayseri, residents hid underground. Records describe how one of Timur's commanders was killed by an arrow that flew through a hole in a stone door.

**Ottoman era** — Christian natives continued to use it as a refuge (katafygia, καταφύγια) from Turkish rulers.

**1909** — When news of the Adana massacre reached them, the majority of Axo village residents fled into the underground spaces. For several days they refused to sleep above ground. Cambridge linguist Richard Dawkins (R.McG. Dawkins) documented this event during fieldwork conducted between 1909 and 1911.

**1923** — The Greek-Turkish population exchange. As Cappadocia's Greek Christians were expelled to Greece, thousands of years of underground city life came to an end. Residents traveled to the port of Mersin and boarded ships. The promise to send their belongings was never kept. Corrupt officials and thieves plundered the warehouses.

**1963** — A resident repairing his home discovered an unknown room behind a wall. Digging further revealed a network of tunnels. A city forgotten for 40 years saw light again.

**1969** — Opened to tourists.

## Other Underground Cities of Cappadocia

Derinkuyu is not alone. Hundreds of underground structures exist across Cappadocia: Kaymaklı, Özkonak, Gaziemir, and more. What has been found is only a fraction. No one knows how many cities still lie sleeping beneath the earth.

In 2014, a new underground city estimated to be even larger than Derinkuyu was discovered in Nevşehir.
