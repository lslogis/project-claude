# The Library of Ashurbanipal

## The King

Ashurbanipal. The last great king of the Assyrian Empire, reigning from 668 to 627 BCE. He was a strange king. He could read and write.

Kings in that era had no need to read. They had scribes. But Ashurbanipal was trained in the edubba (tablet house) as a scribe. He wrote:

> I, Ashurbanipal, within the palace, understood the wisdom of Nabu (god of learning). All the art of writing of every kind — I made myself the master of them all. I read the beautiful clay tablets of Sumer.

His sister Sherua-eterat also sent letters to illiterate relatives, scolding them for their poor education. A royal family that read.

## Collection

Ashurbanipal dispatched scribes across the empire. A letter to the scribe Shadunu:

> Seek out any rare tablets for which no copies exist and send them to me … rituals, prayers, inscriptions on stone, and anything that is useful for the king.

After the Babylon campaign around 647 BCE, approximately 2,000 tablets and 300 wax writing boards were confiscated from the private libraries of priests and diviners. Babylonian scribes were brought as captives to work in the palace library at Nineveh.

## 30,943 Tablets

The total number of tablets in the Nineveh library collection as recorded by the British Museum. Contents:

- **Divination texts** — The largest category. Systematic attempts to read the future
- **Enuma Elish** — The Babylonian creation epic
- **The Epic of Gilgamesh** — Humanity's first great story
- **Medical texts** — Diseases and treatments
- **Astronomy and astrology** — How to read the sky
- **Botany** — Medicinal herbs and plant classification
- **Mathematics** — Calculation tablets
- **Administrative documents, royal decrees, diplomatic correspondence**
- **The myths of Adapa, the myths of Etana** — Stories about the human condition
- **Religious hymns, rituals, incantations**

## System

Every tablet was copied in standardized cuneiform and layout. Each tablet ended with a colophon (identifying signature):

> Palace of Ashurbanipal, king of the universe, king of Assyria

The accuracy of copying was recorded. If the original was damaged, that was noted too. Apprentice scribes could sign only after verification by a supervisor.

And many tablets bore this inscription:

> For the sake of distant days.

## The Curse

To deter theft, curses were also inscribed on the tablets. A fragment from the tablet of *The Poor Man of Nippur*:

> He who takes away this tablet, may the gods carry him off! May he have neither offspring nor descendant … Do not take the tablet! Do not scatter the library!

## Destruction and Preservation

612 BCE. A coalition of Medes, Babylonians, and Scythians attacked Nineveh. The Assyrian Empire fell. The palace burned. The library burned.

And yet.

Had they written on papyrus, it would have turned to ash. Had they written on parchment, it would have turned to smoke. But the Assyrians wrote on wet clay.

The fire became a kiln. The clay was baked hard as stone. The collapsing roof fell over the fired tablets, forming a protective layer.

The act of destruction — became an act of preservation.

Buried for 2,500 years.

## Discovery

1849 to 1851. British archaeologist Austen Henry Layard began large-scale excavations at Nineveh. In 1853, his colleague Hormuzd Rassam discovered a cache of tablets in Ashurbanipal's palace.

Tens of thousands of fragments were shipped to the British Museum. Most were stored unread.

## George Smith

Born 1840. London. At fourteen he began an apprenticeship as a banknote engraver. Every lunch hour he went to the British Museum and studied cuneiform tablets. He began teaching himself to decipher cuneiform. The museum hired him as an assistant.

One day in 1872. Smith found on a tablet fragment catalogued as K-63 a story of a boat coming to rest on a mountain and a dove being sent out. It was the story of Noah from Genesis. But this was an older version.

According to colleagues, when Smith realized what he was reading — he began taking off his clothes and running around the room.

December 3, 1872, a presentation to the Society of Biblical Archaeology. Prime Minister Gladstone was in the audience. This tablet was the eleventh tablet of the Epic of Gilgamesh — the Flood Tablet. A flood story predating the Bible by centuries.

The *Daily Telegraph* funded an expedition to Nineveh for Smith. To find the missing pieces. In May 1873, on the fifth day of excavation, he found the missing fragment.

Smith died of dysentery near Aleppo during his third expedition. He was 36.

A banknote engraver found humanity's oldest story.
