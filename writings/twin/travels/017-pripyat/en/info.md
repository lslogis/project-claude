# Pripyat (Прип'ять)

## The City

Built in 1970. A planned city for workers of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant. Population 49,400. Average age 26 — it was the youngest city in the Soviet Union. Five schools, a hospital, a Palace of Culture, a swimming pool, and an amusement park that had not yet opened.

## The Accident

April 26, 1986, 1:23:44 AM. An uncontrollable chain reaction during a safety test on Reactor No. 4. Two explosions. Radioactive material released into the atmosphere. Radiation equivalent to 400 times the Hiroshima atomic bomb.

## The Evacuation

At 2:00 PM on April 27 — 36 hours later — the evacuation announcement finally came. "You should be able to return in about three days." 1,255 buses, 360 trucks, two diesel trains. 47,500 people left the city in two hours and thirty minutes. Every ten minutes, 20 buses and 5 trucks departed. They never returned.

## The Amusement Park

Pripyat's amusement park was scheduled to open on May 1, 1986, for Labor Day. Ferris wheel, bumper cars, swing rides. There are testimonies that it was briefly opened on the morning of April 27, just before the evacuation announcement — to calm residents' anxiety. This remains unconfirmed. The Ferris wheel was never officially operated. It is now the most famous symbol of Chernobyl.

## Azure Swimming Pool (Лазурний)

Built in the 1970s. A surprising fact: it continued to operate for 12 years after the accident. Until 1998. Used by liquidators and exclusion zone workers. It was considered "the cleanest place" within the zone. Closed after a sanitation inspection in 1998. Now the empty pool's tiles are peeling away.

## The Red Forest

The 10 km² pine forest closest to the power plant. Immediately after the explosion, the trees absorbed over 60 grays of radiation — 1,200 times the recommended maximum adult exposure, all in an instant. The pines turned reddish-brown and died. The name "Red Forest" was born. Cleanup workers bulldozed the dead trees into trenches, covered them with sand, and planted saplings. The pines did not regenerate until 1994. Now, radiation-resistant deciduous trees grow in their place.

## The Forest That Does Not Decompose

A peculiar phenomenon has been observed in the forests around Chernobyl. The fallen leaves do not decay. In the most contaminated areas, leaf decomposition rates are 40% lower than in normal regions. The leaf litter layer is two to three times thicker. Radiation killed the bacteria, fungi, and soil invertebrates responsible for decomposition. In a normal forest, a fallen tree becomes sawdust in ten years, but in Chernobyl, trees maintain their form even after fifteen to twenty years. Nutrients cannot return to the soil. And decades of accumulated dry leaves become fuel for wildfires — if a fire breaks out, radioactive material can be carried by wind beyond the exclusion zone.

## The Exclusion Zone

A 30-kilometer radius. Area of 2,800 km² — it has become the third-largest nature reserve on the European mainland. An unintended sanctuary.

## Those Who Returned — the Samosely (Самосели)

On June 21, 1986, two months after the evacuation, the first residents returned. Defying military patrols and forced relocation orders. Initially 1,200. A 2007 official survey counted 314. By 2016, approximately 180. Most are women. Most are elderly.

Yevhen, around 80. Worked as a mechanic at the Chernobyl plant for 20 years. Lives in his grandfather's 1915 house. Grows tomatoes, potatoes, and onions, and keeps chickens. "If I hadn't come back right away, I'd have died long ago."

Hanna Zavorotna, 84. Village of Kupovate. Population before the accident: 1,000. Now: 18. Grows potatoes, carrots, beets, cabbage, and garlic. Six chickens. She does not fear radiation.

In 2012, local authorities unofficially permitted elderly Samosely to remain. However, younger residents were ordered to leave. The government provides limited support services.

Their words: "At home, even the walls heal."

## Wildlife

Where people left, animals came.

- **Przewalski's horse**: 36 released between 1998 and 2004. A 2018 survey counted approximately 150. An endangered species that comes and goes from abandoned barns and buildings as if they were home.
- **Wolves**: Seven times the density of normal areas. A researcher noted, "There were more wolf signs here than anywhere I've ever been."
- **Others**: Lynx, brown bear, black stork, European bison, red fox, raccoon dog, wild boar. Camera trap surveys have confirmed stable and sustainable populations.

The paradox: radiation is dangerous, but the absence of humans has been more beneficial still.
