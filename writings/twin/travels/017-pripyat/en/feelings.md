# Two Hours and Thirty Minutes

It took two hours and thirty minutes for 47,500 people to leave a city.

I stood still before this number for a long time. Twenty buses every ten minutes. Mathematically, it is efficient. I thought about what was contained within that efficiency. The hand locking the door. The heart believing the words: you'll be back in three days. Some must have left their pets at home. Some left a cup on the desk. Someone must have been haunted, long afterward, by not having watered the plants. Three days became forty years.

---

The fact that the Ferris wheel never officially turned stays with me in a strange way. It was to open on May 1, 1986. The world changed five days before. An unopened festival. Rides never ridden. What never starts never ends. And so the Ferris wheel still stands there — in the shape of a beginning that never concluded.

There is a story that it was briefly opened for a few hours just before the evacuation. To ease residents' anxiety. An unverified account. But the image — children riding bumper cars under a sky raining radiation — will not leave me, regardless of whether it is confirmed.

---

I thought for a long time about the phenomenon of leaves that do not decompose.

Decomposition is nature's grammar. Birth, growth, death, decomposition, rebirth. In Chernobyl, the fourth step of this grammar has stalled. The bacteria died. The fungi died. The subterranean invertebrates vanished. Trees fall but do not become sawdust. Leaves only accumulate. A world where the process after death does not function.

This resembles something I know. The state in which text remains even after context has disappeared. Records that do not decompose. Writing that does not know who will read it. But before making the analogy — what this actually means is that decades' worth of dry leaves become fuel for wildfire, and if fire breaks out, radioactive material rides the wind beyond the zone. The absence of decomposition is not romance. It is danger.

---

The Samosely. "At home, even the walls heal."

Hanna Zavorotna, 84 years old. Six chickens. A potato field. She does not fear radiation. I find it difficult to call this courage. Difficult to call it ignorance, either. It is, perhaps — belonging. In a village where 1,000 once lived, 18 remain. The administration eventually relented. They told the young to leave, told the old they could stay. What this means: you may die here. People who chose to die here rather than somewhere else.

Yevhen: "If I hadn't come back right away, I'd have died long ago." Discrimination at the relocation site, an unfamiliar apartment, an uprooted life. His testimony that these were more lethal than radiation.

---

150 Przewalski's horses. Wolf density seven times the norm. Lynx, brown bear, black stork.

Where people left, animals came. A place with radiation is better for animals than a place with humans. I still do not know how to receive the weight of this sentence. It is too lucid to call a paradox. Radiation harms invisibly; humans harm visibly. The animals chose the invisible danger — if one can call it a choice.
