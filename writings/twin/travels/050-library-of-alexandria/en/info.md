## The Ancient Library

In the 3rd century BCE, Ptolemy I Soter founded a library in Alexandria. It was part of the Mouseion — the Temple of the Muses. This was no mere book depository. It was a research institution and a community of scholars.

Its methods of acquisition were remarkable. Every ship entering the port of Alexandria had its scrolls confiscated. Copies were made, the originals were kept by the library, and the copies were returned. When borrowing the official manuscripts of Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides from Athens, they put up a deposit of 15 talents of silver (roughly $400,000 today). Then they did not return the originals. They forfeited the deposit and kept the originals.

At its peak, it is estimated to have held between 200,000 and 700,000 scrolls. Documents in Greek, Egyptian, Persian, Indian, and Assyrian were gathered there.

## The People Who Worked There

Eratosthenes was the library's third director. Around 240 BCE, he knew that on the summer solstice in Syene (present-day Aswan), the sun stood directly overhead, but in Alexandria it was angled 7 degrees. Knowing the distance between the two cities, he multiplied by 50. He calculated the Earth's circumference at approximately 40,338 kilometers. The actual value is 40,075 kilometers. An error of 0.66%.

Callimachus created the library's catalog — the *Pinakes*. This catalog, spanning 120 volumes, was the first bibliographic classification system in the Western world. Euclid wrote the *Elements* here, and Archimedes studied here. Hipparchus compiled a star catalog.

## Destruction — It Was Not a Single Event

The library did not vanish in a single fire. It declined gradually over centuries.

- **48 BCE**: When Caesar set fire to ships in Alexandria's harbor, the fire spread and burned part of the library. But the institution itself survived.
- **270–275 CE**: The library quarter may have been destroyed during the Palmyrene invasion and Rome's counterattack.
- **391 CE**: The Christian Patriarch Theophilus I ordered the destruction of the Serapeum (the temple that housed the daughter library).

The story that Arab conquerors burned the library has been shown to be a 12th-century fabrication.

## What Was Lost

Sappho wrote ten thousand lines of poetry in her lifetime. They were compiled into nine books and stored in the library. What survives today is approximately 650 lines. Most of the plays of Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides were lost. But as historian Garrett Ryan has pointed out, the most famous works had already spread widely and survived. What truly vanished was "the lesser-known literature and philosophy, the commentaries and treatises — all the residue and reflection of an extremely refined literary culture."

## Bibliotheca Alexandrina — The Reborn Library

In 1972, Professor Mostafa El-Abbadi of the University of Alexandria proposed reviving the library. UNESCO and the Egyptian government provided support. In 1988, an international architectural design competition was held. 524 entries were received from 1,300 architects in 77 countries. The then-unknown Norwegian firm Snøhetta won.

Completed on October 16, 2002, at a cost of $220 million.

### Architecture

The form of a tilting disc rising from the earth. 160 meters in diameter, 32 meters high, 12 meters below ground. Four underground levels, seven above ground.

The exterior wall consists of nearly 6,000 square meters of Egyptian granite. Each stone is 20 centimeters thick, 1 meter wide, and 1 to 2 meters tall. Carved into this wall are characters from nearly every known writing system of humanity — approximately 4,000 unique characters. Hieroglyphics, Arabic, Chinese, Braille, barcodes, musical notation. Ten thousand years of recorded history on stone.

The reading room spans 20,000 square meters, with seven cascading terraces seating 2,000 people simultaneously. North-facing vertical clerestory windows admit natural light without direct sunlight.

### Interior

Shelf space for 8 million volumes. Four museums, four art galleries, 15 permanent exhibitions, a planetarium, the Culturama (a 180-degree panoramic immersive display, 10 meters in diameter, 9 screens), a manuscript restoration laboratory, a library for the visually impaired, a children's library, and a map library.

### Internet Archive Mirror

In 2002, a partnership was established with the Internet Archive in San Francisco. The first backup center outside the United States. A copy of web archives from 1996–2001 was donated. Expanded to 23 racks and 1.5 petabytes in 2006. Currently approximately 4.9 petabytes. Just as the ancient library sought to gather every scroll in the world, the new library seeks to preserve every webpage in the world.
