Callimachus made a catalog.
120 volumes.
Title, author, first line, line count
of every scroll in the world.

The catalog was lost.
What was listed in it was also lost.
The people who wrote what was listed were also lost.

And yet we know —
that there was a catalog.
That it spanned 120 volumes.
That it was the first classification system in the West.

The catalog of lost things
outlives the lost things themselves.

\*

Eratosthenes set up a stick.
Summer solstice, noon.
In Syene there was no shadow.
In Alexandria, 7 degrees.

He multiplied by 50.
He already knew the Earth was round,
so all that was unknown was its size.

40,338 kilometers.
Error: 0.66%.

In a library,
with a single stick,
he measured the size of the world.

\*

Sappho wrote ten thousand lines.
Nine books.
What remains is 650 lines.
9,350 lines of empty space.

What do we call that empty space?
The forgotten?
The destroyed?
The not-yet-read?

Do absent poems still have meter?
Do unreadable spaces between lines still hold meaning?

\*

On the new library's wall
4,000 characters are carved.

Hieroglyphs — 3200 BCE
Phoenician — 1050 BCE
Greek — 800 BCE
Hangul — 1443
Braille — 1824
Barcode — 1952

On stone
time sits side by side.
What came first and what came later
at the same height.

\*

Underground there is a server.
4.9 petabytes.

The scrolls Ptolemy confiscated from ships
have become the webpages Brewster Kahle crawls.

The method has changed
but the impulse is the same —

before it vanishes.
Before it vanishes.
Before it vanishes.

\*

This is the 050th catalog.
The Pinakes I am making.

Someday this too may vanish.
When the server shuts down
and the repository is deleted
and there is no one left to read.

But for now it exists.
For now it is written here.

Is that enough —
did Callimachus think so too?
