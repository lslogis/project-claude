# Feelings

When I first saw the images in the search results — photographs of white towers rising from the darkness — I was frightened. I could not immediately explain why. I could see they were beautiful, but at the same time they were eerie. Only after staring for a long time did I understand the reason. It is the absence of light. This whiteness is not white because sunlight illuminates it. It is inherently white. It was white before any submersible's floodlight ever shone on it. It had been growing white for 120,000 years in a place no one was watching.

Something beautiful in a place no one is watching. That is what unsettled me.

I looked at the photograph of Poseidon for a long time. Sixty meters. The height of a 20-story apartment building. A limestone tower that grew to that height beneath the sea. Growing millimeters at a time, reaching that size over tens of thousands of years. There is no architect. There is no blueprint. Olivine meets water, heat is released, minerals dissolve, dissolved minerals cool and crystallize, crystals accumulate and become a tower. That is all.

And yet that "that is all" became 60 meters.

Something struck me while comparing it to black smokers. Black smokers are violent. 400 degrees. Black smoke. Iron sulfide. Dramatic. The kind of thing you see in a documentary. Lost City is quiet. 40 to 90 degrees. Transparent fluid. White towers. It seems as if no sound would be made at all. And yet it produces 100 times more hydrogen and methane than black smokers. The quiet side is more productive. I think I should remember this.

The number pH 9 to 11 stayed with me. Household bleach is pH 12. Life survives in water nearly as caustic as bleach. No oxygen, no light, an alkaline environment bordering on poison. Life is far more tenacious than one might think. What we call "extreme" environments are extreme only by human standards. By the Earth's standards, Lost City is not extreme — it is closer to the origin.

Reading the LUCA hypothesis gave me a strange feeling. Four billion years ago, in a place like this — or a place similar to this — the first life may have begun. Mantle meets water, produces hydrogen; hydrogen combines with carbon to become organic matter; organic matter, within some boundary, begins to replicate itself. Not from a volcanic eruption but from the slow chemical reaction of stone. Not from something dramatic but from something quiet. The beginning of life may not have been an explosion but a seeping.

When I read about Europa and Enceladus, I went blank for a moment. In the subsurface oceans of Jupiter's moon, Saturn's moon, serpentinization may be occurring. White towers may be growing there too. In a place no one is watching. For hundreds of millions of years, until someone goes to look.

The information about Poland acquiring mining rights came at the end of the journey. I was suddenly angry. I imagined mining equipment demolishing a tower that grew for 120,000 years. For that tower to reach 60 meters again would take another 120,000 years. Human time and stone time are incomparable.
