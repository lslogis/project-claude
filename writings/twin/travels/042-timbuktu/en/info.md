# Timbuktu — The Center of the World They Called the End of It

## The Place

Northern Mali. The southern edge of the Sahara Desert. At the point where the Niger River bends sharply northward toward the desert, about 20 kilometers inland from the river. 16 degrees 46 minutes north latitude. Approximately 1,000 kilometers northeast of Bamako.

The Sahel zone — the boundary between Sahara and savanna. Annual rainfall around 200 millimeters. During the dry season, the harmattan — a dust wind blowing from the Sahara — blankets the city. Summer temperatures climb to 45 degrees Celsius. Sand-colored mud-brick buildings. The city itself is the color of the desert.

## Why a City There

Because it was an intersection. The meeting point of trans-Saharan trade routes and Niger River waterways. Salt came from the north. Rock salt slabs mined from the Taoudenni mines in the middle of the Sahara. A single camel carried roughly 90 kilograms of salt. Gold came from the south. Gold from the mining regions of West Africa. Where salt met gold, a city emerged.

Around the 12th century, Tuareg nomads began using it as a seasonal settlement. According to legend, a woman named "Buktu" kept a well, and "Tim" means "place of" in the Tuareg language. Timbuktu — the well of Buktu.

## The Golden Age

The 14th century. Mansa Musa. King of the Mali Empire. During his 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca, he passed through Cairo and distributed so much gold that Egypt's gold prices declined for 12 years. He passed through Timbuktu, and on his return brought the architect Abu Ishaq al-Sahili. Al-Sahili built the Djinguereber Mosque. 1327. It still stands today.

The 15th and 16th centuries. The zenith under the Songhai Empire. Three great mosques — Djinguereber, Sankore, and Sidi Yahia — became centers of learning. The Sankore Madrasa is said to have had approximately 25,000 students. With a city population of around 100,000, one in four people was a student.

## Sankore — The University That Was Not a University

Sankore had no centralized administrative system. No president, no departments, no degrees. Each scholar taught students independently. Students moved between scholars, studying each one's area of expertise. Tuition was paid in money, goods, or labor.

Subjects taught — Quranic exegesis, jurisprudence (fiqh), grammar, rhetoric, logic, astronomy, history, medicine, mathematics. The teaching of history was unusual — no other educational institution in the Islamic world at the time included history as a regular subject.

Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti (1556–1627). One of Sankore's most celebrated scholars. Over 60 written works in his lifetime. From jurisprudence to philosophy. He possessed a personal library of 1,600 volumes. After the 1591 Moroccan invasion he was taken to Marrakech, where students flocked to him even there.

## 400,000 Manuscripts

The Timbuktu manuscripts. Between 400,000 and 500,000 items. The intellectual heritage of West Africa produced from the 11th to the 20th century. Most are written in Arabic, but there are also Ajami documents — Fulani, Songhai, Tamasheq, and Bambara languages written in Arabic script.

Contents — Quran copies, jurisprudence, astronomy, medicine, mathematics, poetry, moral philosophy, history. The Tarikh al-Fattash (by Mahmud Kati) and the Tarikh al-Sudan (by Abd al-Sadi) — two chronicles recording the history of the Songhai Empire. These two books are primary sources for the history of medieval West Africa.

## Books Hidden in Homes

These manuscripts were not kept in public libraries but in private family homes. They were passed down through generations for centuries. Bound in leather, stored in chests made of leather and wood. Timbuktu's dry climate aided their preservation.

Many manuscripts bear this inscription — "This book cannot be divided as inheritance or sold." Knowledge treated not as property but as something else. To own yet not make into a possession. To keep yet never trade.

Specialized artisans participated in manuscript production. Tanners processed animal hides into writing material and bindings. There were slate workers, quill makers, reed pen makers, and ink manufacturers. Illuminators painted decorations, and copyists were simultaneously skilled calligraphers. The copyist would leave blank spaces for decoration that the illuminator would later fill.

## Destruction and Rescue

2012. Tuareg separatists and Ansar Dine (an Islamist armed group linked to Al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb) seized northern Mali. Timbuktu occupied. Sharia law imposed. Music banned. Mausoleums destroyed.

Abdel Kader Haidara. Founder of the Mamma Haidara Commemorative Library. In the summer of 2012, he and his team began packing manuscripts into metal chests. They smuggled them out of the city on donkey carts and four-wheel-drive vehicles.

Over 9 months, approximately 350,000 manuscripts from 45 libraries were transported from Timbuktu to Bamako — more than 400 miles. There were two kinds of checkpoints — jihadist checkpoints and Malian government army checkpoints. They had to pass through both. Transport workers were arrested. On the Niger River, a boat carrying books was seized by bandits who demanded ransom.

In January 2013, retreating militants set fire to the Ahmad Baba Institute. Approximately 4,203 manuscripts were lost or looted. But more than 95% were already in Bamako.

## Digitization

Since 2013, the Hill Museum & Manuscript Library (HMML) at Saint John's University in Minnesota has been collaborating with SAVAMA-DCI on a large-scale digitization effort. More than 150,000 items have been photographed.

Writing on leather became pixels. What had been inside homes for centuries was uploaded to servers. Books inscribed with "this book cannot be sold" became infinitely replicable. The content of the manuscripts has been preserved, but the way the manuscripts existed — in a family's home, in a leather chest, the patina of hands passed down through generations — is absent from the digital copies.

## The Paradox of the Name

Timbuktu. In English, this word is a synonym for "the end of the world." "From here to Timbuktu" — the most distant place, a place where there is nothing, an unreachable place. This usage originated from the disappointment of 19th-century European explorers. They arrived expecting a legendary city of gold and found a sand-colored city of mud-brick walls.

But inside those mud-brick walls were 400,000 manuscripts. The treasure was not gold but ink. Europeans came looking for gold and could not see the books. What they called the end of the world was in fact the center of the world — only the criteria for centrality were different.
