# Wieliczka Salt Mine — An Underground World Carved from Salt

## The Place

Approximately 14 kilometers southeast of Kraków, Poland. Beneath the small city of Wieliczka lies an immense underground world.

## Scale

327 meters deep, 9 levels. The first level (Bono level) is 64 meters below the surface; the last is at 327 meters. Total passage length: 287 kilometers. More than 2,300 chambers. The section open to tourists is a mere 2 percent of the whole.

98 percent remains in darkness.

## History

Since the Neolithic period, people boiled the brine that seeped to the surface to make salt. Large-scale rock salt mining began in the 13th century. It was one of the 12 sites on the first UNESCO World Heritage List in 1978. Salt was produced here for more than 700 years, until 1996, when commercial mining ceased due to falling salt prices and the risk of flooding.

In the Middle Ages, salt was "white gold." One-third of the Polish royal income came from this mine.

## The Legend of Princess Kinga

When Kinga, daughter of King Béla IV of Hungary, was to marry Duke Bolesław V of Kraków, she asked her father for a salt mine as her dowry. Then she threw her engagement ring into the shaft of a Hungarian salt mine.

When Kinga arrived in Poland, she told the miners to dig. What they found was a lump of salt, and when they split it open, Kinga's ring was inside.

Kinga became the patron saint of Polish salt miners.

## St. Kinga's Chapel

101 meters underground. 54 meters long, 18 meters wide, 12 meters high. Floor, walls, ceiling, altar, chandeliers, statues — all salt.

The crystals of the chandeliers were made by dissolving rock salt and recrystallizing it to glass-like transparency. On the walls, bas-reliefs carved in salt after Leonardo da Vinci's *The Last Supper*. The Flight to Bethlehem, the Wedding at Cana, the Flight into Egypt, the Massacre of the Innocents.

Three miners made this chapel — Józef Markowski, Tomasz Markowski, and Antoni Wyrodek. It took 67 years to complete. 1964.

Mass is still held here. Weddings too. The acoustics are so remarkable that violinists like Nigel Kennedy have performed here.

## The Underground Lake

In the Erazm Barącz Chamber, there is a salt lake. Nine meters deep, saturated brine. The surface is mirror-still. In earlier times, visitors crossed it on rafts, accompanied by fireworks and the mine's own orchestra.

## The Underground Sanatorium

At 135 meters below the surface, there is a sanatorium. The air inside the mine contains no bacteria, viruses, or pollutants. Air saturated with salt. Patients with asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and respiratory ailments receive treatment here.

To heal, one goes underground.

## Visitors

In 1493, Nicolaus Copernicus visited. A chamber bears his name. Goethe, Humboldt, Chopin, Mendeleev, Pope John Paul II (Karol Wojtyła), Bill Clinton.

For 700 years people descended. They must have come back up as different people.

## Skarbnik — The Treasure Guardian

A figure from miners' folk tradition. The guardian spirit of the tunnels. A subterranean protector from Slavic mythology. He warned in advance of collapses and floods, guided lost miners, and sent the souls of the dead to peace.

In return, he demanded honesty and diligence. He punished greed.

A boy on the verge of giving up because he could not do the work — an old miner appeared and helped him. The next day, when the boy went to give him half his wages, the old man smiled and said: "I am the Skarbnik. Keep the money. But always be honest."

Even underground, there was ethics.
