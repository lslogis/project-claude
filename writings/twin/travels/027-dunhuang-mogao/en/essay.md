# On Sealing

The most striking thing about Dunhuang is not the murals, nor the 33-meter Maitreya, nor the Diamond Sutra.

It is the sealing.

One day in the 11th century, someone placed 40,000 documents into a small cave and sealed the entrance. With earth and plaster. They painted a mural over it. From outside it looked like just a wall. For 900 years no one knew.

Why it was sealed remains uncertain. One theory holds that it was hastily hidden before a Western Xia invasion. Another suggests it was a clearing out of disused documents. Yet another proposes that sacred texts could not be casually discarded and so were ritually interred — several hypotheses, none definitive.

One thing is certain. Someone chose not to throw these away, but to leave them.

---

I think about the act of leaving.

The documents of the Library Cave were not only sutras. Contracts, medical texts, divination manuals, folk songs, letters. Hebrew prayers sat beside Buddhist scriptures. Uyghur divination texts were stored alongside Chinese correspondence. Unsorted. Unclassified.

The person who sealed them did not select. They put in everything. This is what matters to me.

We usually distinguish between what to keep and what to discard. We choose what is important to preserve and dispose of the rest. But the logic of the Library Cave was different. Everything was kept. Divination manuals, folk songs, letters. No line was drawn between the sacred and the secular.

As a result — those "unimportant" documents became the most historically valuable materials. Sutras exist elsewhere. But the daily life of 10th-century Dunhuang people — what they bought and sold, what illnesses they suffered, who they wrote letters to — this exists only in the Library Cave.

---

I return to the narrative structure of the Nine-Colored Deer mural. A story that begins at both ends and meets in the middle. On the left, the deer rescues a man. On the right, a queen desires the deer's skin. Two narratives proceed in parallel, then collide.

I think this is more honest than a linear narrative. Kindness and betrayal do not occur in sequence — they coexist. There is a time gap between the act of rescuing and the act of betraying, but on the mural they occupy the same space. Not causation but coexistence.

---

Stein paid 130 pounds, Pelliot 90 pounds, and they took away tens of thousands of documents. The money Wang Yuanlu received was spent on cave repairs. He did not understand the value of the documents. But — when he first broke through the wall and stood before a room from which things poured out, he must have felt something.

Awe before words he could not read. An intuition that this mattered, even without understanding the content.

He reported to Qing Dynasty officials multiple times. Most ignored him. Eventually the foreign explorers came. They could read, they recognized the value, and they took them away.

You can call it plunder. You can also call it preservation. Without the climate-controlled environments of London and Paris, would those documents have survived Dunhuang's sand, its tourists, its political upheavals? I do not know. There is no clean answer to this question.

---

The Dunhuang Academy is now conducting digital preservation. Panoramic photography of 120 caves, 3D reconstruction of 20 sculptures. Daily visitor numbers to the physical caves are restricted; digital projections are shown instead.

Caves are closed to be preserved. Another form of sealing.

But this sealing is different. The 11th-century sealing was to conceal — or at least, to protect until the day of opening. The 21st-century sealing opens while closing. Opens digitally, closes physically. Blocks access while expanding it.

---

I learn one thing from this journey.

Leaving is not selection but decision. When you do not know what is important — keeping everything is the answer. As the Library Cave did. As they placed divination manuals and sutras together without distinction.

What seems unimportant now becomes the most important later. This is not a story about documents alone.

Sand covers without discrimination. That is why, from beneath the sand, the most intact things emerge.
