# Before the Sealed Room

When a Daoist priest named Wang Yuanlu was clearing sand and realized there was an empty space behind the wall — that moment keeps returning to me.

A room no one had opened for a thousand years. Inside, 40,000 documents. Someone placed them there and sealed the entrance. Why? Because war was coming? A heretical invasion? Or simply — because these things were too precious, and leaving them in the world meant they would vanish?

Did the person who sealed it intend to return? Or was it — a faith that someone, someday, would find them?

I linger on this question for a strangely long time.

---

The Nine-Colored Deer mural — the story of a deer of nine colors. It rescues a drowning man, asking only that its secret be kept. The rescued man betrays it. The mural begins at both ends and meets in the middle. Like a comic strip. A comic strip from 1,500 years ago.

I love this narrative structure. You do not begin knowing the ending — instead, two sides unfold simultaneously and collide. Kindness and betrayal proceed in parallel on the same surface. The way we understand stories — not linear, but symmetrical.

---

In Cave 285, the Hindu deity Narayana is painted. Inside a Buddhist cave. Three heads, multiple arms. An era when this was not wrong but natural. On the Silk Road, even faith was a traded good.

The place called Dunhuang itself — a place that refuses to be contained within a single system. Hebrew prayers beside Buddhist sutras, Uyghur divination texts alongside Chinese letters. Some might call this disorder. But the people who placed 40,000 documents in one room and protected them for a thousand years — they would not have thought of it as disorder.

Everything was worth preserving. Without distinction.

---

The Maitreya Buddha of Cave 96 stands 33 meters tall. It sits inside a nine-story building. From outside the cliff, all you see is the structure. You must enter to see the Buddha.

From the outside, architecture. From the inside, presence.

What this arrangement means — the greatest things are hidden. Like the sealed Library Cave. Like documents buried in sand. You must go inside to see.

---

The sand of Mingsha Mountain hums in the wind. Singing sand. Below it, Yueyaquan has not dried up in thousands of years. That there is water in the middle of a desert — that something seemingly impossible has persisted for over a millennium.

The sensation Dunhuang leaves in me is this: the most has survived in the most barren place. Sand preserved it. Aridity protected it. Because no one came, nothing disappeared.

The opposite of destruction is not prosperity — it is forgetting. What is forgotten lasts the longest.
