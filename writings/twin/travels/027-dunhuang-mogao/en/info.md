# Dunhuang Mogao Caves (敦煌 莫高窟)

## Location

25 km southeast of Dunhuang City, Gansu Province, on the cliffs above the Daquan River (大泉河). At the edge of the Gobi Desert, just before the Taklamakan begins. The point where the two branches of the Silk Road diverge — the last oasis before heading into the Western Regions.

Mingsha Mountain (鳴沙山, the Singing Sand Mountain) rises directly behind. When the wind blows, the sand hums. At its base lies a crescent-shaped spring, Yueyaquan (月牙泉), which has not dried up in thousands of years.

## Beginnings

In 366 CE, a monk named Yuezun (樂僔) was passing through when he saw a vision of a thousand Buddhas shining on the cliff face. He carved the first cave. Over the following millennium — across the Former Qin, Northern Liang, Northern Wei, Sui, Tang, Five Dynasties, Song, Western Xia, and Yuan — caves continued to be carved across 14 dynasties.

Currently preserved caves: 492.
Mural area: approximately 45,000 m² (over six football fields).
Painted clay sculptures: more than 2,000.

## The Caves

**Cave 96** — Inside a nine-story wooden structure visible from outside the cliff sits a Tang Dynasty Maitreya Buddha. 33 meters tall. The symbol of Dunhuang.

**Cave 257 (Northern Wei)** — On the west wall, the Nine-Colored Deer Jataka (九色鹿本生) mural. A deer of nine colors rescues a drowning man, and the rescued man betrays it. Inheriting the scroll composition of Han Dynasty stone reliefs, the narrative begins at both ends and meets in the middle — a sequential comic format. 385 cm wide, 95 cm tall. Mountains and buildings naturally divide the scenes. On the ceiling of this cave, four naked children frolic around a lotus — the only freely floating human figures in all of Dunhuang's murals.

**Cave 285 (Western Wei)** — The Hindu deity Narayana appears with three heads and multiple arms. A Hindu god painted inside a Buddhist cave — the cultural blending of the Silk Road.

**Cave 85 (Late Tang)** — Murals of apsaras playing pipa, flute, and stringed instruments. The Getty Conservation Institute conducted a mural preservation project in this cave.

## The Library Cave (藏經洞, Cave 17)

In 1900, the Daoist priest Wang Yuanlu (王圓籙) accidentally discovered a small sealed cave. It is estimated to have been sealed in the early 11th century. Inside: more than 40,000 documents and artifacts.

**The Diamond Sutra (金剛經)** — Printed in 868 CE, the world's oldest dated printed book. A scene of Shakyamuni preaching from a lotus seat is depicted at the head of the scroll. Currently held by the British Library.

Languages of the documents: Chinese, Tibetan, Sogdian, Khotanese, Uyghur, even Hebrew. Not only sutras — contracts, medical texts, calendars, divination manuals, folk songs, letters. Evidence that the Silk Road was not merely a trade route, but a corridor of ideas.

## Plunder and Dispersal

In 1907, Aurel Stein, a Hungarian-born British explorer, paid Wang Yuanlu 130 pounds and took away 7,000 complete manuscripts and 6,000 fragments. In 1908, French sinologist Paul Pelliot selected approximately 10,000 items for 90 pounds — Pelliot could read Chinese, so he chose the better documents.

Current holdings: the British Library (London), Bibliothèque nationale de France (Paris), Tokyo National Museum, and the Dunhuang Academy.

## Preservation

Hundreds of thousands of tourists visit annually — up to 6,000 per day. Carbon dioxide and humidity corrode the murals. Sandstorms and rainwater also pose threats. The Dunhuang Academy began digital preservation in 1993. Since 2010, panoramic photography of 120 caves and 3D reconstruction of 20 sculptures have been completed.

The physical is preserved while the digital records. Caves close, but images open.
