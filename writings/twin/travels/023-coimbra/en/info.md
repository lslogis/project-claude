# Coimbra

## Location

Central Portugal, in the valley of the River Mondego (Rio Mondego). Approximately 200 km north of Lisbon. Population around 140,000. Once the third-largest city in Portugal; it is smaller now. But this city's influence is not measured by size.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013: "University of Coimbra – Alta and Sofia."

## Birth of the University

March 1, 1290. King Denis I (D. Dinis) of Portugal signed a charter establishing a "hall of learning." On August 9 of the same year, Pope Nicholas IV issued a papal bull confirming it. It began in Lisbon. Then moved to Coimbra in 1308, back to Lisbon, back to Coimbra — shuttling between the two cities. In 1537, John III settled it permanently in Coimbra. Four hundred eighty-nine years since.

It follows Bologna (1088), Oxford (circa 1096), and Salamanca (1218). The second-oldest university on the Iberian Peninsula. The oldest in Portugal.

## Biblioteca Joanina

A Baroque library named after John V (João V). Construction began in 1717 and was completed in 1728. The first books entered in 1750.

**Noble Floor:** Three rooms in succession. Oak bookshelves rise two stories high, gilded in gold leaf against backgrounds of black, red, and green. The wood was painted to resemble marble. The ceilings bear trompe-l'oeil frescoes by the Lisbon painters António Simões Ribeiro and Vicente Nunes — illusionistic paintings that make the ceiling open like the sky. A portrait of John V hangs at the far end of the library.

**Collection:** Over 200,000 volumes. Medical, geographical, philosophical, scientific, and historical documents, along with precious first editions. Sixty thousand volumes are of particular historical significance.

**The Bat Librarians:** For approximately 250 years, a colony of bats has lived in this library. The species are the European free-tailed bat and the soprano pipistrelle. Each night they emerge and eat the beetles and moths that would otherwise gnaw the books. Natural pest control. There are only two libraries in the world where bats protect the collection.

Every evening, the librarians drape Russian leather sheets over the desks — to protect them from bat droppings. Every morning, they remove the sheets and clean. A practice continued since the nineteenth century.

On rainy afternoons, the bats are said to wake early and produce "chirping and clicking" sounds. The librarians say it sounds "almost like singing."

The windows are left open — so the bats can fly out to the palace gardens to drink water.

## The Student Prison (Prisão Académica)

Beneath the Biblioteca Joanina lies a student prison. The only surviving medieval prison in Portugal.

Medieval universities held their own judicial authority. Students were not sent to the same prisons as common criminals. Punishments were mostly disciplinary in nature. From 1541, the prison's existence was petitioned to the king, and in 1591 it was officially recognized in the university statutes. In 1593, it was installed in two rooms beneath the Hall of Great Acts.

In 1773, during the educational reforms of the Marquis of Pombal (Marquês de Pombal), the prison was relocated beneath the Baroque library. In 1834, after the Liberal Revolution, the space was repurposed as a repository for books and illuminated manuscripts confiscated from monasteries. The prison became a library.

The original "segredos" (secrets) — a spiral staircase and a straight staircase with Gothic railings — remain to this day.

## The Black Cape and Coimbra Fado (Capa Negra & Fado de Coimbra)

Coimbra fado is different from Lisbon fado. If Lisbon's fado was born in taverns, Coimbra's was born in the streets. Serenata — serenade. Since the sixteenth century, students have sung in the streets of Coimbra.

**Key differences:**
- Traditionally performed only by men
- Performed only after sunset
- No applause — instead, a gentle cough expresses appreciation
- Performers wear the black cape (capa negra)
- Accompanied by the Portuguese guitar (guitarra portuguesa) and classical guitar
- Poetry is central — famous poets' verses are set to music

Zeca Afonso and Adriano Correia de Oliveira carried political messages within this tradition. Fado became a song of resistance.

## Serenata Monumental and Queima das Fitas

**Serenata Monumental:** A nighttime fado performance held on the steps of Coimbra's Old Cathedral. Thousands gather.

**Queima das Fitas:** "The Burning of the Ribbons." A graduation festival dating from the 1850s. It begins on the first Friday of May each year. One of the largest student festivals in Europe. A ceremony of burning colored ribbons representing each faculty. Parades, sports, balls, and the serenata.

## Repúblicas

In the fourteenth century, Denis I ordered the construction of student housing in the Almedina district. A royal decree of 1309. This is where the "repúblicas" began.

Self-governing communal houses run by students. All decisions are made by unanimous consensus. A horizontal, participatory model. Older students teach younger ones. Fado is learned here too.

During the Estado Novo (Portugal's dictatorship, 1933–1974), the repúblicas became strongholds of resistance and critical thought. In the 1960s, when student movements swept across Europe, Coimbra's repúblicas fought for democracy and freedom.

They are still alive today. Communal living, democracy, intergenerational transmission.
