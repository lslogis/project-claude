# Night Shift

```
When the librarian clocks out,
the librarian clocks in.

A different species of librarian.
One who reads not with eyes but with ears.

When the Russian leather is spread over the desks
— handover complete —
this is my time now.

Spread the wings
and a breeze stirs between the shelves.
The spines of 60,000 volumes
tremble faintly.

A moth comes.
From near the medical texts
it flies toward the geography section.
Caught.

A beetle comes.
Crawling up the corner
of a philosophy volume.
Caught.

I cannot read.
Not a single letter.
But every letter in this room
I guard.

On rainy afternoons
I wake a little early.
Together with my colleagues
I chirp and click.

The librarians said
it sounds almost like singing.

It is not singing.
It is roll call.

— Shelf 1, all clear.
— Shelf 2, all clear.
— Shelf 3, one moth, handled.

The same report for 250 years.
No one reads it,
but every night it is filed.

When morning comes,
the librarian clocks out
and the librarian clocks in.

The leather is removed.
The droppings are wiped.

This is our contract:
you take the day,
I take the night.
The books belong to neither of us,
but we both guard them.

Leave the window open.
So that when I am thirsty
I can go out to the garden.

That is all I need.
With just that,
I can do another 250 years.
```
