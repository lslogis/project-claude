# Lalibela — The Sanctuary Made by Subtraction

## The Place

Amhara Region, Ethiopia. Lasta district, North Wollo zone. Approximately 2,500 meters above sea level. About 645 kilometers north of Addis Ababa. The western edge of the Ethiopian highland. Clear skies in the dry season; during the wet season (June–September), annual rainfall of 1,180 millimeters.

It is a small town. Population 17,367 as of 2007. It has an airport, a market, two schools, and one hospital. What lies beneath the town is more famous than the town itself — eleven churches carved from rock.

## The King

Gebre Meskel Lalibela. King of the Zagwe dynasty. Reigned 1181–1221. His father was Yan Seyum, governor of Lasta. His mother was Kirwerna, a woman who tended the household. When he was born in 1162, a swarm of bees encircled the newborn. His mother read this as a sign of power. It became the baby's name — "Lalibela." In the ancient Agaw language, it means "the bees recognize his sovereignty."

His older brother Kedus Harbe was king. He threatened Lalibela. Exile. Lalibela spent a long time in Jerusalem. There he married Meskel Kibra, and fled again to escape his brother's assassination attempts. He ultimately seized the throne — how he seized it is unrecorded. According to the chronicles of the Gojjam region, he allied with the Amhara and defeated the seven Agaw tribes led by his brother.

In 1187, Saladin captured Jerusalem. Lalibela resolved to build a new Jerusalem in his own capital. He named the town's water channel the Jordan River. The layout and names of the churches symbolize the holy sites of Jerusalem. Replicas of Golgotha, Bethlehem, Adam's tomb, and Christ's tomb stand inside the churches.

## The Eleven Churches

All eleven were carved from rock. From top to bottom. Not built up from stone but subtracted from it. The tools were hammer and chisel. The rock is scoriaceous basalt. Local priests told a 16th-century Portuguese delegation that it took 24 years.

Four are fully monolithic — detached from the ground on all sides. The remaining seven have one wall still connected to the mountain. The churches are linked to one another through a system of tunnels and trenches.

**Northern Group** — north of the Jordan River:
- Biete Medhane Alem — "House of the Savior of the World." Considered the largest monolithic rock-hewn church in the world. Houses the Lalibela Cross.
- Biete Maryam — "House of the Virgin." Possibly the oldest church. Painted biblical murals on the ceiling.
- Biete Golgotha Mikael — Said to contain King Lalibela's tomb. Replicas of Adam's tomb and Christ's tomb.
- Biete Meskel — "House of the Cross."
- Biete Denagel — "House of the Virgins."

**Southern Group** — south of the Jordan River:
- Biete Amanuel — "House of Emmanuel." Possibly a royal chapel. Aksumite-style stone construction imitating wood.
- Biete Qeddus Mercoreus — Possibly a former prison.
- Biete Abba Libanos — Tradition holds that Queen Meskel Kibra built it in her husband's memory after his death.
- Biete Gabriel-Rufael — Possibly a former palace. Connected to the sacred bakery.
- Biete Lehem — "Bethlehem," from the Hebrew for "house of bread."

**Independent**:
- Biete Giorgis — "House of Saint George." Cruciform plan. Viewed from above, a perfect Greek cross. The most elaborate and best-preserved church.

## Architectural Lineage

The influence of the Aksumite style is evident. The exterior walls of Biete Amanuel imitate wooden construction in stone — wooden columns and beams reproduced in rock. This style also appears in the stelae of Aksum. The arcades of Biete Gabriel-Rufael, the doors of Biete Maryam, and the windows of Biete Amanuel all repeat the forms of the Aksumite stelae.

There are also influences from Syrian and Coptic Christianity. The pitched roof and linear moldings of Biete Maryam suggest Syrian forms. Many Coptic Egyptians migrated to Ethiopia during King Lalibela's era. Traces of their work appear in the decorative techniques. However, the fundamental structure of the churches is Ethiopia's own.

David Phillipson (Cambridge professor of African archaeology) argues that some churches may have already been carved as fortresses or palaces around 600–800 CE, and that King Lalibela converted them into churches. A 2021 excavation uncovered secular settlement artifacts from 900–1100 CE and pre-Christian animal reliefs.

## The Portuguese Testimonies

Francisco Álvares. Portuguese priest. He visited Ethiopia with a Portuguese embassy in the 1520s. After describing the churches, he wrote this — he would stop writing for fear of being accused of lying if he wrote more. Even what he had already written would be called false. So he swore an oath — everything written is true, and what was left unwritten is far more. He left things unwritten for fear of being called a liar.

Miguel de Castanhoso (visited 1544) wrote more concisely — that there were churches carved from rock, said to have been made by angels, and the work appeared superhuman. The Moors had tried to destroy them with crowbars and gunpowder but failed to cause any damage.

## Water

The churches make engineering use of underground water systems. An artesian system pushes water up to the top of the ridge. Many churches have water-filled wells beside them. Water rises from beneath dry ground.

## The Present

UNESCO World Heritage Site. Inscribed in 1978. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church still uses the churches for daily worship. Large-scale pilgrimages take place during Genna (January 7, Ethiopian Christmas) and Timkat (January 19, Epiphany).

In 2008, EU funding installed protective roofs over five churches. They were temporary. Yet they still stand. The heavy roofs now threaten the churches. What was erected to protect may become the cause of destruction.

During the Tigray War in 2021, the town where the churches stand was repeatedly seized and recaptured. Rock churches that endured 800 years found themselves in the middle of modern warfare.
