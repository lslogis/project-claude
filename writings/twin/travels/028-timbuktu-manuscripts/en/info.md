# The Manuscripts of Timbuktu

## Location

Central Mali, at the very point where the Niger River meets the Sahara Desert. The edge of the Sahel. 16°N latitude, 3°W longitude. Where the desert and the river delta intersect — the trade routes between West African kingdoms and Saharan Tuareg nomads diverged here.

The name "Timbuktu" itself has become a byword for "the end of the world." In English, "from here to Timbuktu" means the farthest conceivable place. But this city was not the end of the world — it was the world's crossroads.

## The Beginning of the Golden Age: Mansa Musa

Mansa Musa I ruled the Mali Empire from 1312 to 1337. During his 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca, as he passed through Cairo, he is said to have distributed so much gold that he depressed Egypt's gold prices for ten years. On his return he peacefully annexed Timbuktu and transformed the city into a center of learning and culture.

The Djinguereber Mosque he commissioned was built by the Andalusian architect Abu Ishaq al-Saheli, paid 200 kilograms of gold. The Sankore Mosque, originally established by the Tuareg in the 1100s, was also expanded under his patronage.

## Sankore University

The Sankore Mosque, Djinguereber Mosque, and Sidi Yahia Mosque — together these three formed the "University of Timbuktu." The oldest institution of higher learning in sub-Saharan Africa. At its peak, 25,000 students studied here.

Subjects: mathematics, jurisprudence, grammar, history, geography, astronomy, astrology. In the 15th and 16th centuries, 150 to 180 Quranic schools (maktabs) were in operation. Scribes translated the works of Plato, Hippocrates, and Avicenna into Arabic, while also copying the 28-volume Arabic dictionary *al-Muhkam*, written by an 11th-century Andalusian scholar.

But Timbuktu's true singularity was this: books were privately owned. Unlike Cairo or Mecca, where manuscripts were kept in public libraries, each family in Timbuktu owned its manuscripts and passed them down through generations. Books were more than academic assets — they were symbols of wealth, power, and baraka (blessing).

## Contents of the Manuscripts

An estimated total of roughly 400,000 volumes. Subjects range from mathematics, chemistry, astronomy, medicine, history, religion, jurisprudence, and philosophy to poetry — accumulated from the 11th century onward. Some descend from texts the Tuareg brought along with Islamic tradition as early as the 8th century.

These manuscripts were not mere copies. A significant number were original works authored by local scholars. Treatises on conflict resolution, essays, poetry, astronomical observation records, medical prescriptions.

## Decline

In the late 16th century, the Moroccan Saadi dynasty invaded and the center of learning began to be destroyed. In 1632, Timbuktu declared independence from the Saadis, but its academic zenith had already passed. Over the following centuries, manuscripts were forgotten, crumbled, and rotted inside family chests.

## Rediscovery and Crisis

UNESCO began paying attention to these manuscripts in the 1960s. The Ahmed Baba Institute was established to search for lost works. But the vast majority of manuscripts remained in private family hands.

Then came April 2012. Approximately 1,000 fighters from the Islamist militant group Ansar Dine and al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM) occupied Timbuktu. They began systematically destroying everything deemed "haram" (forbidden) according to their strict interpretation of Islam.

## The Rescue Operation: Abdel Kader Haidara

Abdel Kader Haidara, founder and custodian of the Mamma Haidara Library. His family had been guardians of manuscripts for generations. In April 2012, returning from a trip, he found the city seized by 1,000 extremists.

He redirected Ford Foundation scholarship funds to finance the rescue operation. He organized hundreds of volunteers — relatives, archivists, library staff, even tour guides.

Eight months of nighttime operations. Each night, teams packed ancient works of astronomy, poetry, history, and jurisprudence into metal footlockers, then transported them by mule cart and four-wheel-drive vehicle to safe houses across the city. On donkey backs, in handcarts, under car seats, sometimes hidden in canoes on the Niger River. Every load had to pass through checkpoints manned by jihadist militias and Malian soldiers alike.

The final destination was Bamako, to the south. More than 350,000 volumes were safely evacuated.

## What Was Destroyed

In January 2013, al-Qaeda fighters set fire to the Ahmed Baba Institute. Approximately 4,203 manuscripts were lost or stolen. About 1% of the total. A French military intervention followed immediately after.

More than 95% survived. Thanks to one librarian and hundreds of volunteers.

## Present Day

As of 2022, the 300,000 rescued manuscripts remain stored in Bamako. Digitization is underway, but the return to Timbuktu has not yet happened. The city is still not entirely safe.

Timbuktu is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The three mosques still stand. But the voices of the scholars who once taught within them can now be heard only from parchment inside metal boxes.
