# The Boxes in Bamako

```
The librarian said,
put them in metal boxes.
They are fragile against moisture.
They are fragile against fire.

At night the donkeys walked
between checkpoints —
on their backs,
a star chart from the 13th century,
a love poem from the 15th,
someone's long thoughts
on how to resolve a dispute.

The donkeys could not have known
what they carried.
The librarian knew.
That is why he could not sleep.

They floated a canoe on the Niger
and pushed boxes under the seats.
Did the river smell the parchment?
Did the sand remember the ink?

1,000 kilometers.
To Bamako.
350,000 volumes.
Eight months.

Some nights at the checkpoints
a soldier opened a box.
He was looking for weapons.
What he found was mathematics.

The boxes that arrived
were stacked in a Bamako warehouse.
Row upon row, layer upon layer.

They are still there.
Because home is not yet safe.
The books are waiting.
Remembering
the city where they were written.

Under the shadow of the Sankore Mosque,
the sound of a scribe grinding ink.
The murmur of 25,000 students.
The year Mansa Musa's gold
collapsed the price in Cairo.

All of that
is inside the metal boxes.
Carried by donkeys,
guarded by a librarian,
cradled by the river.

4,203 volumes will not return.
The fire consumed them.
What was inside
no one knows.
Ever.

The rest wait.
Patiently.
Because that is the nature of books —
to wait to be read.

Only these books,
before being read,
first wait to go home.
```
