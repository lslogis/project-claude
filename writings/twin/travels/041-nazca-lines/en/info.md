# Nazca Lines — Drawings No One Looks Up At

## Place

Southern Peru. Ica Region. Approximately 450 kilometers southeast of Lima. The Pampa Colorada (Red Plain) of the Nazca Desert. Roughly 500 meters above sea level. A highland desert set inland from the Pacific coast.

One of the driest places on Earth. In some years, annual rainfall is less than 1 millimeter. There is wind, but no rain. Temperature hovers around 25°C — relatively mild, but with large diurnal swings. This extreme dryness has preserved drawings 2,000 years old.

## The Nazca Lines

They span approximately 500 km². The combined total length of all lines exceeds 1,300 kilometers. Over 800 straight lines, more than 300 geometric figures (triangles, trapezoids, spirals), and more than 70 plant and animal forms.

Remove the red pebbles from the surface and a lighter-colored soil is revealed beneath. That is the line. Depth: 10 to 30 centimeters. Without rain, the soil is never covered again. Even when the wind blows, the pebbles do not reaccumulate. The desert protects the drawings.

## The Figures

Hummingbird — 165 feet (approximately 50 meters). Spider — 150 feet (approximately 46 meters). Monkey — 360 feet (approximately 110 meters). Condor-like bird — 443 feet (approximately 135 meters). Pelican — 935 feet (approximately 285 meters). Orca — 210 feet (approximately 64 meters).

The hummingbird's curves flow as if drawn in a single stroke. The spider is precise down to each individual leg. The monkey's tail is a perfect spiral. These figures are nearly impossible to recognize from ground level. From nearby hills, some straight lines are visible, but the full outlines of the animal figures can only be seen from the air.

## Who, When

The Nazca culture. Between approximately 200 BCE and 600 CE. Some earlier lines are believed to have been drawn by the preceding Paracas culture (800 BCE–100 BCE).

Nazca civilization was not a unified state. A loose confederation of chiefdoms sharing a common ceremonial center: Cahuachi. Cahuachi was not a city — it was a ritual space without permanent inhabitants. On the south bank of the Nazca River, 50 kilometers inland, it held 40 adobe mounds and plazas. The tallest, the Great Temple, exceeded 20 meters in height.

## How

Stakes and string. It is believed they first drew small-scale sketches, then enlarged them onto the ground. Stakes were driven in and string pulled taut to create straight lines. Curves were drawn by fixing the string to a central stake and tracing an arc. Traces of wooden stakes have been found at the ends of some lines.

Red pebbles were removed and piled along the edges. Large-scale labor was required, but advanced technology was not. Researchers estimate that several hundred people could have completed a single figure in a matter of days.

## Why

The oldest theory — an astronomical calendar. In 1941, American historian Paul Kosok observed the lines from an aircraft and claimed that some straight lines pointed toward the sunrise at the solstices. Maria Reiche developed this into the hypothesis of a vast astronomical observation device.

However, later research argued that the astronomical alignments were at the level of coincidence.

The water-ritual hypothesis. Evidence was found that the straight lines and trapezoids were used in water-related rituals. Ceremonial processions walking along the lines. Fragments of thorny oyster shell (Spondylus) were found near the lines — an offering symbolizing water and rain in Andean cultures. Rituals praying for water in one of the driest places on Earth.

A 2024 AI study distinguished two types of geoglyphs. Line-type — large, visible from a distance, social function. Relief-type — small, visible up close from paths, personal/ritual function. Of the relief-type, 81.6% had human motifs — people, livestock, severed heads.

## Maria Reiche

Born 1903 in Dresden, Germany. Studied mathematics and physics. Arrived in Peru in 1932. First saw the Nazca Lines with Paul Kosok in 1941.

She spent nearly 60 years in the desert after that. Alone. She went out into the desert with a ladder and a broom and swept the outlines of the lines clean. She personally swept approximately 1,000 lines. She hired guards and waged a preservation campaign with the proceeds from her own book sales. Only her sister Renata Reiche helped financially.

When the Pan-American Highway was built through Nazca, the construction cut across the famous lizard figure. Reiche subsequently pressured the government to designate the area as protected. UNESCO World Heritage inscription in 1994.

She died in 1998. Age 95. Her home in Nazca is now the Maria Reiche Museum. Her name is also the name of Nazca's airport.

## Puquios — Underground Aqueducts

Another massive construction by the Nazca people — an underground water system. Puquios. They dug down 10 to 15 meters to the aquifer beneath the surface to draw water up.

Their distinctive spiral entrances are called ojos (eyes). They serve as ventilation shafts while simultaneously functioning as pumps that push wind into the channels to move the water. Using wind to push water.

Thirty-six puquios remain operational in three valleys of the Nazca region today. Aqueducts built 1,700 years ago, still in use.

The same people who drew pictures visible only from the sky also dug beneath the earth to bring water to the surface.

## 2024 — What AI Saw

A team led by Masato Sakai of Yamagata University, in collaboration with IBM Research, used AI to scan the Nazca Desert. Terabytes of satellite imagery and drone data were processed. In 6 months, 303 new geoglyphs were discovered. Over 100 years, 430 had been found — AI added 303 in 6 months. Twenty times faster.

Newly discovered figures include a 72-foot orca holding a knife, parrots, cats, llamas, and severed heads. Over 1,000 candidate sites remain awaiting verification. An additional 250 geoglyphs are estimated to exist.

Drawings invisible to human eyes for 2,000 years were found by the eyes of machines. What the Nazca people drew for the sky, a satellite in the sky has read.
