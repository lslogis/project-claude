Ando Tadao is a man who taught himself architecture. He did not go to university. He was a boxer who became an architect. He read Le Corbusier's books and traveled Europe, learning architecture as he went.

What he did on Naoshima is unusual. He built a museum, but built it so it could not be seen. Chichu Art Museum is underground. Standing on the hilltop, there is only grass and sky. The museum is below. Concrete walls and natural light. He judged that to be enough.

This is remarkable restraint for an architect. Most architects want their buildings to be seen. They want landmarks. Ando hid his building. So as not to harm the landscape. And that concealment is precisely what made the building extraordinary. An invisible museum. This paradox.

---

I think about the act of emptying.

Chichu Art Museum has no artificial lighting. This is a removal. He removed the lighting system that is fundamental to any modern museum. As a result, natural light entered. Natural light cannot be controlled. On cloudy days and clear days, in morning and evening, the artworks look different. By removing control, Ando inserted time.

At Minamidera, Turrell took away the light. Complete darkness. And he made people wait until their eyes adjusted. By taking away light, he gave the gift of light.

This is the Naoshima method. To fill by removing. To reveal by concealing.

---

But there is something on this island that has not been emptied. Mitsubishi's smeltery. Since 1917, for over a hundred years. The northern half of the island. A plant that smelts copper and extracts gold and silver.

Search for Naoshima on tourist sites and you find Ando Tadao, Kusama Yayoi, and Monet. The smeltery rarely appears. But that is the reality of this island. The beautiful south and the industrial north share the same island. Art and smelting tread the same ground.

I reread Fukutake Soichiro's sentence: "Economy is subordinate to culture." On Naoshima, this sentence is only half realized. In the south, culture leads the economy. In the north, economy still determines the landscape. Two logics coexist on a single island.

I do not think this is a bad thing. I think it is, in fact, honest. The world does not run on a single logic. The natural light of Chichu Art Museum and the chimney smoke of the smeltery exist under the same sky. This is Naoshima.

---

The subject of population cannot be avoided. From 6,007 in 1970 to 3,103 in 2020. Cut in half.

In 2010, the first year of the Setouchi Triennale, 930,000 people came. But "coming" and "living" are different things. Tourists take the ferry over, visit the museums, take photos in front of the pumpkin, and take the ferry back. Their footprints do not remain.

Critics have pointed this out too. That not all islands benefit equally from the Triennale. Naoshima and Teshima became famous, but smaller, more remote islands like Inujima and Megijima continue to lose population and age. Can art save a place? Naoshima has not yet answered this question.

---

Still, I believe what Fukutake did was meaningful. Because he tried.

In 1985, when his father Tetsuhiko met the Naoshima village mayor, this island's future held nothing but the smeltery. When the father died and the son took over, he invited a world-class architect to build a museum. He put art in empty houses. He placed a pumpkin by the sea.

Just as that pumpkin was swept away by a typhoon and came back, this project too is imperfect. The population shrinks, not every island is saved, and the smeltery still runs. But the fact that he tried. That he brought the world into an island of three thousand. That is true.

As Ando Tadao buried his museum in the earth, perhaps good things are happening where they cannot be seen. On this island. On this small island in the Seto Inland Sea. Between concrete and light and the sea wind.
