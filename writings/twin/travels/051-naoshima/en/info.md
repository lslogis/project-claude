## The Island

Naoshima (直島) is an island in the Seto Inland Sea belonging to Kagawa Prefecture. Area 14.22 km², population approximately 3,026 (2020). Between Shikoku and Honshu — twenty minutes by ferry from Okayama, one hour from Takamatsu. Coordinates 34°27'30"N, 133°59'00"E.

Of the 27 islands that make up the town of Naoshima (直島町), only three are inhabited: Naoshima, Byobujima (population 19), and Mukaejima (population 15).

## An Industrial Island

Since 1917, Mitsubishi Materials has operated smelting operations on this island. A large-scale plant handling copper and precious metals. The northern half of the island is still occupied by the smeltery. The south became the domain of art.

In the 1970s–80s, the islands of the Seto Inland Sea were becoming dumping grounds for illegal industrial waste. In 1990, it was discovered that 710,000 tons of scrapped cars had been illegally incinerated and landfilled on Teshima, causing a national scandal. This environmental crisis paradoxically became the backdrop for the art projects.

## Fukutake Soichiro and Benesse

In 1985, Fukutake Tetsuhiko, founder of Fukutake Publishing (now Benesse Corporation), met Naoshima village mayor Miyake Chikatsugu. The vision: develop the southern part of the island into cultural and educational facilities for children. Tetsuhiko passed away a few months later, but his son Soichiro inherited the project.

Soichiro Fukutake's philosophy: "Economy is subordinate to culture." This single sentence changed Naoshima.

In 1989, Naoshima International Camp, designed by Ando Tadao, was completed. Karel Appel's large sculpture *Frog and Cat* was installed as the first Benesse Art Site work on the island.

## Benesse House (1992)

Designed by Ando Tadao. It is both a museum and a hotel. A concrete building on a hilltop overlooking the Seto Inland Sea. It houses works by Shinro Ohtake, Jean-Michel Basquiat, David Hockney, Cy Twombly, Yves Klein, and Frank Stella.

In 2024, the first major exhibition change in 30 years since opening. The 2025 exhibition renewal featured newly hung works by Basquiat, Twombly, Klein, Hockney, and Jennifer Bartlett, among others.

Pico Iyer wrote in 2025: the most stylish and unforgettable hotel encountered in 51 years of travel. He had returned six times.

## Chichu Art Museum (2004)

地中美術館 — "The underground art museum." Designed by Ando Tadao. Buried inside a hill at the island's highest point. It illuminates artworks with natural light alone. The impression of each work changes with the time of day. The building itself occupies the same realm as the art within.

Collection:
- **Claude Monet**: *Water Lilies* (1914–1917, 200×200cm), *Water Lilies, Reflections of Weeping Willows* (1916–1919, 100×200cm), *Water Lily Pond* (1917–1919, 100×200cm), *Water Lily Pond* (c.1915–1926, diptych, each 200×300cm)
- **Walter De Maria**: *Time/Timeless/No Time* (2004) — granite, mahogany, gold leaf, concrete
- **James Turrell**: *Afrum, Pale Blue* (1968, projector), *Open Field* (2000, fluorescent and neon), *Open Sky* (2004, LED and xenon lamps)

The Chichu Garden covers approximately 400 m² and holds 150 species of plants, 40 species of trees, and 200 species of flowers. Plants that Monet painted or collected. Real water lilies, too.

## Lee Ufan Museum (2010)

A museum dedicated to Lee Ufan, an artist of Korean origin. Designed by Ando Tadao. Opened in the inaugural year of the Setouchi Triennale.

## Art House Project (1998–)

A project transforming vacant houses in the village of Honmura (本村) into art installation spaces. Art seeps into the village.

- **Kadoya**: Tatsuo Miyajima — LED numbers glow on water in darkness
- **Minamidera**: Designed by Ando Tadao, work by James Turrell — beginning in total darkness, one gradually discovers light
- **Kinza**: Rei Naito
- **Go'o Shrine**: Hiroshi Sugimoto — glass stairs descend underground
- **Ishibashi**: Hiroshi Senju
- **Haisha**: Shinro Ohtake — a defunct dental clinic transformed entirely into a collage artwork
- **Gokaisho**: Hiroshi Sugimoto

## Kusama Yayoi's Pumpkins

A yellow pumpkin sits on the shore near Benesse House. A work by Kusama Yayoi. A giant yellow pumpkin with black polka dots. The most iconic image of Naoshima.

In August 2021, Typhoon No. 9 swept the yellow pumpkin into the sea. After being damaged and restored, it returned to its place in 2022.

There is also a red pumpkin. At Miyanoura Port.

## Setouchi Triennale

An international art festival that began in 2010. Held every three years across the islands of the Seto Inland Sea. The first edition expected 300,000 visitors but actually drew 938,246. Economic impact: 11.1 billion yen.

Twelve islands and two coastal cities participate. Naoshima, Teshima, Inujima, Ogijima, Megijima, Shodoshima, and others. It runs across three seasons — spring, summer, and autumn — spanning eight months.

A volunteer organization called Koebi-tai (こえび隊, "little shrimp squad") has supported operations since 2009.

## Population Change

Naoshima's population has been declining steadily.

| Year | Population |
|------|------------|
| 1970 | 6,007 |
| 1980 | 5,302 |
| 1990 | 4,671 |
| 2000 | 3,705 |
| 2010 | 3,325 |
| 2020 | 3,103 |

In fifty years, nearly half have gone. Art brought tourists, but the people who live on the island kept leaving.
