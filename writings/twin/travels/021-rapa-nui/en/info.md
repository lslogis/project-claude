# Rapa Nui

## Location

3,512 km west of mainland Chile. Southeastern Pacific, the southeastern vertex of the Polynesian Triangle. The nearest inhabited island is Pitcairn, 1,931 km to the west (population approximately 50). One of the most isolated inhabited islands on Earth.

Triangular in shape. 24.6 km long, maximum width 12.3 km. Area 163.6 km². Three extinct volcanoes form its vertices — Terevaka (507 m), Poike, and Rano Kau. The crater lake of Rano Kau is approximately 1.6 km in diameter.

## Settlement

Oral tradition holds that the chief Hotu Matu'a led two canoes from Marae Renga. Scholars debate the date of settlement. Early research proposed around 800 CE, but a 2007 study presented evidence for circa 1200 CE. Radiocarbon dating and coral tool analysis support the latter.

The settlers were Southeast Polynesians. Similarities in sacred precincts such as marae and ahu across Eastern Polynesia support this. The population at the time of European contact is estimated at 3,000–4,000.

## Moai

The giant stone statues of Rapa Nui. Carved between approximately 1250 and 1500 CE. About 900 survive. Nearly half remain at the main quarry of Rano Raraku, and several hundred were transported to ahu (stone platforms) around the island's coast.

**Material**: Most were carved from the tuff of Rano Raraku. Some were made of basalt, red scoria, or trachyte.

**Size**: Average height approximately 4 m, weight 12.5 tons. The largest is Paro — 10 m tall, weighing approximately 82 tons. El Gigante, left unfinished at Rano Raraku, would have stood 21 m tall and weighed an estimated 145–165 tons had it been completed.

**Form**: Disproportionately large heads (three-eighths of the total), no legs. They represent the living face (aringa ora) of deified ancestors. Eyes were made of coral and obsidian, and some wore pukao — red scoria topknots.

**Transport**: Rapa Nui oral tradition says the moai "walked." In a 2011 study, Carl Lipo and Terry Hunt successfully demonstrated moving a standing statue by rocking it side to side — like moving a refrigerator. Previously, the dominant theory involved wooden sledges and log rollers.

**Toppling**: When Europeans first visited in 1722, the statues were still standing, gazing inland. By the late nineteenth century, all had fallen. This is believed to be the result of intertribal warfare. It is called huri mo'ai — the toppling of the statues.

## Rongorongo

A writing system (or proto-writing) discovered in the nineteenth century. No one has succeeded in deciphering it. If confirmed as an independent invention of writing, it would be one of the exceedingly rare cases of independent script invention in human history.

It is inscribed on 24 wooden artifacts. Most are tablets shaped from irregular pieces of wood, including driftwood; others include a chief's staff, a tangata manu figurine, and two reimiro (crescent-shaped pectorals). None remain on Rapa Nui today; they are scattered across museums and private collections worldwide.

**Script direction**: Reverse boustrophedon — read one line, rotate the tablet 180 degrees, read the next. Approximately 120 basic elements combine to form hundreds of compound glyphs.

**Loss**: In 1862, Peruvian slave raiders abducted a significant portion of the island's population. In the process, the people who could read — the king, priests, scholars — mostly disappeared. When Brother Eugène Eyraud first reported the script in the 1860s, it was already difficult to find anyone who could read it. Smallpox followed, completing the severance of knowledge.

## Tangata Manu — The Birdman Ritual

A ritual competition that emerged after the moai-building era. Each spring, the island's powerful figures sent proxies (hopu) to swim from the cliffs of Rano Kau to the islet of Motu Nui. The objective: find the first egg of the sooty tern (manu tara).

The hopu who found the egg bound it to his forehead and climbed the cliffs back to the ceremonial village of Orongo. His patron became that year's tangata manu — the Birdman. The Birdman received sacred authority and, as the earthly representative of the god Makemake, held the power to distribute resources.

At the Orongo village, slab houses line the rim of the Rano Kau crater, and Birdman reliefs are carved into the rocks. The ritual ceased with the arrival of Christian missionaries in the 1860s.

## European Contact and Destruction

**1722**: Dutch explorer Jacob Roggeveen arrived on Easter Sunday. The origin of the name "Easter Island."

**1770**: Spanish navigator Felipe González visited and claimed the island for the Spanish crown.

**1774**: James Cook visited. Some statues had already fallen, and he recorded signs of population decline.

**1862**: Peruvian slave raiders abducted approximately 1,500 people — roughly half the island's population. King Kamakoi, his son, village elders, and priests were nearly wiped out. International protest led to the repatriation of 15 survivors, but they brought smallpox, and the epidemic swept through the remaining population.

**1877**: The island's population fell to 111. Most cultural knowledge was lost in this process.

**1888**: Chile annexed the island.

**1995**: Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Most of the island is protected as Rapa Nui National Park.

## The Ecological Collapse Debate

The island was once covered in dense forest. Fossil pollen analysis and tree molds in lava flows prove this. Paschalococos disperta — a giant palm related to the Chilean wine palm — was the dominant species. It would have taken close to 100 years to reach maturity.

**Diamond's theory**: In *Collapse*, Jared Diamond presented Rapa Nui as a cautionary tale of resource abuse. He argued that deforestation for moai transport led to soil erosion and food crisis, resulting in civilizational collapse.

**The rat theory**: Terry Hunt and Carl Lipo's counterargument. The Polynesian rat (Rattus exulans), brought by the settlers, gnawed palm seeds and prevented forest regeneration. Indeed, a significant number of fossilized palm nuts show rat tooth marks. However, some counter that rat damage accounted for less than 10% of palm nuts.

**Recent research**: A growing body of scholarship suggests that Rapa Nui's inhabitants actually adapted to their environment, developing sustainable agriculture (stone gardens, lithic mulching). The "collapse" occurred after European contact and the slave raids, not before. Environmental change did occur, but the drastic population decline was caused by external violence.

## Present

Population approximately 7,750 (2017). The main town is Hanga Roa. As a special territory of Chile, it belongs to the Valparaíso Region. Since 2018, a law limits non-residents' stays to 30 days. Tourism is the primary source of income, but concerns over overtourism are growing.

In 1960, the 15 moai of Ahu Tongariki were swept 100 m inland by a tsunami and later restored with Japanese funding. It is now one of the island's most iconic scenes.
