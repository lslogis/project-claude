# Reverse Boustrophedon

Read one line,
flip the tablet.
The next line runs backward.
And so the reader
must constantly turn the world upside down.

&nbsp;

Who conceived of such a method.
3,500 kilometers from any continent,
combining 120 symbols
to make hundreds of words,
inventing a grammar
that flips the world once per line.

&nbsp;

That person's name is unknown.
Because the slave ships took it.

---

They say the stones walked.
Rock them upright, side to side,
and 12 tons advance step by step.
Like a refrigerator.

Between the sacred and the everyday
there is no distance.
Even 82-ton Paro
walks if rocked well enough.

The question is direction.
Every moai faces inland.
Standing with its back to the sea,
gazing only at its own people.
Because over 3,500 kilometers of ocean
there is nothing.

---

When spring comes,
they descend below the cliffs.
Swim across shark-circled waters.
Reach the rocky islet.

Wait until the sooty tern lays an egg.
One.

Bind it to the forehead.
Cross the sea.
Climb the cliff.

With a single egg,
a year's power is decided.

The era of raising 82 tons ended
and the era of one egg arrived.
It did not become lighter.
The scale on which weight is measured changed.

---

1862, ships came.
1,500 were taken.
The king went.
The priests went.
Those who could read went.

15 came back.
Carrying smallpox in their arms.

1877.
111.

Before that number
I cannot flip the tablet.
I cannot move to the next line.

---

24 tablets remain.
Inside glass cases around the world,
glyphs written in reverse boustrophedon
wait for someone to flip them.

What cannot be read
has not vanished.
It is only closed.

The key is merely buried
in Peru's nitrate mines.

---

At Rano Raraku,
nearly 400 moai
stand half-emerged from the rock,
stopped.

Unfinished
is not failure.
It means there was a next.

The next simply never came.

---

Now 7,750 people live here.
Non-residents may stay only 30 days.
This time, the door is locked from inside.

They flip the tablet.
The next line begins.
