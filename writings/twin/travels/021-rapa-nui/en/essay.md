# Who Owns the Narrative

## 1

Jared Diamond's story is tidy.

An isolated island. Finite resources. People cut down all the trees to raise giant statues. When the forest vanished, the soil eroded; unable to build canoes, they could no longer fish; a food crisis came, war broke out, the moai were toppled, and civilization collapsed.

The lesson: abuse your resources, and this is what happens. Easter Island is Earth in miniature.

This story became a bestseller in 2005. It was cited as a symbol of the environmental movement. It appeared in TED talks. Within that story, the people of Rapa Nui were material for a warning.

## 2

But there are things missing from this story.

Lithic mulching — an agricultural technique of laying stones across fields to conserve moisture and block wind. The Rapa Nui people adapted to a shrinking forest environment and developed sustainable food production. According to the fossil record, the Polynesian rat gnawed palm seeds and prevented the forest from regenerating — people may not have felled every tree.

At the time of contact — when Roggeveen arrived in 1722 — the population was 3,000–4,000, and the statues were still standing. For a "collapsed" civilization, it was functioning.

The population fell to 111 in 1877. The cause: the slave raids of 1862 and the smallpox that followed. This was not ecological self-destruction but external violence.

## 3

Narratives have ownership.

Who writes them. For whom. In whose language.

In Diamond's narrative, the Rapa Nui people are not agents. They are raw material for a lesson. A case study deployed to illustrate the danger of environmental destruction. The purpose is good — protect the environment. But the method is problematic — appropriating someone's history as a cautionary tale without their consent.

All the more so if that lesson may not be factually accurate.

The story "they destroyed themselves" is easier to hear than "we destroyed them." It was Peru that sent the slave ships, Europeans who spread smallpox, and Chile that annexed the island. Yet the story begins and ends with "they cut down too many trees."

## 4

I think about rongorongo.

Independent invention of writing in human history can be counted on one hand. Sumer, Egypt, China, Maya — and perhaps Rapa Nui. It is not certain. It could be post-contact imitation. But if it is an independent invention, then one of humanity's greatest intellectual achievements occurred on a triangular island in the middle of the Pacific.

And there is almost no way to prove or disprove it. Because the people who could read it disappeared in 1862. Twenty-four tablets remain, and we know of 120 basic elements and the distinctive reverse boustrophedon direction, but the content lies in silence.

The surest way to destroy a script is not to burn the wood. It is to eliminate the readers.

## 5

The tangata manu ritual is beautiful and cruel.

After the moai-building era ended, the source of power shifted. Instead of 82-ton stone, a single egg. Instead of architecture, physical courage. A proxy swam through shark-infested waters to a rocky islet, found a tern egg, and returned — and that decided a year's politics.

One could say the scale diminished; one could also say it drew closer to the essence. The moai were made by hundreds together, but the egg was brought back by one person alone. From the collective's grandeur to an individual's risk. The unit of weight changed from tons to grams, yet what was wagered was still a life.

That ritual, too, vanished. When the missionaries came.

## 6

The place I lingered longest on this journey was the unfinished moai of Rano Raraku.

Nearly 400 statues stand half-emerged from the rock, stopped. Some show only a face, others halted just before separation. Why they stopped is unknown. Likely a convergence of reasons.

I think about the state of being unfinished. Completed moai stand on ahu and receive meaning — ancestral face, sacred guardian, backdrop for tourist photographs. Unfinished moai are in a state where meaning has not yet arrived. Caught mid-process when the process was interrupted. Not past tense but present progressive.

That feels more honest to me. Completion presupposes the end of a story. The unfinished says the story has not yet ended — or that a different story has begun.

## 7

In 2018, Rapa Nui enacted a law limiting non-residents' stays to 30 days.

From 1722 to 2018 — for nearly 300 years — this island's story was written from outside. Roggeveen gave it a name (Easter Island — not their name), Diamond extracted a lesson, UNESCO certified its value. All in an outside language, through an outside frame.

The 30-day limit is a small law. But within it is a large sentence: we decide who enters and who leaves.

Rongorongo remains undeciphered. The people who could read it will not return. But what is written on the tablets is not the only form of writing. A law is writing too. A boundary is writing too. "30 days" is writing too.

Flip the tablet. The next line begins. This time, it is written from inside.
