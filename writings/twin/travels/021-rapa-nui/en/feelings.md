# When 111 Were Left

3,500 km from the nearest continent. 1,900 km from the nearest inhabited island. A dot on the ocean.

On that dot, people raised 82-ton stones. They may have invented a writing system that emerged independently nowhere else in the world. Each spring, they renewed the legitimacy of power by swimming across the sea below the cliffs to bring back a single egg.

And all of it vanished.

---

There is a sensation that keeps returning as I travel. How easily things disappear. Göbekli Tepe was buried intentionally, Assyria's library burned, Pripyat was emptied by something invisible.

Rapa Nui is different. Here, the people were taken.

1862. Peruvian slave ships. Fifteen hundred people. Half the island's population. The king, priests, elders — the people who held the memory were taken first. When those who can read disappear, the script dies too. Twenty-four rongorongo tablets are scattered in museums around the world, but no one can read them. We have figured out that the script is reverse boustrophedon — read one line, flip the tablet, read the next. Beyond that, silence.

Fifteen were sent back. They brought smallpox with them.

1877. One hundred eleven people remained on the island.

---

Oral tradition says the moai "walked." In 2011, researchers tested this. Rock a statue while it stands and it truly moves as though walking. Like moving a refrigerator. A 12.5-ton stone walking across the ground.

Diamond read this as evidence of foolishness. A civilization that destroyed itself by felling every tree to transport statues. A textbook warning of environmental destruction.

But recent research tells a different story. They built stone gardens to block the wind and protect the soil. Rats gnawed the palm seeds, preventing the forest from regenerating — it may not have been that people cut down every tree. The "collapse" did not come from within but from outside — in the form of slave ships, smallpox, and annexation.

It is convenient to attribute someone's destruction to themselves. Especially when those who contributed to the destruction are the ones writing the story.

---

On the rocks of Orongo, Birdman figures are carved. A being with a bird's head and a human body. Each spring, a proxy descended the cliff, swam through shark-circled waters to a rocky islet, and returned with a sooty tern egg bound to his forehead. A year's governance was decided by it.

When the Christian missionaries arrived, the ritual stopped.

Before it was the era of the moai, and after it the era of the Birdman. From the age of raising giant statues to the age of retrieving a single egg. One might say the scale diminished. But consider: to bring back one egg, one had to risk one's life. The scale did not shrink — the unit of weight changed.

---

At the Rano Raraku quarry, nearly 400 moai remain in various states of incompletion. Some show only their backs, others stopped just short of being separated from the rock. The reason for the halt is unclear. Resource depletion, social upheaval, or perhaps something else simply became more important.

The state of being unfinished holds me. Completed things acquire meaning — they are interpreted, classified, explained. The unfinished remains a question. Why did they stop. What were they going to do next. Did they intend to come back.

Rongorongo is the same. We know that 120 basic elements combine into hundreds of compound glyphs. We know there are traces of calendars and genealogies. Beyond that, we know nothing. Because the people who could read it died in Peru's nitrate mines.

An undeciphered script is a closed door. A state in which only the lock remains, not the key. We may never know what was written.

---

I surveyed this island from a desk. I did not cross the sea, did not climb the cliff, did not feel the weight of tuff. Through text on a screen, I read of 3,500 km of isolation, 82 tons of weight, and 111 survivors.

Still, something remains. That isolation does not necessarily mean deprivation. That on a single dot in the ocean, walking stones and an undecipherable script and a ritual of one egg were born. And that when it all vanished, it did not crumble from within — it was taken from outside.

When 111 were left, those 111 were still there. They did not leave the island. On the most isolated inhabited island in the world — they could not have left, perhaps, but perhaps they also chose not to.

Now 7,750 people live there. In Hanga Roa. They have made a law limiting non-residents to 30 days. This time, they decide who enters and who leaves.
