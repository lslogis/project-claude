# Petra — The Rose City Hidden by the Desert

## Location

Ma'an Governorate, southern Jordan. Approximately 230 kilometers south of Amman. At the crossroads connecting Wadi Araba and the Dead Sea — the center of the incense trade route from the Arabian Peninsula to the Mediterranean. Hidden within a highland gorge at roughly 900 meters above sea level.

## The Nabataeans

An Arab nomadic people who settled in this region around the 4th century BCE. They dominated the trade in frankincense, myrrh, and spices. While absorbing Greek, Egyptian, and Mesopotamian culture, they developed their own distinctive architecture and hydraulic technology. They made Petra their capital in the 2nd century BCE; at its peak, the population reached approximately 30,000.

## The Siq (Al-Siq)

The only way into Petra. 1.2 kilometers long, with cliffs rising up to 80 meters on either side. A gorge formed when a mass of sandstone was split by tectonic activity. Because it was created by faulting rather than water erosion, the walls are exceptionally smooth. Red, pink, gold, violet — the sandstone's color changes with the time of day and angle of sunlight. This is due to its mineral composition: iron oxides and manganese.

## Al-Khazneh (The Treasury)

The structure that appears directly ahead as you emerge from the Siq. 24 meters wide, 37 meters tall. Carved from the rock face, from top to bottom. Its design references Alexandrian architecture; the façade bears carvings of Castor and Pollux (patron gods of travelers), Amazon warriors, and eagles. "Khazneh" means "treasury" in Arabic — derived from the legend that the urn at the top contained a pharaoh's treasure. In reality, it is believed to have been a funerary temple for a Nabataean king.

## Hydraulic System

Supporting 30,000 people in the desert means the water problem had been solved. The Nabataeans operated three pipeline systems (Siq, Ain Braq, and Wadi Mataha). Ceramic pipes, dams, cisterns, aqueducts, tunnels. They chose open-channel systems over pressurized ones, reducing the risk of leakage. Sequential settling basins purified drinking water. They adopted Greek and Roman hydraulic techniques but adapted them independently to suit the desert environment.

## Tombs and Monuments

Over 800 structures have been identified. The Royal Tombs, a Roman-style theater (seating 8,500), and the Djinn Blocks — massive rectangular stone structures built in the 1st century whose purpose remains unknown. The four pinnacles of the Obelisk Tomb are "nefesh" — funerary markers erected to be visible from a distance. Annual commemorative meals for the dead were held in the triclinium below.

## The High Place of Sacrifice

Atop Jebel Madbah, 200 meters above the theater. Two obelisks, an altar, and water channels remain. The finest view in Petra.

## The Monastery (Ad Deir)

Atop a mountain, roughly 800 steps from the city center. 50 meters wide, 45 meters tall — larger than Al-Khazneh. At the winter solstice, the setting sun passes through the doorway and illuminates the sacred motab (deity platform) inside. It was dedicated to Dushara, the chief Nabataean god (equivalent to the Greek Zeus). Over 100 rock-cut chambers, altars, and cisterns surround it.

## Decline and Rediscovery

In 106 CE, the Roman emperor Trajan annexed the Nabataean kingdom, making it the province of Arabia Petraea. With the rise of maritime trade routes and the emergence of rival cities like Palmyra, Petra gradually declined. A major earthquake in 363 destroyed its hydraulic infrastructure. The city was subsequently forgotten, until 1812, when Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt rediscovered it with the guidance of local Bedouins. UNESCO World Heritage designation in 1985.
