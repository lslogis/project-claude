# Aokigahara (青木ヶ原)

## The Name

Aokigahara. In kanji, 青木ヶ原. It means "plain of green trees." In Japanese it is also called Jukai (樹海) — Sea of Trees. Yamanashi Prefecture, Honshu Island, northwestern foot of Mount Fuji.

## Birth

864 CE. The Jogan eruption. One of Mount Fuji's last major eruptions.

Lava flowed to the northwest. Basaltic lava covered approximately 30 square kilometers of land. Records say a lake that had been there was split in two — what are now Lake Saiko (西湖) and Lake Shojiko (精進湖). The lava cooled. It became porous black rock.

And then 1,160 years. A forest grew on top of it.

## Physical Characteristics

Area of approximately 35 square kilometers (14 square miles). Temperate broadleaf and mixed forest. The floor is mostly volcanic rock. The porous lava absorbs sound. That is why the forest is quiet. Wind barely enters. Only your own footsteps and your own breathing remain.

The story that compasses malfunction here is famous. It is true — partially. Iron content in the lava creates natural magnetic fields. If you place a compass directly on the lava floor, the needle follows the rock's magnetism rather than north. But hold it at normal height and it works fine. Since 1956, the Japan Ground Self-Defense Force has conducted ranger course navigation training here.

## Caves

Narusawa Ice Cave (鳴沢氷穴). A lava tube. 156 meters long, 1.5 to 11 meters wide, 1 to 3.6 meters high. Average interior temperature about 3 degrees Celsius. Ice forms in winter. There are sections that never melt year-round.

In the Edo period, ice cut from here was sent to the shogun in Edo. Until the early 1900s, before electric refrigerators, ice from this cave served as a means of refrigeration.

Fugaku Wind Cave (富岳風穴). Saiko Bat Cave (西湖蝙蝠穴). All three caves were designated Japanese Natural Monuments in 1929.

## Ecology

Abundant. Hinoki cypress, cedar, red pine, five-needle pine, hemlock. Four or five species of maple alone. Ericaceous shrubs. Moss and liverwort. Ferns.

Mammals: Asian black bear, Honshu deer, red fox, masked palm civet, wild boar, badger, dormouse, flying squirrel, raccoon dog, Japanese macaque, marten, weasel.

Birds: great tit, great spotted woodpecker, jay, white-eye, thrush, cuckoo.

Insects: silver-washed fritillary, hairstreak butterfly, and many other Lepidoptera.

All of this grows and lives on thin soil over lava.

## One More Thing

The forest is so dense that deep inside, there are places where you cannot see the sky. Tree roots wrap around lava and rise upward — because they grew on rock, not soil. Unable to dig down, the roots crawl along the rock surface. As if clinging to the stone.

The destruction of 1,160 years ago created the ecosystem of today. Without the lava, this forest would not exist.
