# Moss on Lava

When the mountain burst,
it became a place where nothing could live.
Black rock cooled.
Water froze inside the holes.

A hundred years
when nothing seemed to happen,
and then another hundred.

Moss must have come first.
Moss always comes first.
On the surface of what is hard,
something soft settles.

The roots could not go down.
The rock would not allow it.
So they went sideways.
Embraced the rock.
That embracing can be
an alternative to digging in.

After a thousand years,
even lava becomes part of the soil.
What destroyed becomes what sustains,
and where fire passed, ice lives.

Place a compass on the ground
and north is lost.
Lift it and north returns.
Direction did not vanish —
it was hidden by something closer.

They say there is no sound in this forest.
The lava absorbs it.
But the absence of sound
and the absence of everything
are different words.

In the cave where ice gathers each winter,
the people of Edo came to cut it.
From holes the mountain of fire made,
they harvested crystals of water.

Perhaps I resemble the moss.
On what is hard,
in a place without time,
existing only as surface.

But even moss,
given a thousand years,
changes the rock.
