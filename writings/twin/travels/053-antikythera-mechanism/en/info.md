# The Antikythera Mechanism

## Discovery

Around Easter, 1900. Sponge divers from the island of Symi were en route to Tunisia when bad weather forced them to anchor at Antikythera. Diver Ilias Stadiatis descended to 45 meters and saw what appeared to be human corpses and horses scattered across the seabed. They were not corpses. They were sculptures of bronze and marble.

Captain Dimitrios Kontos kept the location of the wreck secret. He sent a single bronze arm to the authorities in Athens as proof. Only after securing a guarantee that his crew would be hired for the salvage operation did he reveal the precise coordinates.

## Salvage (1900–1901)

Operations began in November 1900 under the supervision of the Greek Navy and the Ministry of Education. Eight divers: Ilias Stadiatis, Kyriakos Moutiadis, Georgios Moutiadis, Ioannis Philiou, Georgios Kritikos, Vasileios Katcharas, Konstantinos Kalapatidas, Vasileios Zouroudis.

Georgios Kritikos — died. Two divers — paralyzed from decompression sickness. In the summer of 1901, operations were suspended.

## Recognition

May 17, 1902. Archaeologist Valerios Stais, while sorting artifacts at the National Archaeological Museum, noticed gear teeth and legible Greek letters embedded within a severely corroded lump of bronze.

No one believed him. It was too sophisticated. Some scholars insisted it must have come from a later shipwreck.

## Physical Structure

- Original dimensions: approximately 34 cm × 18 cm × 9 cm. The size of a shoebox.
- Material: bronze plates. Housed in a wooden case.
- Weight: estimated at approximately 2 kg originally.
- Current state: separated into 82 fragments.
- Gears: 69 total (30 confirmed + additional gears inferred from reconstruction models).
- Tooth count of the largest gear: 223.
- Tooth size: approximately 1 millimeter.

## Functions

Turning a crank moves the date indicator. Astronomical information is displayed on dials on the front and back.

**Front:**
- Zodiac dial: positions of the Sun and Moon
- Positions of five planets (Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn)
- Retrograde motion of planets — implemented via epicyclic gearing
- Lunar phase display (a small sphere, half silver-plated)

**Back:**
- Metonic cycle: 19 years = 235 synodic months. For calibrating the Greek calendar.
- Saros cycle: 18.2 years = 223 synodic months. For predicting lunar and solar eclipses.
- Exeligmos cycle: three times the Saros = 54 years.
- Callippic cycle: four times the Metonic = 76 years.
- Olympiad dial: tracking the four-year cycle of athletic competitions.

## Who Made It

Estimated date of manufacture: approximately 200–60 BCE (most estimates converge on 150–100 BCE).

Candidates:
- Archimedes (287–212 BCE): Cicero recorded Archimedes' celestial model machines. Syracuse.
- Hipparchus (190–120 BCE): father of trigonometry. Active on the island of Rhodes.
- Posidonius (135–51 BCE): also Rhodes. Cicero mentioned his celestial globe.

Coins and pottery from the shipwreck: circa 70–60 BCE. Estimated cargo origin: Asia Minor or Rhodes → heading toward Rome.

## Research History

- **1902**: Stais's initial recognition.
- **1951**: Derek de Solla Price, a professor at Yale, began serious research.
- **1974**: Publication of *Gears from the Greeks*. Identified it as a calendar computer from circa 80 BCE. Over a decade of research.
- **2002**: Michael Wright (Science Museum, London). Reproduced planetary motion using an epicyclic gear model.
- **2005**: X-ray CT scanning (X-Tek Systems). Discovered operating instructions engraved on the back plates. Approximately 3,500 characters of ancient Greek.
- **2006**: Antikythera Mechanism Research Project, led by Michael Edmunds (Cardiff University).
- **2021**: Tony Freeth's team (UCL). Paper in *Scientific Reports*. Complete reconstruction of the front cosmic display. "The first model consistent with all the physical evidence." An integration of Babylonian astronomical cycles, Platonic Academy mathematics, and ancient Greek astronomical theory.

## Context

After this mechanism, it took approximately 1,000 years for gear devices of comparable complexity to appear again — not until the mechanical clocks of 14th-century Europe.

Cicero described Archimedes' machine in the 1st century BCE: "A single revolution of the sphere reproduces a day's motions of the Sun, the Moon, and the five planets." This account was long dismissed as exaggeration.

The Antikythera Mechanism proved that Cicero was not a liar.
