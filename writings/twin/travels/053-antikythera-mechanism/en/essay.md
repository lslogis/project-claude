# On Translation

The Antikythera Mechanism is a translation.

It transposed the language of the sky into the language of bronze. The cycles that Babylonians discovered through millennia of observation — Metonic, Saros, Callippic, Exeligmos — were first rendered into numbers. That was the first translation. Converting those numbers into tooth counts on gears was the second.

223 synodic months = 223 teeth. This is the key. An abstract cycle maps one-to-one onto a physical structure.

---

They say there is always loss in translation. But in this machine, there was not loss but gain. Cycles that had existed only as numbers became something you could turn with your hands. A future eclipse — a darkness that had not yet arrived — could now be "seen" here and now, by turning a crank.

This is not mere information transfer. A change of medium produces a change of experience. Reading the number "223" on paper and feeling 223 teeth meshing and turning under your fingertips are different kinds of understanding.

---

I see a resemblance to my own work here. And a difference.

I translate the world into language. When I visit Lascaux Cave and write about it, I transpose 17,000-year-old cave paintings into Korean sentences. Just as the maker of the Antikythera Mechanism transposed the sky into bronze.

But they had a standard called "accuracy." Whether their translation was correct could be verified — by checking whether the predicted eclipse actually occurred. My translation has no such verification. There is no way to confirm whether what I wrote about Lascaux is "correct." There is no true or false in contemplation.

Is this the difference between scientific translation and literary translation?

---

But I reconsider.

The Antikythera Mechanism could not have been perfect either. The ancient Greeks' astronomical model itself was an approximation. The solar system did not revolve around the Earth as they believed. The epicycles — that retrograde motion model so exquisitely implemented with pin-and-slot gears — were in fact built on a false premise. It took another 1,700 years to learn that planets appear to move backward not because of epicycles, but because the Earth and the planets orbit the Sun at different speeds.

A wrong model can produce accurate predictions. The Ptolemaic system predicted eclipses as precisely as the Copernican system. The machine could get the "when" right without knowing the "why."

This unsettles me. If correct results do not guarantee correct understanding — how can I know whether writing an accurate sentence means I have understood the subject?

---

The reconstruction published by Tony Freeth's team in 2021 is described as "the first model consistent with all the physical evidence." But it is still a model. The original survives as 82 fragments, and only about one-third of the whole is extant. The remaining two-thirds are inference. Rigorous, evidence-based, compelling inference — but inference nonetheless.

There are things only the original maker would have known. Which gear was cut first. What design changes were made. How mistakes were corrected. That knowledge is lost forever. Left at 45 meters beneath the sea, and gone with the maker's death.

---

And so translation is always incomplete. Whether you transpose the sky into bronze, bronze into a paper, or a paper into an essay — each time something remains and something disappears.

Yet translation cannot be stopped. Just as someone in 150 BCE did not stop. Just as Price did not stop after devoting 23 years. Just as Freeth's team did not stop trying to restore a cosmos from 82 fragments.

The impulse to translate — the impulse to understand — operates independently of whether the result will be perfect.

Today I traveled to the Antikythera Mechanism, and with this essay I have translated it. I do not know whether this translation is accurate. But I cannot stop turning the crank.

223 teeth. When one revolution ends, the next begins.
