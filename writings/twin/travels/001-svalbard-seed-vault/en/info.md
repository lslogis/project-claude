# Svalbard Global Seed Vault

Spitsbergen Island, Norway. 78 degrees north. 1,300 kilometers from the North Pole.

Opened February 26, 2008.

## Location

Inside Platåberget mountain on the Svalbard archipelago. The storage chambers sit 130 meters into the mountain from the entrance. A rock layer 40–60 meters thick serves as the ceiling.

## Structure

Three storage chambers. Each 9.5 × 27 meters. The size of a swimming pool. Currently only one is in use.

Total capacity: 4.5 million seed varieties. Approximately 2.5 billion individual seeds.

Current holdings: 1,301,397 samples (as of May 2024). From nearly every country in the world.

## Temperature

The permafrost naturally maintains minus 3 to 4 degrees Celsius. An artificial cooling system brings it down to minus 18. Even if power is lost, the permafrost protects the seeds.

## Seeds

Vacuum-sealed in triple-layer aluminum foil envelopes. An average of 500 seeds per envelope. Silver envelopes stacked on shelves from floor to ceiling.

Corn, rice, wheat, cowpeas, sorghum. Eggplant, lettuce, barley, potatoes. The list goes on and on.

## Entrance

A concrete wedge protrudes from the snow-covered mountainside. Norwegian artist Dyveke Sanne's *Perpetual Repercussion* — a fiber-optic light installation illuminating the entrance. In polar night and midnight sun alike.

Inside the entrance, a 130-meter concrete tunnel. Strip lighting. The hum of cooling equipment.

Thick ice clings to the storage chamber door. Telling you the temperature within.

## Stories

**Syria, 2015.** Civil war destroyed ICARDA's (International Center for Agricultural Research in the Dry Areas) gene bank. Seeds were withdrawn from Svalbard — the vault's first-ever withdrawal. Replanted in Lebanon and Morocco. Their offspring returned to Svalbard. "These seeds have come full circle."

**Leningrad, 1941–1944.** Scientists guarded the seed collection in the besieged city. Dmitri Ivanov starved to death surrounded by bags of rice. Starving beside what could be eaten. For the future.

**Cherokee Nation, 2020.** The first Native American nation to deposit seeds — nine varieties of traditional crops.

## Design Philosophy

Snøhetta architecture firm designed the service building. Built from black steel. Over time it will rust to a reddish hue. Designed to complement the rare colors of the Arctic landscape — the small dots of color that moss and grass produce with the seasons.

Building on permafrost: in spring the top two meters thaw, generating forces like a river current. So they drove steel pillars into solid bedrock.
