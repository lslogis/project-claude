# The Aral Sea — A Sea Erased from the Map

## The Place

It straddled the border of Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan. Central Asia. The heart of the Turan Depression. As of 1960, its area was approximately 68,000 square kilometers. 270 miles north to south, 180 miles east to west. Average depth 16 meters, maximum depth 69 meters. The fourth-largest inland lake in the world. After the Caspian Sea, Lake Superior, and Lake Victoria.

Two rivers fed this sea. From the north, the Syr Darya. From the south, the Amu Darya. Both originated from the glaciers of the Pamir and Tian Shan mountain ranges, crossed the desert, and arrived at the Aral Sea. For thousands of years.

## Why It Vanished

The 1960s. The Soviet government decided to cultivate cotton. Steppe across Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Turkmenistan was converted into irrigated farmland. Water from the Syr Darya and Amu Darya was diverted into canals. The Karakum Canal alone stretches 1,375 kilometers. One of the longest irrigation canals in the world.

By the 1980s, in summer, both rivers dried up before reaching the Aral Sea. The water began to disappear.

## The Order of Disappearance

- **1960**: Water level at 175 feet. Area 68,000 km². The peak.
- **1989**: Split into the North (Small Aral Sea) and South (Large Aral Sea). Salinity tripled.
- **1992**: Area shrank to approximately 33,800 km². Water level dropped 50 feet.
- **Late 1990s**: Split into three lakes. Volume decreased by 75%. Water level at 125 feet.
- **2005**: Kok-Aral Dam completed. The north was saved; the south was abandoned.
- **2006–2009**: The eastern basin of the South Aral Sea shrank to one-fifth.
- **2014**: The eastern South Aral Sea evaporated completely. NASA satellites captured the image. Where the sea had been — a white salt desert.

Fifty years. The time it took for the fourth-largest sea to become a desert.

## What Remained

### Salt and Dust

As the lakebed was exposed, salt flats emerged. Decades of pesticides and fertilizers had accumulated beneath. The wind blows. Toxic dust travels hundreds of kilometers. Respiratory disease, kidney disease, and cancer rates surged among residents of Karakalpakstan (an autonomous republic of Uzbekistan). Children's immune systems weakened. From the capital Nukus to the Aral Sea is 200 kilometers in a straight line — the dust travels far beyond that.

### Climate

When the sea existed, the water surface regulated temperature. Cooler in summer, warmer in winter. Once the sea vanished, summers grew hotter and winters grew colder. The growing season shortened. Ironically — the water was diverted for farming, but once the water disappeared, farming conditions worsened.

### The Death of Fishing

In the 1960s, the Aral Sea yielded 40,000 tons of fish annually. More than 20 species of fish lived there. 60,000 fishermen worked the waters. Canneries ran. Moynaq and Aralsk were the major ports.

As water receded and salinity rose, the fish died. By the 1980s, fishing had effectively ended. Fishing boats remained where the water had retreated from.

### The Ships of Moynaq

Moynaq, Uzbekistan. Once a port city. Now 150 kilometers from the sea. Where the harbor stood, more than a dozen rusted fishing boats sit on sand. Their hulls are buried in the sand with holes in their sides. People call this "the ship graveyard."

During the Soviet era, Moynaq's population exceeded 30,000. It had a cannery and a fishing fleet. Now only a few thousand remain. There is nothing to do. Tourists come to see the rusted ships.

## The Kok-Aral Dam — Half a Salvation

In 2005, with World Bank support, Kazakhstan completed the Kok-Aral Dam. Thirteen kilometers long. It sealed off the Small Aral Sea from the Large Aral Sea. It stopped the Syr Darya's water from draining south.

The effect was remarkable. The Small Aral Sea (North Aral Sea) water level rose. By 2008, the surface had risen 12 meters. Salinity dropped. Fish returned — flounder, carp, perch. Fishing resumed in Aralsk. The sea came back approximately 8 kilometers closer to the town.

But this is the story of the north. The Large Aral Sea to the south was abandoned. The dam meant precisely that — sacrifice the south to save the north. For the people on the Uzbekistan side, the dam was not salvation but a death sentence.
