# Sargasso Sea

## Boundaries

The Sargasso Sea has no coastline. It is the only sea in the world defined not by land but by ocean currents. The Gulf Stream to the west, the North Atlantic Current to the north, the Canary Current to the east, the North Atlantic Equatorial Current to the south. Four currents rotating clockwise, trapping the water inside. The North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre. The only land is Bermuda, at the western edge.

Size: approximately 1,100 km wide and 3,200 km long. An area of roughly 3.2 million km² — similar to the area of India. Depth ranges from the surface where coral reefs grow to the abyssal plain at 4,500 meters.

## Water

The water of the Sargasso Sea is a deep indigo. Underwater visibility reaches up to 60 meters. This transparency means little life. Weak currents, low precipitation, high evaporation, faint winds — thermal mixing does not occur, so nutrients cannot rise to the surface. A biological desert with almost no plankton. Yet on that desert, a forest floats.

## Sargassum

The sea takes its name from a genus of brown algae, Sargassum. Golden-brown seaweed clumps together and drifts on the surface like rafts. What makes this species special is that it is holopelagic — it takes no root on the seafloor, reproducing vegetatively in the open ocean. Based on oxygen production measurements from 1975, the sargassum of the Sargasso Sea is estimated to produce approximately 2.2 billion liters of oxygen per hour.

A sargassum raft is an ecosystem. Sea turtle hatchlings come seeking shelter and food. Shrimp, crabs, and fish live among the tangled seaweed. The sargassum frogfish (Histrio histrio), described by Linnaeus in 1758 with the Sargasso Sea as its type locality, is a master of camouflage that perfectly mimics sargassum. Its scaleless skin bears countless fleshy tabs and projections that replicate the branches and leaves of the seaweed. It changes color, crawls through the weed on its fins, and eats even its own kind.

## Columbus

September 20, 1492, from Columbus's logbook: the seaweed was "so thick that it actually held back the ships." He feared that shallow water lay beneath the weed and they would run aground. The water was miles deep.

## Horse Latitudes

Between latitudes 30 and 35 degrees, a calm belt crossing the Sargasso Sea. Spanish ships would drift for weeks, and to conserve drinking water, had to throw their horses overboard. Hence "Horse Latitudes."

## The Eel Question

Aristotle dissected eels and concluded they were "born from the entrails of the earth." He could find no reproductive organs. Pliny said eels rubbed their bodies against rocks on the riverbed and new eels grew from the particles that fell off. For 2,300 years, no one witnessed eel reproduction.

In 1777, Carlo Mondini was the first to identify ovaries in a female eel. But the male reproductive glands remained unfound.

In 1876, twenty-year-old Sigmund Freud arrived in Trieste. His assignment: find the testes of the eel. He dissected approximately 400 eels over four weeks "amid stench and slime." Finally, around the 400th, he discovered a gonad buried deep in the abdominal cavity. Later, however, Freud deleted this paper from his list of publications.

The reason no one could find the male reproductive glands: eels do not become sexually mature until they begin their journey toward the Sargasso Sea. In eels caught in rivers, there were simply no gonads to find.

## Johannes Schmidt's 17 Years

From 1904 to 1921 (with a five-year interruption for World War I), Danish zoologist Johannes Schmidt trawled the Atlantic collecting eel larvae. The larvae near the European coast were the largest. They grew smaller heading west. They kept getting smaller. In the middle of the Atlantic he found the smallest. The Sargasso Sea.

The eel's metamorphosis:
- Leptocephalus: a transparent larva millimeters in size, shaped like a willow leaf
- Glass eel: first metamorphosis upon reaching the European coast
- Elver: a young eel entering freshwater
- Yellow eel: the muscular form that lives in rivers for decades
- Silver eel: the final form, sexually maturing and returning to the sea

European eels and American eels — every individual on earth is born in the Sargasso Sea. Every individual returns there to die.

## 2022 Confirmation

In 2018–2019, satellite tracking devices were attached to 26 female eels off the Azores coast. One year later, 5 arrived at the Sargasso Sea. A journey of 3,000 to 6,200 miles — the second-longest migration ever recorded for a bony fish. This was the first direct evidence in 101 years since Schmidt.

But still: no one has ever seen an eel spawn.

## Crisis

Since the 1980s, the European eel population has declined by 95%. Causes: dams, disease, illegal trade, climate change. The Sargasso Sea itself is vulnerable to shifting currents and rising sea levels. A sea without a coastline falls under no nation's jurisdiction — the 2014 Hamilton Declaration launched conservation efforts, but it has no enforcement power.
