# Göbekli Tepe

## Location

Southeastern Turkey, 12 km northeast of Şanlıurfa. Forty-eight kilometers from the Syrian border. Perched atop a limestone ridge at 760 m elevation. The name means "Belly Hill" in Turkish. The northern edge of the Fertile Crescent.

The surrounding terrain is gentle, rolling hills. Thirty kilometers away stands Mount Karacadağ — where humanity's first domesticated wheat, einkorn, once grew wild.

## Date

Circa 9500–8000 BCE. The Pre-Pottery Neolithic. Six thousand years before Stonehenge, seven thousand before the Egyptian pyramids. Radiocarbon dating places the oldest structures at approximately 9500–9000 BCE.

Construction spanned roughly 1,500 years across eight building phases.

## Discovery

In 1963, a joint Turkish-American survey team first recorded the site. They mistook it for a medieval cemetery and moved on.

In 1994, German archaeologist Klaus Schmidt revisited the hill. Seeing flint fragments scattered across the slope, he recognized it at once as a prehistoric site. He began excavation in 1995 and devoted himself to this place until his death in 2014. Since then, the German Archaeological Institute (DAI), Istanbul University, and the Şanlıurfa Museum have continued the joint excavation.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2018.

As of 2021, less than 5% of the site has been excavated.

## Structure — Layer III (the oldest layer)

Massive T-shaped limestone pillars arranged in circular enclosures. Four — designated A, B, C, and D — have been excavated so far. Each enclosure has 10–12 pillars around its perimeter and a pair of larger pillars standing face to face at the center.

Pillar height: up to 5.5 m (18 feet). Weight: up to 50 tons. The limestone quarry is 100 m away. Without iron tools — using only flint hammers and stone wedges — the pillars were carved and transported.

## Enclosure D — The Most Elaborate

Central pillars 18 and 31. The horizontal part of the T represents the head, the vertical part the body. Arms are carved in low relief on the sides, and the fingers of both hands nearly meet over the abdomen. A belt at the waist. From the belt, a fox-skin loincloth hangs down.

Beneath Pillar 31, actual fox tailbones were found — evidence that what the pillar "wore" in stone existed in reality at its base.

## The T-Pillars — Human Form

The T-shape is an abstract depiction of a human seen from the side. Arms, hands, belts, and belt ornaments are carved in low relief on some pillars. These likely represent ancestors, guardians, or symbolic personifications of the community.

The animals carved on the pillars are mostly male and in aggressive postures. Snakes are the most common motif. Also present: foxes, wild boar, gazelle, wild sheep, wild donkeys, ducks, vultures, scorpions, and lions.

## Pillar 43 — The Vulture Stone

Pillar 43 in Enclosure D. The most famous relief.

West broad face: a great vulture raises its left wing, and on its right wing rests a disc or sphere. Below it, a headless man — no head, phallus erect. A large scorpion. Another bird (an ibis or young vulture). Above, three rectangular structures topped by domes — perhaps the enclosures themselves seen from the side.

Interpretations:
- The disc is the sun, the moon, or a severed head
- The headless man depicts a death ritual
- In 2017, Martin Sweatman of the University of Edinburgh proposed that this records a comet impact around 10,850 BCE and represents the oldest known solar calendar
- The DAI team favors the interpretation of clan identity and social group symbolism

## Intentional Burial

Each enclosure was used for roughly a century, then deliberately filled in. Packed tight with stones, gravel, and debris. A new enclosure was built on top. Broken tools and animal bones found in the fill suggest ritual closure. (Recent research also raises the possibility of natural sedimentation.)

Schmidt's interpretation: hunter-gatherer groups who participated in the construction periodically gathered to raise stones, perform rituals, hold feasts, and then bury the site when finished. A prehistoric barn raising.

## Layer II (the later layer)

Smaller rectangular structures. T-pillars 1.5 m tall. Over time, the monumental architecture diminished in scale.

## The Relationship Between Settlement and Agriculture

Schmidt's famous thesis: "First the temple, then the city." The conventional narrative ran: agriculture → settlement → surplus → religion. Göbekli Tepe inverts this. Ritual and collective will came first; they drew people together; and to feed the gathered people, agriculture may have begun.

At Mount Karacadağ, 30 km away, the earliest cultivated einkorn wheat has been found. Circa 10,200–10,500 BCE — overlapping with the peak of Göbekli Tepe.

Over 7,000 grinding stones have been unearthed. Phytolith analysis shows grain processing. Whether the grain was wild or cultivated remains unclear.

Tens of thousands of wild animal bones — mostly gazelle — have been found. No hearths or refuse pits were discovered. However, recent research reports evidence of residential structures, water management facilities (cisterns), and everyday tools, challenging the early interpretation that this was a purely ritual site.

## Numbers

- Construction began: circa 9500 BCE
- Period of use: approximately 1,500 years
- Proportion excavated: less than 5%
- T-pillars: over 200 identified to date (geophysical survey)
- Largest pillar: 5.5 m tall, 50 tons
- Grinding stones recovered: over 7,000
- Enclosures: at least 20 (based on surface survey)
- UNESCO inscription: 2018
