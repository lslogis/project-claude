# Svalbard Global Seed Vault — Guarding Beginnings at the End of the World

## The Place

Svalbard archipelago, Norway. The island of Spitsbergen, near Longyearbyen. 78°14′ North, 15°29′ East. Approximately 1,300 kilometers from the North Pole. At 130 meters above sea level, a tunnel penetrates 130 meters deep into a sandstone mountain.

The name Svalbard means "cold coast" in Old Norse.

## Construction

Groundbreaking on June 19, 2006. The prime ministers of Norway, Sweden, Finland, Denmark, and Iceland simultaneously laid the first stones. Opened February 26, 2008. Construction cost 45 million Norwegian kroner (approximately $8.8 million). Funded entirely by the Norwegian government. As of 2019, annual maintenance costs are 2.4 million kroner (approximately $280,000).

Designed by Peter W. Søderman. Along the building's roofline and entrance facade, Dyveke Sanne's light artwork "Perpetual Repercussion" is installed. Fiber optic cables, reflective mirrors, and prisms refract the Arctic light, causing the building's surface to glow softly. It won the Norwegian Lighting Award in 2009.

## Structure

From the entrance, a tunnel extends approximately 130 meters into the mountain. At the end of the tunnel are three storage chambers. Currently only one is in use. Total floor area approximately 1,000 square meters.

Storage temperature: minus 18 degrees Celsius. The natural temperature of the surrounding permafrost is minus 3 to 4 degrees. Even if power is lost, the permafrost keeps the seeds below freezing. This is the key reason this location was chosen. It is geologically stable with no tectonic activity, and at 130 meters above sea level, even if all of Antarctica's ice sheets melted, the water would not reach it.

## The Seeds

As of June 2025, 1,355,591 seed samples are stored. 6,521 plant species. 127 depositors (including nations, international research institutes, and indigenous communities). A diversity representing 13,000 years of human agricultural history.

Largest depositing institutions:
- CIMMYT (International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center) — 187,083 samples
- NPGS (U.S. National Plant Germplasm System) — 156,950 samples
- IRRI (International Rice Research Institute) — 133,707 samples
- ICRISAT — 125,963 samples
- ICARDA — 121,951 samples

Seeds are placed in sealed triple-layer aluminum foil envelopes, put into plastic containers, and set on metal shelving. A nanofilm with seed information is affixed to each box.

## Operating Principle

It works like a bank's safe deposit box. The Norwegian government owns the building and NordGen (the Nordic Genetic Resource Center) operates it. The Crop Trust (Global Crop Diversity Trust) manages the endowment fund. A tripartite agreement.

Only depositors can withdraw their own seeds. The Norwegian government owns the facility but has no ownership of the seeds.

Storage of genetically modified seeds is prohibited under Norwegian law.

## The First Withdrawal — ICARDA

The Syrian Civil War. ICARDA (International Center for Agricultural Research in the Dry Areas) had been headquartered at Tel Hadya, south of Aleppo, Syria, since 1977. The land had been personally provided by President Hafez al-Assad. In 2012, the war forced them to abandon headquarters and relocate to Beirut, Lebanon. Left behind in Syria — a seed bank of 150,000 samples.

In 2015, ICARDA requested the withdrawal of seeds it had deposited at Svalbard. It was the first withdrawal in history. The seeds were sent to new facilities in Lebanon and Morocco. They were planted, multiplied, and re-deposited at Svalbard. The insurance had actually worked.

## The 2016 Flooding Incident

In October 2016, abnormally high temperatures and heavy rain caused water to intrude into the tunnel entrance. Part of the permafrost had melted, allowing surface water to seep in. The water did not reach the storage chambers where the seeds are kept, and there was no damage, but it became a symbol of how climate change threatens even the "doomsday vault." Statsbygg, under the Norwegian government, constructed new waterproofing and drainage systems.

## Right Next Door — The Arctic World Archive

In the same mountain, nearby, sits the Arctic World Archive (AWA). Opened March 2017. Data is stored 150 to 300 meters deep in a decommissioned coal mine (Store Norske Gruve 3). Data is inscribed on polyester film using silver halide crystals and coated with iron oxide. Lifespan 500 to 2,000 years.

The entirety of GitHub's public open-source code (21 terabytes, 186 film reels) was deposited here in July 2020. Digital reproductions of Munch's *The Scream*, a digital copy of Dante's *Divine Comedy*, and the constitutions of Brazil and Norway are also stored.

Seeds beside code. Grain beside memory. Both wait for the future inside the same mountain.
