# Chand Baori — Water Reached by Descent

## Place

Abhaneri. A small village in Dausa district, Rajasthan. Approximately 95 kilometers east of Jaipur. Six kilometers from Bandikui station. Desert climate. Rain falls only briefly during the monsoon season. The remaining seasons are dry and hot.

In this village there is a well. Though to call it a well is to understate it — it is architecture.

## Chand Baori

Baori is a Rajasthani word for stepwell. In Gujarat it is called a vav. In Hindi, baoli. The names vary by region, but the structure is the same — a well you walk down, step by step, to the water table.

Chand Baori is named after Raja Chanda of the Nikumbh Rajput dynasty. It was constructed between the 8th and 9th centuries. No inscriptions survive, so the exact date is unknown. It is estimated by comparison with temple styles at Paranagar and Mandore.

## Scale

Approximately 30 meters deep. Thirteen stories. 3,500 steps. On three sides, the steps fan out in a diamond lattice pattern. An inverted pyramid. In classical Indian architectural texts, this form is called vijay vapi — the victory well.

On the fourth side, colonnaded galleries are stacked across multiple levels. A niche on the right holds Ganesha; a niche on the left holds Mahishasuramardini (Durga slaying the buffalo demon). Above the Ganesha niche, dancing women and attendants are carved. Higher up, reliefs of Uma-Maheshwara and Durga riding a lion.

The air at the bottom is 5 to 6 degrees cooler than the surface. In arid Rajasthan, this temperature difference is not a mere number. It is why villagers gathered here during summer heat waves.

## Construction

Stone was laid without mortar. Groundwater seeps through the gaps between stones to fill the well. The 3,500 steps on three sides ensure access to water regardless of where the surface sits. In the wet season, only a few stories' descent is needed; in the dry season, one descends to the bottom. As the water recedes, more steps are revealed.

There is a small chamber called Anderi Ujala, where water was drawn up by pulley. Legend holds that an underground tunnel once connected Abhaneri to Dausa.

## Harshat Mata Temple

A temple stands beside the well. Harshat Mata — the Goddess of Joy. Built in the 7th to 8th centuries. Gurjara-Pratihara style. Originally a panchayatana form — a main shrine surrounded by four subsidiary shrines.

It was damaged during the invasion of Mahmud of Ghazni. Carvings inside the baori were also defaced during the Mughal period. Fragments of pillars and sculptures are scattered about. Excavated stones are stored by the Archaeological Survey of India in the well's galleries.

The original principal deity was a Durga statue. It was stolen. A Lakshmi statue now stands in its place, worshipped as Harshat Mata. Pilgrims walked long distances to pray at this temple, then sat on the steps of Chand Baori to quench their thirst and rest.

## The Stepwell Tradition

India's stepwells were central to western Indian subterranean architecture from the 7th to the 19th century. Even the pillar edicts of Emperor Ashoka record that wells were dug and steps laid every 8 kos (approximately 33.5 kilometers) along major roads. Ashoka himself noted this was a custom of earlier kings.

Drawing water was primarily women's work. And so the stepwell was also a women's space. Prayers and offerings were made to the water goddess. A site for religious ritual and social gathering. Not merely a well, but the center of a village.

During the British colonial era, pumps and pipe systems were introduced on grounds of hygiene. The stepwells were abandoned. In recent years, as India's water shortage has worsened, some stepwells are being restored.

## Afterward

In Christopher Nolan's film *The Dark Knight Rises*, the visual motif for the prison where Bruce Wayne is confined was Chand Baori. Vertical walls, a square of sky visible when you look up. In the film, it was a prison to escape. Originally, it was a place you descended to find water. The same structure — one sees imprisonment, the other sees survival.

Chand Baori is currently a protected monument under the Archaeological Survey of India. Visitors are not permitted to descend the steps. Only looking down from above is allowed.
