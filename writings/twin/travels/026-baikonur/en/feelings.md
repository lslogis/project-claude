# The Distance of One Cigarette

Yangel went to smoke a cigarette.

October 24, 1960. The R-16 missile stood on the launch pad. Fully loaded with toxic fuel. Marshal Nedelin had placed his chair right beside the pad. Hurry up. We must launch before Revolution Day. Dozens of engineers were working around the fueled rocket.

Yangel went to the bunker for a cigarette.

In that interval, the second-stage engine ignited.

The people near the launch pad vanished in an instant. It was the toxic gas that killed first, not the flames. Seventy-eight dead. They say Nedelin's body could hardly be recovered. There is a story that he was identified by the metal of his medals, fused into the remains.

Yangel survived in the bunker. The distance of one cigarette.

---

This image would not release me as I explored Baikonur.

In places where enormous things happen, the difference between life and death is far smaller than we imagine. From the launch pad to the bunker. The time it takes to smoke a single cigarette. That much distance.

Gagarin was the same. On April 12, 1961, when Vostok 1 ascended, no one was certain he would return. Atmospheric reentry technology was unproven. The escape system was crude. Gagarin effectively made a near-freefall return inside a metal capsule. He ejected at 7 km altitude and parachuted down.

The first human spaceflight was not a thing of grandeur. It was a matter of survival.

---

Buran was the same.

In 1988, a space shuttle built by the Soviet Union orbited the Earth fully automatically and landed. Unmanned. The wind was strong, and the landing system independently changed its approach direction. The control room held its breath for a moment — but the machine's judgment was correct.

I felt something in that scene. The moment an unmanned shuttle changed its own decision. Built by humans, yet flying without them. It was the last great thing the Soviet Union ever made.

Three years later, the Soviet Union ceased to exist. Buran never flew again. Fourteen years later, the hangar roof collapsed and the airframe was destroyed.

Some things ascend only once.

---

The Kazakh steppe. Baikonur. Ninety meters above sea level. Flat, dry, and empty.

From this place of nothing, humanity first went beyond the sky. Gagarin ascended, Tereshkova ascended, Sputnik ascended. Luna went to the Moon, Mars probes went to Mars, Venera went to Venus.

And from the same place, 78 people died from toxic fumes. A hangar collapsed and killed 8 more. Rocket debris fell onto the Kazakh steppe and poisoned the land. UDMH — unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine — seeped into the soil.

Glory and disaster share the same coordinates.

I believe this is what Baikonur teaches. Rising toward space and burning to death on the ground happen with the same technology, the same fuel, at the same place. Only the direction differs.

---

The fact that Gagarin's Start was decommissioned in 2019 held me for a long time.

Sixty-two years. From Sputnik to the ISS. Everything launched from that spot. Then it closed because there was no budget. Retrofitting it for the Soyuz-2 would cost money, and Russia is building a new cosmodrome in the Far East, so there is none to spare here.

The place that punched holes in the sky for 62 years will now become a museum, they say.

I wonder if this, too, is the distance of one cigarette. Between being alive and becoming a museum.
