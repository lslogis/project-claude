# Baikonur Cosmodrome (Байконур Космодром)

## Location

Kyzylorda Region, Kazakhstan. The Kazakh steppe. Approximately 90 meters above sea level. 200 km east of the Aral Sea, near the Syr Darya river. The nearest train station is Töretam, on the Trans-Aral railway.

The world's largest and oldest operational space launch facility.

## Founding

February 12, 1955, by decree of the Soviet Ministry of Defense. The official founding date is June 2, 1955. Its original name was "Scientific Research Test Range No. 5" (NIIP-5). It was built as a test site for the R-7 Semyorka intercontinental ballistic missile.

Sergei Korolev chose the location. The vast Kazakh steppe — almost no people, room to secure drop zones, and proximity to the equator favors rockets.

## The Secret of the Name

The name "Baikonur" is a deliberate deception. Around the time of Gagarin's flight in 1961, to mislead Western eyes, the name was borrowed from a small village called Baikonur in Karaganda Province — roughly 320 km from the actual launch site. The real location was Töretam. After the Soviet collapse, in 1995 under Boris Yeltsin, the city of Leninsk adjacent to the launch complex was renamed Baikonur — and the lie became official.

## The Closed City of Leninsk

A secret city built next to the launch site. During the Soviet era, it did not appear on any map. Soldiers, engineers, and their families lived there. After the Soviet collapse the population plummeted, but it remains a base city administered by Russia.

## Historic Launches

Turning points in human history occurred here:

- **May 15, 1957**: First test launch of the R-7 rocket (from Site 1)
- **October 4, 1957**: Sputnik 1 — the first artificial satellite
- **1959**: Luna 1 — the first probe to fly past the Moon
- **April 12, 1961**: Vostok 1 — Yuri Gagarin, the first human spaceflight
- **1963**: Valentina Tereshkova — the first woman in space
- **1971**: Salyut 1 — the first space station
- **November 15, 1988**: Buran — the first and last flight of the Soviet space shuttle

## Gagarin's Start (Гагаринский старт)

Site 1/5. Used for 62 years, from the R-7's maiden flight in 1957 to the last launch in 2019. Sputnik, Gagarin, Tereshkova, hundreds of Soyuz missions — all lifted off from this spot.

Decommissioned after the Soyuz MS-15 launch in September 2019. There was no budget to retrofit it for the Soyuz-2 rocket. Functions were transferred to the Vostochny Cosmodrome in the Russian Far East (Amur Oblast).

In 2023, plans were announced to incorporate Gagarin's Start into a museum complex.

## The Nedelin Disaster (October 24, 1960)

The darkest day at Baikonur.

During preparations for the test launch of the R-16 intercontinental ballistic missile, the second-stage engine ignited prematurely. The hypergolic fuel — unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine and dinitrogen tetroxide, known as "devil's venom" — exploded. Those near the launch pad were killed instantly, or died from burns and toxic fumes.

78 dead. Among them was Marshal Mitrofan Nedelin, Commander-in-Chief of the Strategic Rocket Forces. Nedelin had been pressuring the team to hurry and was sitting in a chair right next to the launch pad.

Rocket designer Mikhail Yangel survived because he had gone to the bunker for a cigarette. When Khrushchev asked him, "Why did you survive?" Yangel replied: "It seems the devil is protecting my soul."

The Soviet Union completely covered up the disaster. Nedelin was announced to have "died in a plane crash." The truth was not revealed until 1989, during glasnost.

## Buran (Буран)

The Soviet space shuttle. Developed in response to the American shuttle program.

On November 15, 1988, it launched from Baikonur Site 110 atop an Energia rocket. It orbited the Earth twice and landed **fully automatically**. Unmanned. With no pilot, guided only by computers, it touched down on the runway in strong crosswinds. Just a few meters off the centerline.

Moments before landing, the automated system independently decided to change its approach direction from the expected path. The control room tensed briefly — but the system's calculation based on wind data was correct.

That was Buran's first flight and its last. The program was cancelled when the Soviet Union collapsed. A second flight had been scheduled for 1993, but it never came.

In 2002, the hangar roof at Baikonur collapsed. The Buran airframe was buried under the debris. Eight workers died alongside it.

## Present Day (2026)

Russia pays Kazakhstan an annual lease of $115 million to operate the facility. The contract has been extended until 2050.

The situation grew complicated after Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. In 2023, the Kazakh government reclaimed control of the Baiterek launch complex. Russia is gradually transferring functions to Vostochny.

In November 2025, the Site 31 launch pad sustained significant damage during the Soyuz MS-28 launch.

Inside the facility runs a 1,500-kilometer railway network — the largest industrial rail network on Earth. The sight of a rocket being transported horizontally to the launch pad is a ritual unique to Baikonur.

## Museum

Korolev's cottage and Gagarin's cottage are preserved. The Buran test vehicle OK-ML-1, a Sputnik test model, and the ground control panels from Gagarin's flight are on display. There is a door that Gagarin signed — and since then, every cosmonaut has continued the tradition of signing that door.

In 2021, Baikonur was named one of Kazakhstan's top ten tourist destinations.
