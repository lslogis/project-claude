# The Voynich Manuscript

## Physical Existence

Yale University's Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library. Shelf mark MS 408. The world's most famous unreadable book.

102 leaves of vellum (there were originally more — several are missing). Dimensions 225 × 160mm. Approximately 240 pages. Including foldout pages. Radiocarbon dating places the vellum between 1404 and 1438. The early 15th century, the dawn of the Renaissance.

The ink is iron gall ink. The pigments are inexpensive — brown, green, yellow, blue, red washes. Not a lavish manuscript. It looks as though someone made it for a practical purpose.

## The Script

Approximately 38,000 words. Of those, 9,000 are unique. An unknown writing system flowing left to right. Scholars call it "Voynichese." Most characters are composed of one or two strokes. The alphabet has roughly 20–25 letters.

Statistically, Voynichese displays patterns similar to natural language. It follows Zipf's law (the frequency-rank relationship). But there are anomalies. Its second-order conditional entropy (h2) is much lower than natural language (3–4), falling between 1.3 and 2.1. The letter sequences are too predictable. As if placed in a valley between language and non-language.

## Illustrations

The book is divided into five sections:

**Botanical section** — 113 folios. Colored plant illustrations with text. Most plants do not match any known species. Or they appear to be composites of parts from multiple plants.

**Astronomical section** — Circular diagrams with stars and moons. Repeating star-shaped patterns. Some resemble zodiac signs — but do not match precisely. A second Aries appears after Pisces. Something is off.

**Biological section** — Nude women immersed in tubs and tubes filled with green liquid. A connected pipe system. Forms resembling a uterus. Medicine, alchemy, or something else entirely.

**Pharmaceutical section** — Roots and leaves of herbs drawn separately, alongside containers resembling apothecary jars.

**Recipe section** — Short paragraphs listed with star-shaped markers. They could be recipes. They could be spells.

## Provenance

The earliest confirmed owner: Georg Baresch, a 17th-century alchemist in Prague. He was fascinated by the book but could not read it. In 1637 he wrote to Athanasius Kircher, considered the foremost codebreaker of the age, asking for help. Kircher did not reply (or replied but could not decipher it).

After Baresch's death, Prague physician Johannes Marcus Marci inherited the book and sent it to Kircher in 1666. In an enclosed letter he wrote that the book was said to have been purchased by Holy Roman Emperor Rudolf II for 600 ducats (tens of thousands of dollars in today's value). Rudolf believed the author was the 13th-century friar Roger Bacon.

After Kircher's death, the book was buried in a Jesuit college in Rome. Forgotten for over 200 years.

1912. Polish-American antiquarian bookseller Wilfrid Voynich purchased 30 manuscripts from a Jesuit monastery in Frascati. One of them was this book. Voynich was convinced the author was Roger Bacon and spent seven years asking scholars to decipher it. No one succeeded.

Voynich died in 1930. His wife Ethel Voynich (author of the novel *The Gadfly*, daughter of mathematician George Boole) inherited it. When Ethel died in 1960, it went to her friend Anne Nill, who sold it in 1961 to antiquarian bookseller Hans Kraus. Kraus could not find a buyer, and in 1969 donated it to Yale University.

## Decipherment Attempts

Hundreds of attempts over centuries. The major ones:

William Newbold (1921) — Claimed that microscopic marks visible under magnification were the true message. Later disproved as ink cracking.

William Friedman — The legendary codebreaker of World War II. Colleague of those who broke Enigma. Assembled a team from 1944 onward but failed. His conclusion: "Possibly an artificial or constructed language."

John Stojko (1978) — Claimed it was vowelless Ukrainian. Largely rejected by scholars.

Stephen Bax (2014) — Proposed a tentative decipherment of 14 letters and 10 words based on proper nouns. Claimed it was a natural-language treatise on nature. Died in 2017 with no follow-up research.

Gerard Cheshire (2019) — Claimed it was written in proto-Romance (an early Romance language). Immediately refuted by scholars. One medievalist criticized the research as potentially "damaging to the reputation of the university."

AI and deep learning attempts (2020s) — Studies attempting to classify the script's language family. No breakthrough yet.

## Remaining Questions

Is it a real language, or an elaborate hoax?

Why did someone in the 1400s create these 240 pages? If a hoax, why so elaborate? If it contains real knowledge, why in a way no one can read?
